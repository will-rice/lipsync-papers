# Introduction

a cutting-edge research, facial animation synthesis integrates multiple application technologies such as artificial intelligence, natural language processing and computer graphics. It can bring unprecedented experiences to fields such as virtual reality, personal assistants, film production and education.

The speech-driven 3D facial animation synthesis method essentially realizes the mapping from 1D voice signals to 3D time-varying facial motion signals, and finally generates facial animations with consistent lip movements, realistic expressions and motions. The main difficulty is the highly ill-posed nature of this cross-modal mapping. To address this issue, some methods employ parametric face models, such as 3DMM  or FLAME , to constrain the optimization space of 3D facial motion signals, whereas others leverage deep learning techniques to directly regress such signals. The basic strategy of these parametric representation methods  is to learn the mapping from voice signals to facial parametric representation coefficients through data-driven technology, and finally reconstruct a 3D face through the parametric representation model. However, limited by the linearity assumption of the facial representation model, this kind of methods are difficult to represent facial expression details such as wrinkles. In order to regress more realistic facial geometry, researchers  introduce the animation synthesis by directly using the 3D facial mesh representation. The main idea of these methods is to use deep learning technology to directly regress the positions of vertices on the facial mesh or the displacements relative to the template in an end-to-end manner, which has good flexibility. However, the facial surface is essentially a two-dimensional manifold, which is non-convex in the three-dimensional space, resulting in this kind of method being easy to fall into local optimal solutions when optimizing the vertices or offsets of face mesh. In addition, limited by the fix representation of the face template or parametric representation, these methods can only generate facial animations with the same geometry structure, which are difficult for applications that require various face resolutions.

To address the above challenges, we propose MMTalker in this paper, a novel 3D audio-driven facial animation synthesis method. Although our method falls under the category of facial mesh synthesis approaches, we address the ill-posedness of cross-modal mapping through the following aspects. One is we use mesh conformal parameterization technique and non-uniform differentiable sampling approach to reduce the optimization difficulty of 3D facial mesh representation. The mesh parameterization technique establishes the correspondence between UV plane and 3D facial mesh and is used to offer ground truth for the continuous learning. Differentiable non-uniform sampling enables precise facial detail acquisition by setting learnable sampling probability in each triangular face. The other is we adopt geometry-guided graph convolutional network (GGNN) and dual cross-attention mechanism (DCAM) to fuse the hierarchical features of speech and the explicit spatiotemporal geometric features of facial mesh, and the fused facial motion features significantly reduce the uncertainty of cross-modal mapping. The proposed GCNN takes the spatio-temporal position coding features of mesh vertices and the local vertex-wise features of audio as input, outputs the vertex-wise propagated features guided by the adjacent matrix of facial mesh. The DCAM is used to fuse the vertex-wise propagated features and the global feature of audio clip. With the help of this two aspects, our method can generate a realistic and detailed 3D head animation at different resolution. <span style="color: blue">Figure <a href="#fig:1" data-reference-type="ref" data-reference="fig:1">1</a> illustrates the performance of our method.</span> Numerical experiments demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method in realistic expressions and movements.

In summary, the main contributions of this work include:

- A continuous and detailed facial representation method is introduced based on mesh parameterization and non-uniform differentiable sampling, which reduces the optimization difficulty of facial mesh representation.

- A new multi-modal fusion strategy is proposed based on geometry-guided graph convolutional network and dual cross-attention mechanism, which further reduce the uncertainty of cross-modal mapping for talking face animation.

- Qualitative and quantitative experiments show that the proposed method outperforms state-of-the-art methods.

# Multi-resolution 3D Talking Face Synthesis

In this chapter, we introduce the proposed multi-resolution 3D talking face synthesis with multi-modal feature fusion. Given a template facial mesh and an input audio, our goal is to generate a realistic and detailed 3D head animation at different resolutions with the input audio. The architecture of the proposed method is illustrated in Fig <a href="#fig:Pipeline" data-reference-type="ref" data-reference="fig:Pipeline">2</a>. We first use mesh parameterization technology  to obtain the UV planar representation of the template mesh and get the 3D face with different resolution by an detail-preserved non-uniform sampling strategy. Next, the spatio-temporal position coding features of mesh vertices, the local vertex-wise features of audio clip and the global feature of audio clip are fused through graph convolutional neural network and dual-channel cross-attention mechanism. Then, the barycentric interpolation is used to obtain the latent movement features of sampling points in the multi-resolution 3D face. Finally, the deformed face can be predicted by a decoder network.

## Symbol Definition

To introduce the experimental process, we provide relevant explanations for the symbols used in this paper. We organize the training data in the following form, $`\{(\mathbf{I},\mathbf{y}_i,\mathbf{d}_i)\}^T_{i=1}`$. $`\mathbf{I}\in\mathbb{R}^{N\times 3}`$ denotes the template mesh and each row of $`\mathbf{I}`$ contains the $`x`$, $`y`$, $`z`$ coordinates of a vertex. $`N`$ is the number of vertices in the template mesh. $`\mathbf{y}_i\in\mathbb{R}^{N\times 3}`$ defines the ground truth spatial coordinates of the $`i`$th frame. $`\mathbf{d}_i\in\mathbb{R}^{D}`$ is the speech feature at the $`i`$th frame extracted by Wav2Vec2.0, where $`D`$ is the length. $`T`$ is the total number of frames. Our goal is to synthesize multi-resolution face $`\hat{\mathbf{y}}_i\in\mathbb{R}^{M\times 3}`$, which is as similar as possible to the ground truth $`\mathbf{y}_i`$. $`M`$ is the number of vertices of the multi-resolution face.

## Mesh Parameterization and Non-uniform Sampling

Mesh parameterization is an important technique in computer graphics and geometric processing. Its core goal is to establish the mapping between a UV parameter domain and the three-dimensional mesh surface. To further maintain the invariance of scaling rates and local angle in the surface mesh, we adopt the conformal mapping . Since UV parameter domain stores the same geometry topology as the template mesh $`\mathbf{I}`$, we can easily build the continuous correspondence between them by barycentric interpolation. In addition, to make the generated face have more facial details, we perform non-uniform sampling in the UV parameter domain. For each triangle in the face mesh, its sampling probability is related to the distance from the key points on the human face. Figure <a href="#fig:parameterization" data-reference-type="ref" data-reference="fig:parameterization">3</a> shows the performance of mesh parameterization and non-uniform sampling.

## Multi-modal Semantic Alignment

In this section, we first introduce how to extract the spatio-temporal encoding features of mesh vertices, the local vertex-wise encoding features of audio and the global encoding feature of audio. Then, in order to achieve the alignment of audio and motion modalities, the multi-model feature fusion strategy from these above features will be displayed in detail.

**Spatio-temporal vertex feature encoding.** In order to enhance the alignment of cross-modal mapping, previous methods  employ encoder-decoder structured networks to build the mapping between audio and facial space. VOCA  only consider the single speech modality in the encoder structure. Although GDPnet  and MeshTalk  take face mesh as part of the input in encoder structure, they use the global features of mesh while discarding the local features. Hence, this oversimplified form leads to ambiguous local correspondences. In addition, none of the aforementioned methods utilize local spatial positional encoding techniques, nor do they incorporate temporal sequence encoding. The only method employing temporal sequence encoding is FaceFormer . However, they integrate the temporal encoding with the global features of the facial mesh, which similarly results in ambiguous local correspondences. In this paper, we propose a simple and effective position encoding form:
``` math
\begin{equation}
    STE(\mathbf{s},t) = \{cos(i\cdot arccos(\mathbf{s}) + wt/T)\}^L_{i=1}
\end{equation}
```
where $`\mathbf{s}=(u,v)`$ is the sampling point, $`t`$ represents the current time step in the sequence, $`w`$ is a learnable parameter, and $`L`$ is the number of dimensional index. As mesh parameterization builds the vertex correspondences between UV domain and face mesh surface, the spatio-temporal vertex encoding feature can be defined by $`f_{st} \in R^{T \times N \times L}`$, here $`f_{st}\{\mathbf{s},t\} = STE(\mathbf{s},t)`$. The proposed mechanism encodes both local spatial positions and temporal sequences. More importantly, it establishes precise cross-modal correspondences for facial animation synthesis, including explicit geometric correspondences on the facial surface and temporal alignments across the motion sequence.

**Local and global audio feature encoding.** Following these methods  we also use the architecture of the state-of-the-art self-supervised pre-trained speech model wav2vec2.0 for audio feature extraction. The difference is that we extract global feature and local vertex-wise feature from audio. The global feature relates to facial expression which are crucial to facial movements. The local vertex-wise feature is able to capture subtle nuances of mouth and eye movements which are important for creating realistic facial animation. In our implementation, we get the final layer feature $`\mathbf{d}_i`$ from the pre-trained Wav2Vec2.0 model for each audio clip and interpolate the output with the desired frame rate to obtain the latent audio features. The global audio feature $`f_{ag}\in R^{T\times H}`$ are obtained by encoding the latent audio features using the long short-term memory network, where $`H`$ denotes the feature dimension. The local vertex-wise audio feature $`f_{al}\in R^{T\times N \times H}`$ are extracted by applying 1D convolution and multi-layer perceptrons to the latent audio features.

**Multi-modal feature fusion.** After obtaining the spatio-temporal vertex feature and the local and global audio feature, we employ residual graph convolutional network (RGCN) and dual cross-attention mechanism (DCAM) to extract discriminative facial motion feature.

RGCN module is used to fuse the spatio-temporal vertex feature $`f_{st}`$ and the local audio feature $`f_{at}`$, which is defined by:
``` math
\begin{equation}
    O^{l+1} = ReLU(AO^{l}W^{l}) \quad l=0, 1
\end{equation}
```
where $`O^{0} = f_{st} \oplus f_{at}`$, $`W^{l}`$ denotes the weight parameter matrix of the $`l`$-th layer, and $`A`$ can be expressed by:
``` math
\begin{equation}
    A=\left\{\begin{matrix}
    1, \quad i=j
 \\  1/d_{i}, \quad (i,j)\in e_{ij}
 \\0, \quad others
\end{matrix}\right.
\end{equation}
```
where $`d_{i}`$ is the degree of the $`i`$-th vertex on the template mesh, $`e_{ij}`$ represents an edge between the $`i`$-th vertex and the $`j`$-th vertex on the template mesh. RGCN module achieves the feature propagation and fusion after semantic alignment, which contains explicit geometric correspondences on the facial surface and temporal alignments across the motion sequence. Our paper employs a two-layer residual graph convolutional network, which is shown in Fig. <a href="#fig:gcn" data-reference-type="ref" data-reference="fig:gcn">4</a>.

<figure id="fig:dcam">
<img src="DCAM.png" />

</figure>

To capture how the global audio feature $`f_{ag}`$ affects the movement of the mesh vertex and further enhance the fused feature $`f_{pl} = O^2`$, we introduce DCAM module. DCAM module first linearly projects $`f_{pl}`$ into query $`Q`$ and projects $`f_{ag}`$ into value $`V`$ and key $`K`$. Then, the dependencies between each vertex motivation feature and the global audio feature can be expressed as follows:
``` math
\begin{equation}
Att(Q,K,V) = Softmax(\frac{QK^{T}}{\sqrt{d_{k}}})V
\end{equation}
```
where $`d_{k}`$ is the dimension of the queries and keys. Last, the enhanced movement feature $`f_{lg}`$ of each vertex are defined by
``` math
\begin{equation}
    f_{lg} = (f_{pl} + Att(Q,K,V)) \oplus (f_{ag} +  Att(Q,K,V))
\end{equation}
```
where $`\oplus`$ represents feature concatenation. The whole structure of DCAM module can be found in Fig. <a href="#fig:dcam" data-reference-type="ref" data-reference="fig:dcam">5</a>.

## Multi-Resolution Motion Synthesis

Section <a href="#sec:msa" data-reference-type="ref" data-reference="sec:msa">3.3</a> achieves the multi-modal semantic alignment between mesh sequence and audio in the original resolution. Hence, to generate face animation with $`M`$ surface points, barycentric interpolation is employed into the encoded movement feature $`f_{lg}`$ of the original surface vertices.

Given a sample point $`\mathbf{s}`$ that belongs to the triangular surface on the UV plane and its corresponded triangular surface on facial mesh is $`F`$, we first calculate the barycentric coordinate $`(b_{1}, b_{2}, b_{3})`$, and $`\sum_{j=1}^{3} b_{j}=1`$. Then, the movement feature $`f^s_{gl}`$ of $`\mathbf{s}`$ can be obtained by
``` math
\begin{equation}
    f^s_{gl} = (b_{1}z_{1} + b_{2}z_{2} + b_{3}z_{3})
\end{equation}
```
where $`z_1`$, $`z_2`$ and $`z_3`$ are the movement features that corresponding the vertices of $`F`$. Next, we use a 5-layer fully connected network to predict the vertex displacement relative to the reference mesh $`\mathbf{I}`$. Finally, the geometry topology of the generated face mesh can be got by Delaunay triangulation algorithm which is performed in the UV plane with $`M`$ sample points.

## Loss function

To train the proposed MMTalker, we employ a loss function that comprises reconstruction loss $`L_{rec}`$, velocity loss $`L_{v}`$ and eye loss $`L_{eye}`$
``` math
\begin{equation}
    L= \lambda _{1}L_{rec}+\lambda _{2}L_{v}+\lambda _{3}L_{eye}
\end{equation}
```

The reconstruction loss $`L_{rec}`$ measures the difference between the predicted $`\hat{y}_{i}`$ and the ground-truth $`\bar{y}_{i}`$ that can be pre-calculated through barycentric interpolation. Specifically, we use least squared error as the reconstruction loss
``` math
\begin{equation}
    L_{rec}=\frac{1}{TM}\sum_{i=1}^{T}\sum_{j=1}^{M} \| \bar{y}_{i}^j-\hat{y}_{i}^j\|^{2}
\end{equation}
```
where $`j`$ indicates the index of vertex in $`\bar{y}_{i}`$.

The velocity loss is used to encourage smooth and natural movements over time, it measures the predicted smoothness in the context of the sequence to induce temporal stability. $`L_{v}`$ can be expressed as:
``` math
\begin{equation}
    L_{v}=\frac{1}{TM}\sum_{i=1}^{T}\sum_{j=1}^{M} \| (\bar{y}_{i}^j - \bar{y}_{i-1}^j)-(\hat{y}_{i}^j-\hat{y}_{i-1}^j) \|^{2}
\end{equation}
```

Besides, following to the recent research work , we also use the loss function by calculating the KL divergence of the movement of the eye area to track eye movement, as defined below:
``` math
\begin{equation}
    L_{eye}= \underset{j\in G_{eye}}{KL}(\hat{y}^{j},\bar{y}^{j})
\end{equation}
```
where $`\hat{y}^{j}`$ represents the predicted coordinates of the $`j`$-th vertices of all frames in the batch, $`\bar{y}^{j}`$ represents the corresponding true coordinates that also can be pre-calcugated through barycentric interpolation, and $`G_{eye}`$ is a mask for the eye region to focus on the relevant vertices of the eye region. This loss function design helps the model to better capture the features of eye movement, thus improving the accuracy of prediction.

## Quantitative Evaluation

In this section, we compare the audio-driven 3D facial animation methods, VOCA , FaceFormer ,SelfTalk,CodeTalker and PATS on both VOCASET and Multiface datasets.To measure lip and eye synchronization, we calculated the lip and eye vertices error as used in MeshTalk and FaceFormer,which is defined as mean of the maximum L2 error at the lip and eye regions of each frame,denoted as $`E_{vl}`$ and $`E_{ve}`$,The errors are calculated by comparing the predictions and the ground truth in the test set.$`E_{vl}`$ and $`E_{ve}`$can be written in the following form:
``` math
\begin{equation}
    E_{vl} = \frac{1}{T} \sum^{T}_{i=1}\max_{j\in G_{lip}}(\left \| \hat{y}_{i}^{j} - y_{i}^{j} \right \|^{2} )
\end{equation}
```

``` math
\begin{equation}
    E_{ve} = \frac{1}{T} \sum^{T}_{i=1}\max_{j\in G_{eye}}(\left \| \hat{y}_{i}^{j} - y_{i}^{j} \right \|^{2} )
\end{equation}
```

where $`\hat{y}_{i}^{j}`$ denotes the $`j`$-th vertices coordinates in the predicted $`i`$-th frame, $`y_{i}^{j}`$ denotes the ground truth, $`\left \| \cdot  \right \|^{2}`$ denotes the Euclidean distance, $`G_{lip}`$ and $`G_{eye}`$ denote the mask of lips and eyes, and $`T`$ denotes the total number of frames.

Due to the $`E_{ve}`$ cannot fully assess the accuracy of expressions within the eye region, therefore We follow the CodeTalker principles and utilize the Upper-face dynamics deviation (FDD) metric. The FDD technique measures the dynamic changes in the face during a sequence of motions and compares them to ground truth,which is calculated by:

``` math
\begin{equation}
```

## Ablation experiments

We proposed several key modules including Spatio-Temporal vertex feature Encoding(STE), Residual Graph Nonvolutional Networ(RGCN) module and Dual Cross-Attention Mechanis(DCAM). In this section, we conduct ablation studies to evaluate the impact of different components in our proposed MMTalker framework on the quality of the generated 3D speaking faces.

**Spatio-Temporal vertex feature Encoding:** STE injects time information into the vertex position features; otherwise, the position features will be replicated T times in the module and cannot generate discriminative features for the vertices of each frame. To evaluate its effectiveness, we implemented a method variant, Remove the STE module and instead use the position encoding without time, table II Three indicators showed significant degradation. This indicates that audio information plays a crucial role in refinement of fine lip movements and enhancing audio-visual consistency and rhythmic smoothness.

**Residual Graph Nonvolutional Networ:** RGCN fuses local audio with temporal sequence features based on the topological connection relationship of the original mesh,which improves the similarity and difference of each facial area when speaking. We replaced RGCN with MLP. The comparison shows that the values of each evaluation index have all improved.

**Dual Cross-Attention Mechanis:** As discussed in Sec. III, global audio features encode the overall attributes of the audio, and local features guide the vertices movement under the global attributes. After removing the DCAM module, we use MLP to directly fuse the global and local features. Compared with MLP($`F_{l},f_{g}`$), we propose that DCAM($`F_{l},f_{g}`$) fuses global and local features, which can generate more accurate facial expressions and avoid the need to replicate $`f_{g}`$ N times. This leads to an excessively high similarity among each vertices in one frame.

# LIMITATION

Although the research methods in this paper have achieved certain success, there are still some shortcomings. The current work mainly focuses on single-person voice-driven interaction. In the future, it can explore the response of expressions and gazes in multi-person dialogue interaction scenarios to enhance social immersion. The robustness of this method for complex scenarios with strong emotions needs to be further strengthened. If combined with more refined voice emotion analysis, it may be possible to generate more expressive animations. Further research can consider introducing a more refined voice emotion recognition module or integrating text semantic information to achieve deeper alignment and generation with more expressive content.

# CONCLUSION

This paper introduces MMTalker, a voice-driven 3D face animation method based on multi-modal fusion, which can generate high-fidelity, multi-resolution expressive facial animation. Through the innovative cross-modal coupling design, the method realizes the efficient coordination of speech signal, geometric structure and timing information, and significantly improves the generation quality of complex facial expressions. We designed a local audio feature fusion module to effectively enhance lip synchronization accuracy and facial motion consistency. The multi-resolution representation mechanism designed at the same time supports the flexible generation of facial animation with different precision. Quantitative and qualitative experimental analysis shows that MMTalker is superior to the existing methods in terms of quantitative indexes and visual effects, and can generate more accurate and natural facial animation.
