# Do Protective Perturbations Really Protect Portrait Privacy under Real-world Image Transformations?

Sun Ruiqing , Yao Xingshan , Wu Zhijing Beijing Institute of TechnologyBeijingBeijingChina , Lan Tian , Cui Chenhao , Zhao Huiyang Beijing Institute of TechnologyBeijingBeijingChina , Shi Jialing , Yang Chen and Mao Xianling [maoxl@bit.edu.cn](2604.23688v1/mailto:maoxl@bit.edu.cn) Beijing Institute of TechnologyBeijingBeijingChina

###### Abstract.

Proactive defense methods protect portrait images from unauthorized editing or talking face generation (TFG) by introducing pixel-level protective perturbations, and have already attracted increasing attention for privacy protection. In real-world scenarios, images inevitably undergo various transformations during cross-device display and dissemination—such as scale transformations and color compression—that directly alter pixel values. However, it remains unclear whether such pixel-level modifications affect the effectiveness of existing proactive defense methods that rely on pixel-level perturbations. To solve this problem, we conduct a systematic evaluation of representative proactive defenses under image transformation. The evaluated methods are selected to span different generation architectures such as diffusion and GAN-based models, as well as defense scopes covering both portrait and natural images, and are assessed using both qualitative and quantitative metrics for subjective and objective comparison. Experimental results indicate that defense methods based on pixel-level perturbations struggle to withstand common image transformations, posing a risk of defense failure in real-world applications. To further highlight this risk, we propose a simple yet effective purification framework by leveraging the vulnerabilities induced by real-world image transformations. Experimental results demonstrate that the proposed method can efficiently remove protective perturbations with low computational cost, highlighting previously overlooked risks to the research community. ## 1. Introduction

The rapid advancement of generative adversarial networks (GANs) and diffusion models has substantially accelerated the development of AI-generated content (AIGC) technologies, such as image editing and talking face generation (TFG) (Xu et al., 2024; Brooks et al., 2023; Chen et al., 2020; Choi et al., 2018). While these advances demonstrate remarkable technical capabilities, they also substantially increase the risk of malicious misuse, including fraud, political manipulation, and the dissemination of misinformation.

Figure 1. Impact of real-world image transformations on defense performance. Talking-face samples are generated by Hallo (Xu et al., 2024), while edited images are produced by InstructPix2Pix (Brooks et al., 2023) with the prompt “Let the person wear purple makeup.”

To mitigate these risks, a variety of defense techniques have been proposed and can be broadly categorized into passive detection and proactive defense (Deng et al., 2025). Passive detection methods attempt to identify whether facial content has been edited or forged by training generative content detectors (Zheng et al., 2024). In contrast, proactive defense methods proactively interfere with the generation process by injecting imperceptible protective perturbations into images, causing the synthesized outputs to degrade (Gan et al., 2025; Qu et al., 2024; Wang et al., 2025). By preventing privacy abuse at the source, proactive defense has therefore attracted increasing attention. Recent studies demonstrate that such perturbations can effectively prevent generative models from modifying facial attributes or identity (Jeong et al., 2025; Wang et al., 2025), or from synthesizing talking portrait videos using TFG models (Gan et al., 2025; Sun et al., 2025) in local test.

Compared to local testing, images may inevitably undergo various image transformations during real-world transmission. Recent studies have investigated how image compression during dissemination on online social network platforms affects the effectiveness of proactive defenses (Qu et al., 2024; Zhang et al., 2025). However, they largely overlook effects of other transformations. For example, to accommodate heterogeneous display devices with varying resolutions and screen sizes, images are frequently resized via interpolation-based resampling operations. Since most existing defense algorithms are not explicitly designed to handle such transformations and are highly sensitive to scale variations, even benign user behaviors—such as taking screenshots for sharing or archiving—can unintentionally degrade their protective effectiveness, as illustrated in Fig. 1. Similar vulnerabilities also arise from color compression during mobile photography, platform-specific image processing, and storage formats, all of which introduce irreversible modifications to pixel statistics. As a consequence, if defense methods fail to adapt to these diverse and often inevitable real-world transformations, they may substantially overestimate their robustness, ultimately providing users with a false sense of security.

In this work, we systematically examine the practical viability of protection methods based on adversarial perturbations under realistic deployment conditions. Our study focuses on two representative application scenarios: (i) protecting facial attributes from malicious manipulation, and (ii) preventing portrait images from being exploited for TFG. In terms of evaluation coverage, we consider a broad range of open-source defense models targeting both GAN-based and diffusion-based AIGC frameworks, encompassing general defenses as well as specific, open-source methods. Moreover, our evaluation spans both widely cited representative approaches and recently released state-of-the-art models. Extensive experiments demonstrate that under common real-world transformations—such as resolution changes and color compression—protective perturbations often fail to reliably prevent portrait editing or talking-face synthesis.

To further raise awareness of these risks within the community, we propose a simple yet effective training-free purification method, termed Transformation-Induced Purification via Region-wise Super-Resolution (TIP-RSR), by exploiting the above vulnerabilities. The proposed approach adopts differentiated purification strategies for facial regions and background areas in portrait images. Experimental results show that TIP-RSR can efficiently remove protective perturbations while maximally preserving the structural integrity and visual fidelity of the original image. These findings underscore the urgent need to revisit the robustness assumptions of existing protection methods and to explore more resilient solutions for mitigating privacy abuse risks posed by modern AIGC technologies

Our contributions are summarized as follows:

- •
  We conduct a systematic evaluation of state-of-the-art and prevalent proactive defense methods under two representative real-world image transformations: scale variations and color compression.
- •
  We demonstrate that common real-world transformations can significantly degrade the effectiveness of protective perturbations, causing existing methods to fail to reliably prevent portrait editing and TFG in practice.
- •
  We introduce TIP-RSR, a training-free purification approach that effectively removes protective perturbations from protective images while preserving most of the original structural information, exposing previously overlooked vulnerabilities in current protection methods.

## 2. Related Works

### 2.1. Talking Face Generation

Generating realistic talking portrait videos conditioned on a given audio signal and a target portrait image is a core task in TFG, and has attracted considerable attention due to its broad practical applications. Early works primarily focused on synthesizing lip movements that are highly synchronized with the input audio, achieving notable success in scenarios such as video translation and dubbing (Prajwal et al., 2020; KR et al., 2019). More recently, the development of diffusion models has substantially advanced the TFG field (Liu et al., 2024; Huang et al., 2025). The Hallo (Xu et al., 2024) series introduces hierarchical audio-driven visual synthesis modules along with data augmentation strategies, enabling high-resolution, pose-controllable, and long-duration talking portrait video generation. Loopy (Jiang et al., 2025) exploits long-term motion dependencies to synthesize vivid talking portrait videos with subtle facial details, without relying on explicit motion constraints or predefined templates. Ditto (Li et al., 2025) further improves diffusion-based TFG frameworks by enabling streaming processing, real-time inference, and low first-frame latency.

### 2.2. Image Editing

In the field of AIGC, image editing has been widely recognized as a task of significant practical importance. Early studies were predominantly built upon GAN-based frameworks, enabling the manipulation of specific visual attributes such as hair color, skin tone, and even facial identity (Choi et al., 2018; Chen et al., 2020). More recently, the rapid development of multimodal large-scale models has made it possible to directly edit images according to natural language instructions. For example, InstructPix2Pix (Brooks et al., 2023) leverages multiple large models to construct bimodal paired data, enabling instruction-following image editing guided by human-written instructions. HiDream-E1-1 (Cai et al., 2025) adopts a sparse Diffusion Transformer architecture together with a dynamic mixture-of-experts design, achieving a favorable trade-off between computational efficiency and image generation quality. Qwen-Image (Wu et al., 2025) further improves visual consistency and semantic coherence between edited images and their originals through comprehensive data engineering, progressive learning strategies, enhanced multi-task training paradigms, and scalable infrastructure optimizations. While these advances in image editing and TFG substantially improve controllability, fidelity, and usability, they also exacerbate ethical and societal risks, particularly raising concerns about privacy violations and the misuse of AIGC.

### 2.3. Proactive Defense

To protect personal images from potential misuse by TFG and image editing models, several recent studies have explored perturbation-based protection by injecting imperceptible noise to disrupt the generation process. FaceLock (Wang et al., 2025) integrates a facial recognition model as an adversary into the diffusion loop, inducing substantial visual discrepancies between edited outputs and the original images. DF-RAP (Qu et al., 2024) explicitly incorporates compression operations commonly used on online social network platforms, thereby improving the robustness of image editing defenses under practical deployment conditions. The Silencer (Gan et al., 2025) series aims to silence speech generation by applying protective perturbations to the input images, achieving effective defense against TFG methods. Despite their effectiveness, these approaches generally overlook the fact that images may undergo multiple resolution changes and color compression operations during real-world dissemination, which can significantly undermine the reliability of such protections in practice.

## 3. Evaluation of the Protective Perturbation

### 3.1. Dataset and Selected Models

Our evaluation focuses on two key application scenarios: facial attribute editing and TFG. To ensure that the evaluation metrics more accurately reflect the effectiveness of defense methods, we adopt the CelebA-HQ (Karras et al., 2018) dataset, as it is explicitly used in the original papers of the evaluated methods, thereby minimizing potential interference introduced by out-of-domain data. Considering the substantial computational cost of large-scale evaluation, following the work of Ditto (Li et al., 2025), we select a subset of the CelebA-HQ dataset consisting of the first 100 facial images. For GAN-based facial attribute editing experiments, we employ DF-RAP (Qu et al., 2024) and AntiForgery (Wang et al., 2022) as representative proactive defense methods to protect portrait images against StarGAN-based attacks (Choi et al., 2018). Notably, DF-RAP explicitly accounts for the influence of online social network platforms, making it particularly suitable for evaluating defense robustness under realistic dissemination conditions. For diffusion-based model experiments, we adopt two recently released open-source defense methods, FaceLock (Wang et al., 2025) and FaceShield (Jeong et al., 2025), to protect portrait images against attacks generated using InstructPix2Pix (Brooks et al., 2023) and IP-Adapter (Ye et al., 2023). For talking-face generation (TFG) attacks, we evaluate a set of general-purpose defense methods, including AdvDM(-) (Xue et al., 2023), MIST (Liang and Wu, 2023), and PhotoGuard (Salman et al., 2023), together with the task-specific defense Silencer-I (Gan et al., 2025). All methods are applied to protect portrait images against attacks generated by Hallo (Xu et al., 2024).

### 3.2. Evaluation Metrics

To investigate how common image processing operations affect protective perturbations, we apply three representative transformations to adversarially protected samples: JPEG compression with a quality factor of 75, image resizing, and a combination of JPEG compression followed by resizing. The transformed images are then used as inputs for both image editing and TFG tasks. Followed by DF-RAP (Qu et al., 2024), qualitative and quantitative analyses are conducted by comparing the generated results with those obtained using undefended clean images as inputs.

For image editing attacks, we measure the PSNR and SSIM to quantify the distortion between images generated before and after defense. In addition, FID (Heusel et al., 2017), LPIPS (Zhang et al., 2018), and BRISQUE (Mittal et al., 2011) are used to assess the visual quality of adversarial examples and generated images. The same set of metrics is also used to evaluate the overall quality of adversarial examples. For TFG attacks, PSNR and SSIM are adopted to evaluate the visual quality of generated videos, while Sync-C (Chung and Zisserman, 2016) and M-LMD (Chen et al., 2019) are employed to assess audio–visual synchronization.

### 3.3. Experimental Results

The experimental results indicate that existing defense methods struggle to remain effective under common image transformations. As shown in Table 1 and Table 2, when protected images processed by JPEG compression and resizing are used for image editing and TFG, the resulting outputs become noticeably closer to those generated from clean images, compared to results obtained from untransformed protected inputs. For image editing tasks, this trend is reflected by clear improvements in PSNR and SSIM, along with consistent reductions in FID, LPIPS, and BRISQUE. For TFG, in addition to improved visual fidelity, the generated results exhibit lip-motion patterns that are more similar to those produced from clean inputs, as evidenced by increased Sync-C scores and M-LMD. Additional quantitative results for settings involving resize-only and JPEG-only transformations are provided in the Table 9 and Table 8 (Appendix).

Qualitative results show that existing defense models tend to lose their effectiveness after undergoing scale variations, and this degradation becomes more pronounced when scale changes are combined with JPEG compression, as illustrated in Fig. 2 and Fig 3. The evaluation results further suggest that common real-world image processing operations should be explicitly considered during model design, which otherwise may result in overestimated defense performance and a false sense of security for users. For instance, DF-RAP exhibits relatively stronger robustness against JPEG compression, as it explicitly accounts for compression operations, which is commonly applied by online social network platforms, during its design. Such targeted modeling strategies remain inherently fragile. For instance, they struggle to defend against joint attacks under the C&R setting, where compression and resizing are applied in combination. This observation suggests that explicitly modeling a single image transformation in isolation may not constitute a robust solution. Moreover, during the C&R process, the semantic content of images is largely preserved, while subtle protective perturbations are effectively removed. This behavior can be attributed to the fact that image transformations in real-world dissemination pipelines are typically designed to be perceptually inconspicuous, aiming to maintain visual fidelity. As a result, imperceptible perturbations—which form the basis of many proactive defenses—become intrinsically expendable under such transformations.

### 4.1. Problem Formulation

Both facial attribute editing and TFG can be formulated as face manipulation tasks. Given an original face image $`x`$ and a trained manipulation model $`M`$, the former aims to transform $`x`$ into a manipulated output $`y`$ by modifying a set of reference conditions $`r`$, such as lip color or target identity. In contrast, the latter generates a high-fidelity talking-portrait video $`y`$ by conditioning on an input audio signal $`a`$ while preserving the identity information extracted from $`x`$. Accordingly, the objective of face manipulation can be described as follows:

``` math
y=\begin{cases}M(x,r),&\text{image editing;}\\
M(x,a),&\text{TFG.}\end{cases}
```

The formula can be simplified to:

``` math
y=\mathcal{M}(x).
```

Proactive defense methods generate adversarial examples $`\hat{x}=x+\eta`$ by injecting imperceptible perturbations $`\eta`$ into an original face image $`x`$. The perturbation $`\eta`$ is optimized to maximize a distance metric $`D`$ between the outputs of a manipulation model $`\mathcal{M}`$ given $`x`$ and $`\hat{x}`$, subject to an $`\ell_{\infty}`$-norm constraint $`\|\eta\|_{\infty}\leq\epsilon`$ to preserve imperceptibility:

$`\displaystyle\max_{\eta}\quad`$$`\displaystyle D\big(\mathcal{M}(x),\mathcal{M}(x+\eta)\big),`$$`\displaystyle\|\eta\|_{\infty}\leq\epsilon.`$

Purification methods aim to protect the manipulation model $`\mathcal{M}`$ by removing defensive perturbations from an adversarial example $`\hat{x}`$ and aligning it with the original image $`x`$. The objective of purification can be formulated as minimizing the discrepancy between the purified image and the original image:

``` math
\min_{\hat{x}}\;D(x,\hat{x}).
```

Applying image transformations to an adversarial example $`\hat{x}`$, particularly down scaling or compression, inevitably introduces irreversible modifications to pixel values, including the injected perturbations. As demonstrated in Section 3, such transformations can render the original adversarial perturbations ineffective. In contrast, natural images typically exhibit redundancy and strong correlations among neighboring pixels (Torralba and Oliva, 2003; Wang et al., 2004), where important features and structures are often replicated across spatial regions, making critical information less susceptible to severe degradation. Although naive downsampling alone is sufficient to invalidate most defense mechanisms, it is generally insufficient to completely remove the residual perturbations embedded in the image. Consequently, these residual artifacts may still degrade the output quality of the manipulation model $`\mathcal{M}`$, as further illustrated in Fig. 2 and Fig. 3.

To improve the visual quality of compressed images, a naive approach is to apply existing general super-resolution (SR) models to adversarial examples $`\hat{x}`$. However, most existing SR models are trained on low-resolution samples synthesized from high-resolution images via down-sampling, JPEG compression, or additive Gaussian or Poisson noise (Yi et al., 2025; Wang et al., 2021b; Chen et al., 2025; Lin et al., 2024; Agustsson and Timofte, 2017). As a result, directly applying off-the-shelf SR models to adversarial examples often fails to completely remove protective perturbations and may even introduce high-frequency artifacts on facial regions, as shown in Fig. 8 for Image Purification ‣ Do Protective Perturbations Really Protect Portrait Privacy under Real-world Image Transformations?") (Appendix).

### 4.2. Transformation-Induced Purification via Region-wise Super-Resolution

![Refer to caption](https://arxiv.org/html/2604.23688v1/figures/method.png)

Figure 4. The pipeline of the TIP-RSR framework.

![Refer to caption](https://arxiv.org/html/2604.23688v1/figures/method_motivation.png)

Figure 5. Illustration of the hybrid purification strategy. The general SR model (GSR) preserves structural details, while the face-specific SR (FSR) model removes facial perturbations. The final result fuses non-facial regions from GSR with facial regions from FSR, balancing structural fidelity and perturbation removal.

Training or fine-tuning a SR model specifically tailored to protective perturbations is a feasible alternative. Nevertheless, constructing paired datasets of clean images and adversarial examples incurs substantial computational overhead. Moreover, protective perturbations introduced by different protection methods follow distinct distributions due to their heterogeneous optimization objectives and strategies. Consequently, the training cost required for such customized SR models is typically prohibitive in practical purification scenarios. Diffusion-based purification methods provide another line of solutions by injecting a small amount of noise during the forward diffusion process and recovering clean images through an iterative reverse denoising procedure (Nie et al., 2022; Pei et al., 2025). Although these approaches avoid making explicit assumptions about the perturbation distribution, the step-wise reverse generation process introduces non-negligible computational overhead.

Motivated by observation mentioned on chapter 3, we propose the training-free TIP-RSR framework, as illustrated in Fig.4, which exploits the spatial redundancy and local correlations among neighboring pixels in natural images under compression and resizing operations. To avoid directly relying on general SR models while still leveraging strong structural priors inherent in human faces, we draw inspiration from diffusion-based purification methods and first apply down-sampling $`Down(\cdot)`$ and JPEG compression $`J(\cdot)`$ to adversarial examples to smooth defensive perturbations, as shown in Fig. 4(d). A face-specific SR model is then applied to restore facial regions, producing the intermediate result in Fig.4(e). Inspired by GridPure (Zhao et al., 2024), we further enhance visual fidelity by computing a weighted combination of the adversarial image and the purified result, which helps preserve fine-grained details. In addition to suppressing protective perturbations, down-sampling and compression also attenuate intrinsic image details. In the absence of appropriate priors, this may lead to blurred or distorted reconstructions after super-resolution, as demonstrated in Fig. 5. Therefore, for non-facial regions, we directly apply a general SR model to the adversarial example $`\hat{x}`$, followed by down-sampling, as illustrated in Fig.4(b,c), to better preserve geometric structures outside the facial area. Finally, guided by a face-specific SR model, the purified facial and non-facial regions are seamlessly fused to produce the final output, as shown in Fig.4(f). The overall purification process can be formulated as follows:

``` math
x_{f}=\mathrm{SR}_{f}\!\left(\mathrm{Down}(J(\hat{x}))\right)+\lambda\cdot\hat{x},
```
``` math
x_{g}=\mathrm{Down}\!\left(\mathrm{SR}_{g}(\hat{x})\right),
```

``` math
x^{\prime}=\mathrm{Mask}\odot x_{f}+(1-\mathrm{Mask})\odot x_{g}.
```

where $`\mathrm{Mask}`$ denotes a semantic segmentation mask used to distinguish facial regions from background regions, $`\mathrm{SR}_{f}(\cdot)`$ and $`\mathrm{SR}_{g}(\cdot)`$ represent the face-specific and general-purpose SR models, respectively, $`x_{f}`$ and $`x_{g}`$ denote their corresponding outputs, and $`x^{\prime}`$ denotes the final purified image.

![Refer to caption](https://arxiv.org/html/2604.23688v1/figures/pure_img.png)

Figure 6. Qualitative results of purification images.

Following common practice, we adopt Lanczos interpolation for image resizing and set the JPEG quality factor to 75. The weighting parameter $`\lambda`$ is fixed to 0.2. The segmentation mask $`\mathrm{Mask}`$ is generated using BiSeNet (Yu et al., 2018). To balance purification effectiveness and computational efficiency, we employ a lightweight general SR model, ESRGAN (Wang et al., 2018), for background regions and a lightweight face-specific SR model, GFPGAN (Wang et al., 2021a), for facial regions. All model parameters are adopted directly from publicly available open-source projects, and no additional training or fine-tuning is performed. A comparison with state-of-the-art super-resolution models is provided in Appendix.

### 4.3. Experimental Results

The experiments were conducted on a server running Ubuntu 22.04.4 LTS, equipped with two Intel(R) Xeon(R) Gold 6133 CPUs clocked at 2.50 GHz and one NVIDIA GeForce RTX 4090 GPU with 24 GB of memory. Comparisons with recent super-resolution models are provided in the appendix. We perform qualitative analysis using the evaluation metrics introduced in Section 3.

Experimental results demonstrate that TIP-RSR consistently outperforms existing purification methods across a wide range of evaluation metrics. These results indicate that TIP-RSR is able to effectively suppress adversarial perturbations while largely preserving structural fidelity and perceptual quality of the input images. It is worth noting that TIP-RSR performs slightly worse than DiffPure on the BRISQUE metric. This behavior can be attributed to the fusion of super-resolved images with adversarial samples at a fixed ratio, which introduces a mild distribution shift from natural images. Nevertheless, qualitative results confirms that this design leads to improved perceptual quality, as illustrated in Fig. 6. In contrast, images purified by DiffPure tend to appear overly smooth, resulting in noticeable loss of fine-grained details.

Finally, we evaluate the computational efficiency of TIP-RSR. Compared to recently released purification approaches and general super-resolution methods, TIP-RSR exhibits substantially lower processing cost. As shown in Table 4, TIP-RSR requires only 1.256 seconds on average to process a single image, making it approximately 13.5× faster than DiffPure. This efficiency stems from the fact that TIP-RSR produces purified outputs via a single forward pass, whereas competing methods typically rely on iterative or multi-step denoising procedures. Such efficiency highlights the strong potential of TIP-RSR for real-time applications. More importantly, the low purification cost further underscores the necessity of accounting for common real-world image transformations when designing defense models, as neglecting these factors may significantly overestimate their practical robustness.

#### 4.3.3. Ablation Study

Ablation Study. We conduct an ablation study to validate the effectiveness of our proposed TIP-RSR pipeline. Specifically, we compare it against three core baseline components: (1) ESRGAN applied directly to the adversarial image (containing intact perturbations); (2) GFPGAN applied to the JPEG-compressed and resized version of the adversarial image (where perturbations have been largely removed); (3)ESRGAN+GFPGAN, a mask-based fusion of the two outputs.

As shown in Table 7, our full TIP-RSR method consistently achieves the best or second-best performance across all key metrics—including SSIM, PSNR, FID, and LPIPS—on both the Facelock and Silencer-I adversarial defenses. This demonstrates that TIP-RSR effectively integrates and surpasses the individual contributions of its constituent modules.

Critically, TIP-RSR outperforms the ESRGAN+GFPGAN baseline in most key metrics, despite both approaches combining outputs from the two branches. This improvement can be attributed to our method’s adaptive integration strategy, which retains a controlled proportion of information from the original adversarial image through structured feature fusion, rather than relying on a fixed mask-based blend. By preserving certain high-frequency structural cues that are otherwise lost in the fully preprocessed GFPGAN branch, TIP-RSR achieves a better balance between perturbation suppression and detail fidelity, leading to higher perceptual and quantitative quality. The results validate that a purposefully designed, adaptive fusion mechanism is more effective than a straightforward mask-based combination.

## 5. Conclusion

In this work, we present a comprehensive analysis of perturbation-based proactive defense methods for portrait images, using image editing and talking-face generation (TFG) as representative application scenarios. Extensive experimental results indicate that existing approaches struggle to maintain their effectiveness when subjected to common real-world transformations, such as scale variations and color compression. Motivated by these findings, and to further raise awareness of potential risks, we introduce TIP-RSR, a training-free purification approach built upon super-resolution techniques. The proposed method consistently outperforms state-of-the-art diffusion-based purification baselines while requiring less than one-tenth of the computational cost. We also encourage the community to place greater emphasis on evaluating and improving defense robustness under realistic deployment conditions, particularly in the presence of diverse and unavoidable image transformations.

## Appendix A Protective Perturbations Degrade under Real-world Image Transformations

To further analyze the robustness of proactive defenses under practical deployment conditions, we separately evaluate two common post-defense image transformations: JPEG compression and image resizing. In this setting, Protected denotes directly using the protected image as input for generation, while Protected + JPEG and Protected + Resize denote applying JPEG compression or resizing to the protected image before feeding it into the downstream generator.

As shown in Tables 8 and 9, once protected images are subjected to these common real-world transformations, the quality of the generated outputs consistently improves across both image editing and talking face generation (TFG) tasks. In most cases, SSIM and PSNR increase, while FID, LPIPS, and M-LMD decrease, indicating that the generated results become closer to those obtained from clean inputs and exhibit better visual fidelity and structural consistency.

These results further support our main conclusion that existing perturbation-based proactive defenses are highly sensitive to post-processing operations commonly encountered in real-world image dissemination. ## Appendix B Quantitative Results on Multiple Defenses

To further evaluate the cross-defense effectiveness of TIP-RSR, we test it against four additional proactive defense methods with different optimization strategies and perturbation patterns. The goal of this experiment is not merely to enhance image quality, but to examine whether TIP-RSR can consistently neutralize diverse protective perturbations and thereby weaken the intended defensive effect.

As shown in Table 10, after applying TIP-RSR, the purified images consistently exhibit improved reconstruction quality across all evaluated defenses, as reflected by higher SSIM and PSNR and generally lower FID and LPIPS. These improvements indicate that the protective perturbations embedded in the defended images are effectively removed or substantially suppressed, making the purified images closer to natural clean images.

These results demonstrate that TIP-RSR is not tailored to a specific defense mechanism, but can broadly invalidate multiple perturbation-based protection methods. In other words, once processed by TIP-RSR, the protected images become significantly more usable for downstream generation models, suggesting that the original defensive effect is largely weakened or even neutralized. This strong cross-defense transferability further highlights the practical risk that existing proactive defenses may fail when facing simple but effective purification pipelines.

## Appendix C Analysis of Super-Resolution Model Selection

To justify the model design in TIP-RSR, we compare several representative super-resolution (SR) models for adversarial image purification under the Silencer-I defense setting. The purpose of this experiment is not simply to identify the newest or strongest SR model on generic restoration benchmarks, but to determine which models are most suitable for our purification objective. Different from standard SR, our task requires not only recovering visual details, but also suppressing protective perturbations while preserving identity-related facial structures and maintaining downstream generative usability.

As shown in Table 11, newer SR models do not necessarily yield better purification performance in this setting. Although TVT, a recently proposed SR model, achieves competitive restoration quality, it requires over 27 seconds per image, making it substantially less practical for our scenario. In contrast, ESRGAN and GFPGAN provide a more favorable balance between purification effectiveness, reconstruction quality, and computational efficiency. Specifically, ESRGAN achieves the highest SSIM and PSNR, indicating stronger structural fidelity, while GFPGAN obtains the lowest FID and LPIPS, suggesting better perceptual restoration quality.

These observations are consistent with the design motivation of TIP-RSR. ESRGAN is more suitable for preserving global structure and non-facial details, whereas GFPGAN, benefiting from a face-specific prior, is more effective at restoring realistic facial regions after perturbation suppression. As further illustrated in Fig. 5, both models exhibit more favorable restoration behavior than newer alternatives in the adversarial purification setting. Therefore, instead of adopting the most recent SR model by default, we deliberately choose ESRGAN and GFPGAN because they better satisfy the practical requirements of our method: efficiently weakening defensive perturbations while preserving visually and semantically usable content.

## Appendix D Comparison with Vision Foundation Models (VFMs) for Image Purification

To further investigate the practical security implications of our method, we compare TIP-RSR—a lightweight, training-free purification pipeline—with several large-scale vision foundation models (VFMs) accessed via their official APIs. Unlike TIP-RSR, these VFMs rely on massive model capacity and extensive training, making them significantly more computationally expensive and less accessible in real-world scenarios.

As shown in Table 12 for Image Purification ‣ Do Protective Perturbations Really Protect Portrait Privacy under Real-world Image Transformations?"), despite its simplicity and training-free, TIP-RSR achieves superior or comparable performance to these large-scale models. In particular, TIP-RSR attains the highest SSIM and PSNR, indicating better structural fidelity, while achieving competitive results on perceptual metrics such as FID and LPIPS. In contrast, although VFMs exhibit strong generative capabilities, they fail to consistently recover clean image structures after defensive perturbations, leading to inferior overall reconstruction quality.

More importantly, this comparison reveals a critical security concern. TIP-RSR requires no training, minimal computational resources, and can be easily deployed, yet it is sufficient to effectively suppress protective perturbations and restore high-quality inputs for downstream generation models. This suggests that existing perturbation-based defenses can be bypassed by extremely low-cost purification pipelines, even without access to large-scale models.

Qualitative results in Fig. 8 for Image Purification ‣ Do Protective Perturbations Really Protect Portrait Privacy under Real-world Image Transformations?") further support this observation. Compared to VFM-based purification, TIP-RSR produces cleaner structures and more natural facial details, indicating that the defensive perturbations are effectively removed.

Overall, these findings highlight a fundamental vulnerability of current proactive defense methods: their robustness can be compromised by simple, efficient, and widely accessible purification techniques, raising significant concerns about their reliability in real-world deployment.
