# Two-Stream Spatial-Temporal Transformer Framework for Person Identification via Natural Conversational Keypoints

Masoumeh Chapariniya1, Hossein Ranjbar, Teodora Vuković, Sarah Ebling, Volker Dellwo 1Department of Computational Linguistics, University of Zurich, Zurich, Switzerland\
masoumeh.chapariniya@uzh.ch, hoseinrbr@gmail.com, teodora.vukovic2@uzh.ch,\
ebling@cl.uzh.ch, volker.dellwo@uzh.ch

###### Abstract

In the age of AI-driven generative technologies, traditional biometric recognition systems face unprecedented challenges, particularly from sophisticated deepfake and face reenactment techniques. In this study, we propose a Two-Stream Spatial-Temporal Transformer Framework for person identification using upper body keypoints visible during online conversations, which we term ”conversational keypoints”. Our framework processes both spatial relationships between keypoints and their temporal evolution through two specialized branches: a Spatial Transformer (STR) that learns distinctive structural patterns in keypoint configurations, and a Temporal Transformer (TTR) that captures sequential motion patterns. Using the state-of-the-art Sapiens pose estimator, we extract 133 keypoints (based on COCO-WholeBody format) representing facial features, head pose, and hand positions. The framework was evaluated on a dataset of 114 individuals engaged in natural conversations, achieving recognition accuracies of 80.12% for the spatial stream, 63.61% for the temporal stream. We then explored two fusion strategies: a shared loss function approach achieving 82.22% accuracy, and a feature-level fusion method that concatenates feature maps from both streams, significantly improving performance to 94.86%. By jointly modeling both static anatomical relationships and dynamic movement patterns, our approach learns comprehensive identity signatures that are more robust to spoofing than traditional appearance-based methods.

###### Index Terms:

Person identification, conversational keypoints, Spatial-temporal transformer, Sapiens pose estimation model.

## I Introduction

The evolution of person identification systems has seen a significant shift from reliance on static biometric features to the integration of dynamic characteristics. Static features encompass physiological traits such as facial geometry, fingerprints, and hand measurements \[1\], which remain consistent over time. Dynamic features, on the other hand, pertain to behavioral patterns like gait \[2\], voice \[4\], and signature dynamics\[3, 10\], capturing the unique ways individuals perform actions. Early research primarily focused on static biometrics; however, the limitations in addressing variations due to pose, expression, and environmental factors led to the exploration of dynamic biometrics. Studies have demonstrated that combining static and dynamic features enhances recognition accuracy. For instance, the fusion of body measurements with gait dynamics has been shown to improve person identification performance \[14\]. Farhadipour et al. \[11\] explored multimodal approaches for person identification and verification by integrating facial and voice features. They proposed three fusion strategies: sensor-level, feature-level, and score-level fusion. For voice, they employed gammatonegram representations and x-vectors, while VGGFace2 was used for the face modality. While recent studies have explored the recognition of individuals through head, hands, and facial movements \[5\], this area remains underexplored, leaving ample opportunities to address numerous unanswered questions. Hill and Johnston \[15\] explored how head and facial movements independently convey identity-related information beyond static facial features. Using motion capture and animation techniques, the authors demonstrate that rigid head movements are particularly effective for distinguishing individuals, while non-rigid facial movements are more useful for categorizing sex. The study highlights the critical role of dynamic motion in face-based recognition tasks, providing a foundation for leveraging head and facial movements in person identification systems, especially in contexts where static features are unreliable. Girges et al.\[16\] investigated the role of facial motion in identity recognition using marker-less motion capture technology to generate realistic facial animations. They captured both rigid (e.g., head movements) and non-rigid (e.g., expressions) motions from human actors during natural speech, applying the data to a computer-generated face. By eliminating individual facial appearance cues, they assessed participants’ ability to discriminate identities solely based on motion patterns. The results demonstrated that participants could accurately recognize identities using only motion cues, highlighting the significance of dynamic facial motion in human face perception and identity recognition. \[20\] investigated the role of different types of facial movements in conveying identity information. Using a motion capture system, they recorded and animated emotional, emotional-in-social-interaction, and conversational facial movements. Their findings showed that conversational facial movements transmitted the most identity-related information, while purely emotional expressions conveyed the least. They concluded that conversational facial dynamics, with their subtle and personal nuances, offer significant cues for person identification, suggesting their utility for biometric systems in dynamic and interactive environments. These studies \[15, 16, 20\] relied on motion capture techniques and human experiments rather than computational models like deep neural networks. Papadopoulos et al. \[17\] proposed a novel framework for 3D dynamic face identification using spatio-temporal graph convolutional networks (ST-GCN). Their method utilized 3D facial landmarks to construct graphs capturing both spatial and temporal features of dynamic facial expressions. They used the BU4DFE \[6\] dataset which is a 3D dynamic facial expression database consisting of 101 subjects performing 6 facial expressions. Kay et al. \[18\] explored the potential of facial micro-expressions for person recognition using deep learning. Their study demonstrated that subtle and spontaneous micro-expressions provide unique identity-related cues. By leveraging the CASME II\[8\] and SAMM\[7\] datasets and employing a SlowFast CNN model to capture fine-grained temporal dynamics, they achieved notable improvements in recognition accuracy. Saracbasi et al. \[19\] introduced the MYFED database to analyze six basic emotions for person identification. Their approach leveraged facial landmarks and dynamic features across expression phases, such as onset and apex, to highlight the identity-related significance of emotions like surprise and happiness. The study emphasized the critical role of dynamic emotional expressions in improving biometric system reliability in real-world conditions. Recent studies primarily rely on datasets collected in controlled laboratory settings when they want the participants to express the six universal emotion categories. These datasets typically consist of high-quality videos captured under ideal lighting and consistent camera angles. In contrast, real-world video data, though abundant, is recorded in natural, uncontrolled environments, exhibiting significant variations in lighting, camera angles, and quality. Our approach utilized a Two-Stream Spatial-Temporal Transformer (ST-TR) framework for person identification leveraging both structural and dynamic patterns extracted from conversational keypoints. Unlike existing methods that rely solely on static facial features or simple motion patterns, our approach comprehensively analyzes both spatial configurations and temporal evolution of upper body keypoints captured during natural conversations from the CANDOR corpus \[26\]. Our key contributions are as follows:

- •
  We introduce a holistic framework that leverages 133 COCO whole-body keypoints \[12\] to capture both anatomical relationships and motion signatures during conversations and combine them to create a robust biometric representation.
- •
  We adopt and adapt the ST-TR architecture \[24\] originally designed for action recognition, as its dual-stream design is particularly well-suited for our person identification task. The architecture’s Spatial Transformer (STR) branch excels at capturing distinctive structural patterns in keypoint configurations, while its Temporal Transformer (TTR) branch effectively models sequential motion patterns. We selected this architecture based on its proven ability to disentangle spatial and temporal features in skeletal data, which is crucial for distinguishing individual behavioral patterns during conversations. While previous applications focused on action classification, we demonstrate that with appropriate modifications to the feature fusion strategy, this architecture can effectively learn person-specific biometric signatures from conversational dynamics.
- •
  We demonstrate the effectiveness of combining spatial and temporal information through extensive experiments on naturalistic conversational data, achieving 82.22% for shared loss function method and 94.86% for feature-level fusion.
- •
  We establish conversational keypoints as a promising new biometric modality that is inherently more resilient to spoofing compared to traditional appearance-based methods. By analyzing both spatial structure and temporal dynamics, our approach provides a more comprehensive identity signature that is difficult to replicate.
- •
  By evaluating our framework on the CANDOR corpus of natural conversations, we provide insights into how individuals maintain distinctive spatial-temporal patterns during real interactions, opening new directions for behavioral biometrics that go beyond controlled experimental settings.

  The rest of the paper is organized as follows: Section II describes our proposed methodology, including the ST-TR framework and feature extraction techniques. Section III presents comprehensive experimental results and performance analysis, and Section IV concludes with discussions and future research directions.

## II Proposed Method

The block diagram of the proposed method is shown in figure1. This framework consists of the following main subblocks: a person detection module for identifying and cropping the region of interest specific to the person, a pose estimation module for extracting keypoints, and a spatial-temporal transformer network for robust identity representation. In the rest of this section, we provide a detailed explanation of each component. (See Figure 1.)

### II-A Person Detection

The person detection module forms the foundation of the proposed framework. We employed the YOLOv8 model \[21\], an object detection architecture known for its accuracy, speed, and efficiency in real-time applications. Its anchor-free design and adaptive computation for variable image sizes enable reliable detection even in challenging scenarios involving occlusions or diverse lighting conditions. In our framework, YOLOv8 is utilized to isolate and extract the person’s region, ensuring high precision in identifying individuals. This step is critical for downstream tasks, as it provides clean, localized input for subsequent pose estimation and identity representation processes.

### II-B Pose Estimation

The pose estimation module leverages the Sapiens model \[22\], a state-of-the-art system trained on over 300 million in-the-wild human images from the Humans-300M dataset. We utilize the Sapiens-0.3B variant (0.3 billion parameters) to extract COCO-WholeBody format keypoints, capturing detailed facial expressions, hand gestures, and body movements. The model’s robust performance across varying lighting conditions and partial occlusions ensures reliable keypoint extraction in natural conversational settings. This high-fidelity pose data is the foundation for our framework’s ability to model distinctive identity signatures through both structural and temporal patterns.

### II-C Spatial-Temporal Transformer Network

The Spatial-Temporal Transformer Network (ST-TR) serves as the core component of our person identification system, effectively capturing both spatial relationships and temporal motion patterns of keypoints.

#### Network Architecture

Our implementation processes skeleton sequences of dimension (C×T×V×M), where C=3 represents coordinate channels, T=60 frames capture temporal dynamics, V=133 represents COCO WholeBody keypoints (including face, body, hands, and feet), and M=1 denotes single-person tracking. The network employs a two-stream architecture as illustrated in figure 2.

#### Spatial Transformer Stream (STR)

The STR stream models spatial relationships between keypoints within each frame through Spatial Self-Attention (SSA). Building on initial features extracted by ST-GCN layers \[25\], SSA computes dynamic attention scores across all keypoint pairs:

```math
A_{s}=\text{softmax}\left(\frac{(Q_{s}K_{s}^{T})}{\sqrt{d}}\right)
```

where $`Q_{s}`$ and $`K_{s}`$ are learned queries and key matrices. Unlike traditional fixed graph structures, this dynamic attention mechanism captures:

- •
  Distinctive upper body posture configurations
- •
  Person-specific spatial correlations in keypoint arrangements

#### Temporal Transformer Stream (TTR)

The TTR stream analyzes the temporal evolution of keypoints through Temporal Self-Attention (TSA):

```math
A_{t}=\text{softmax}\left(\frac{(Q_{t}K_{t}^{T})}{\sqrt{d}}\right)
```

This temporal modeling captures dynamic motion signatures through:

- •
  Characteristic timing patterns in conversational gestures
- •
  Distinctive temporal coordination between face and hand movements
- •
  Long-range dependencies in personal interaction styles

### II-D Training Strategy and Loss Function

We employed a four-phase training strategy:

- •
  Independent STR training: Transfer learning from action recognition on the Kinetics-400 dataset \[9\] facilitates spatial feature extraction, accelerating convergence and enhancing performance compared to training from scratch
- •
  Independent TTR training: Similarly, leveraging pretrained weights from Kinetics-400 provides a strong foundation for temporal motion understanding, improving convergence and performance by utilizing shared temporal patterns between action and identity recognition.
- •
  Joint training with shared loss function: In this section, as shown in Figure 2, we employed a strategy to train both branches simultaneously, optimizing the entire dual-stream architecture with equal weighting of their respective loss functions. Although we tested various weighting coefficients, this balanced ratio achieved the best performance.

  ```math
  L_{\text{total}}=0.5\times CELoss_{\text{STR}}+0.5\times CELoss_{\text{TTR}}
  ```

  ```math
  L_{\text{Cross Entropy}}(\hat{y},y)=-\sum_{k=1}^{K}y^{(k)}\log\hat{y}^{(k)}
  ```

  where $`CELoss_{\text{STR}}`$ and $`CELoss_{\text{TTR}}`$ are cross-entropy losses for person classification, $`K`$ denotes the number of classes, $`y^{(k)}`$ indicates the true label, and $`\hat{y}^{(k)}`$ represents predicted probabilities.

- •
  Joint training with feature fusion: We propose a feature-level fusion approach that combines high-level features from both streams before classification. Specifically, 256-dimensional L2-normalized feature vectors from each stream are concatenated channel-wise into a 512-dimensional representation. This fused vector passes through a multi-layer fusion classifier with two linear layers (512→512→256) interleaved with batch normalization, ReLU activation, and dropout (0.2), followed by a final classification layer (256→114). The network is trained end-to-end using a weighted loss combination:

  ```math
  L_{\text{total}}=0.3\times\text{CELoss}_{\text{STR}}+0.3\times\text{CELoss}_{\text{TTR}}\\
  +0.4\times\text{CELoss}_{\text{FUSION}}
  ```

Our network design is motivated by key insights about person identification from conversational dynamics. The separation of spatial and temporal processing through parallel streams allows learning both static postural characteristics and dynamic motion patterns unique to each individual. The attention mechanisms adapt to different conversational scenarios, while the architecture’s ability to handle long sequences captures sustained behavioral patterns. Our progressive training strategy and feature fusion approach ensure complementary information from both streams contributes to identity recognition, with dropout and normalization layers preventing overfitting. This architecture thus provides a robust framework for learning identity signatures from natural conversations.

## III Experiments

### III-A Dataset

#### Original Dataset

The emergence of deepfake technologies highlights the need to explore dynamic biometric features for person identification, as static features become increasingly unreliable in such scenarios. Research by \[20\] has shown that conversational facial movements carry more identity-specific information than emotional expressions alone. To analyze these dynamic patterns, we utilized the CANDOR corpus, which includes 1,656 natural conversations spanning over 850 hours of footage. Unlike databases that focus on posed expressions (e.g., BU4DFE, CASME II, MYFED), CANDOR captures spontaneous, real-world interactions, making it ideal for developing robust biometric features based on natural behavioral patterns. With a diverse participant pool representing various demographics, the dataset provides a rich source of conversational data, including vocal, facial, and gestural expressions. As one of the largest publicly available collections of naturalistic conversational data, CANDOR is uniquely suited for studying dynamic biometric features in real-world conditions.

#### Our Subset for Person Recognition

To tailor the CANDOR dataset for our specific person recognition task, we constructed a subset and processed the CANDOR corpus as follows:

- •
  Subject Selection: From the complete corpus, we selected 114 unique individuals who participated in multiple sessions. For each individual, we selected their longest recording session to ensure sufficient data for analysis.
- •
  Video Segmentation: We segmented the full sessions into shorter utterance clips using the provided transcription timestamps. The resulting clips range from 3.03 to 4 seconds (average 3.9 seconds). The videos maintain frame rate (30 fps) and resolution (320x240 pixels).
- •
  Data Split: For each subject, we employed an 80:20 train-test split ratio, ensuring the same segments do not appear in both sets to avoid potential bias.
- •
  Temporal Sampling: To optimize computational efficiency while preserving motion dynamics, we implemented a frame skip parameter of 2, extracting keypoints from alternating frames.

### III-B Implementation Details

We implemented our framework using PyTorch and trained it on a single NVIDIA GeForce 4080 GPU with 12 GB memory. We employed YOLOv8 for person detection and the Sapiens-0.3B pose estimation model. YOLOv8 detects individuals in each video frame, while Sapiens extracts 133 keypoints in the COCO WholeBody format. Video frames were resized to 1024×768 pixels before being processed by the Sapiens model. To reduce computational overhead, we applied frame skipping by processing every second frame. The extracted keypoints were stored in structured JSON files for use in our identification framework. We initially train each stem, STR, and TTR, independently with standard cross-entropy loss. Subsequently, as illustrated in Figure  2, we train them jointly using the defined shared loss function and feature fusion methods. These two transformer streams share several key architectural and training characteristics. Both streams process sequences of 60 frames, utilize 8 attention heads with dimension ratios dk=0.25 and dv=0.25, and are trained for 120 epochs with learning rate adjustments. For STR, we employed the Adam optimizer with an initial learning rate of 0.01 and weight decay of 0.0001. The TTR used SGD optimizer with momentum, featuring an initial learning rate of 0.001 and weight decay of 0.0001 In the joint training with the shared loss function and feature fusion, we maintained a batch size of 32 for training and 8 for testing. The Adam optimizer with an initial learning rate of 0.01 and weight decay of 0.0001 is utilized for both methods. We used equal weights (0.5) for both STR and TTR streams in the shared loss computation to ensure balanced learning between spatial and temporal features. (See Equation 3.) For feature fusion training, the loss function formulated in Equation 5 assigns weights to each cross entropy loss.

### III-C Performance Evaluation

In this study, we conducted extensive experiments to evaluate three transformer-based architectures for person identification: Spatial Transformer (STR), Temporal Transformer (TTR), STTR with shared loss function and feature-level fusion. Our experimental results show the following progression in performance (see Tabel  I):

#### Individual Stream Analysis

- •
  The STR achieved 80.12% accuracy, demonstrating that spatial relationships between facial, hand, and body keypoints contain significant identity-discriminative information.
- •
  The TTR achieves 63.61% accuracy, demonstrating that temporal patterns in conversational dynamics contain important identity-specific information. This performance indicates that individuals exhibit characteristic temporal signatures in their movements during natural interactions..
- •
  The relatively lower TTR performance compared to STR likely stems from the inherent complexity of modeling temporal dependencies across longer sequences and the natural variation in conversational dynamics.

#### Joint Stream Performance

- •
  The shared loss function approach improved accuracy to 82.22%, showing modest gains from joint optimization.
- •
  The feature-level fusion strategy significantly boosted performance to 94.86%, indicating that the careful combination of spatial and temporal features is crucial for robust identification.

The substantial improvement from feature-level fusion stems from the concatenation of L2-normalized 256-dimensional features from each stream, preserving their distinct characteristics and ensuring balanced contributions. Additional fusion layers optimize feature combinations while maintaining discriminative power, and a weighted loss function ensures balanced learning, preventing dominance by either stream. Our analysis reveals key insights about identity information in conversational dynamics. The strong performance of the spatial stream shows that individuals maintain characteristic spatial configurations in their facial and upper body keypoints, while temporal features provide crucial complementary information for resolving ambiguous cases. The significant improvement achieved through feature-level fusion (94.86% vs 82.22%) demonstrates that spatial and temporal patterns offer complementary information about identity, suggesting that robust person identification requires considering both static configurations and dynamic evolution of keypoints during conversations.

#### Comparisons with Other techniques

Table  II presents a comparison of our methods with other research. While direct comparison is challenging due to the unique nature of our task, which focuses on conversational keypoints and naturalistic data, our model achieves promising results. Unlike most existing works that concentrate on controlled expressions or specific gestures, our approach addresses the complexities of unconstrained conversational behavior, highlighting its novelty and effectiveness.

#### Suggestions and Feature Work

Our framework reveals several promising avenues for future research and improvement. A critical next step is expanding the dataset size and increasing utterance length from the current 3-4 seconds to 8-10 seconds, which would allow more robust temporal feature extraction and potentially reduce the impact of short-term behavioral variations. The current performance variability, particularly in the temporal stream, suggests the need for more sophisticated sequence modeling approaches and advanced feature fusion strategies. Exploring additional modalities like audio cues and developing more sophisticated attention mechanisms could further enhance the framework’s person identification capabilities. Future work should also focus on improving generalizability by incorporating more diverse conversational contexts and demographic variations, ultimately creating a more comprehensive and reliable biometric identification system.

## IV Conclusion

This work introduces a Two-Stream Spatial-Temporal Transformer Framework that achieves 94.86% accuracy in person identification by leveraging both spatial relationships and temporal dynamics of conversational keypoints. Our framework’s success on natural conversational data from the CANDOR corpus demonstrates the effectiveness of combining structural patterns through STR and dynamic motion through TTR, creating robust biometric signatures that are inherently resistant to spoofing. The significant improvement achieved through feature-level fusion highlights the complementary nature of spatial and temporal information in conversational behavior. This research establishes conversational keypoints as a promising new biometric modality, particularly valuable in an era where traditional appearance-based methods face increasing challenges from deepfake technologies.
