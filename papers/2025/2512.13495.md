###### Abstract

We propose a multimodal-driven framework for high-fidelity long-term digital human animation termed Soul, which generates semantically coherent videos from a single-frame portrait image, text prompts, and audio, achieving precise lip synchronization, vivid facial expressions, and robust identity preservation. We construct Soul-1M, containing 1 million finely annotated samples with a precise automated annotation pipeline (covering portrait, upper-body, full-body, and multi-person scenes) to mitigate data scarcity, and we carefully curate Soul-Bench for comprehensive and fair evaluation of audio-/text-guided animation methods. The model is built on the Wan2.2-5B backbone, integrating audio-injection layers and multiple training strategies together with threshold-aware codebook replacement to ensure long-term generation consistency. Meanwhile, step/CFG distillation and a lightweight VAE are used to optimize inference efficiency, achieving an 11.4$`\times`$ speedup with negligible quality loss. Extensive experiments show that Soul significantly outperforms current leading open-source and commercial models on video quality, video-text alignment, identity preservation, and lip-synchronization accuracy, demonstrating broad applicability in real-world scenarios such as virtual anchors and film production.

## 1 Introduction

Digital human animation has become pivotal in entertainment, education, and telepresence, demanding semantic consistency with text/audio, high-fidelity, high-resoltuion, and long-duration generation \[[1](https://arxiv.org/html/2512.13495v1#bib.bib1), [2](https://arxiv.org/html/2512.13495v1#bib.bib2), [3](https://arxiv.org/html/2512.13495v1#bib.bib3)\]. However, existing methods struggle to balance these requirements, limiting real-world deployment. This work targets generating dynamic, multimodal-aligned human videos from multimodal inputs (single image + text + audio), bridging the gap between technical feasibility and practical utility.

Despite advances in video generation and audio-driven animation, three critical challenges remain unaddressed: First, datasets lack comprehensive coverage and fine-grained annotations: existing collections are biased toward single scenarios (e.g., only portraits) and lack detailed labels for actions, gestures, and scene dynamics, hindering model generalization. And the size of the training set is also important for practical performance. As shown in [Fig. 1](https://arxiv.org/html/2512.13495v1#S1.F1 "In 1 Introduction ‣ Soul: Breathe Life into Digital Human for High-fidelity Long-term Multimodal Animation"), the latest Wan-S2V \[[2](https://arxiv.org/html/2512.13495v1#bib.bib2)\] and OmniAvatar \[[4](https://arxiv.org/html/2512.13495v1#bib.bib4)\] (which only include 1.3K hours of training data) both fail to enable humans to ”walk” effectively. Second, long-term inference suffers from identity drift and semantic degradation, where latent feature shifts in extended generation break consistency, as models are trained on short clips but deployed for long sequences. It will also lead to a decline in the quality of the generated video, which may appear artifacts or blurriness. Third, high-resolution (1080P) and high-fidelity generation conflicts with efficiency: mainstream models either compromise quality for speed or require prohibitive computational resources, limiting real-time applications.

![Refer to caption](https://arxiv.org/html/x1.png)

Figure 1: Soul produces clearer results (red box), achieves stronger text consistency (walking shown in yellow box), and is faster (30s 1080P in 2-Hours) over recent Wan-S2V \[[2](https://arxiv.org/html/2512.13495v1#bib.bib2)\] and OmniAvatar \[[4](https://arxiv.org/html/2512.13495v1#bib.bib4)\]. The data is derived from the AI-generated Soul-Bench, where the data is generated by the T2V model.

To tackle these challenges, we propose a holistic solution: 1) curate Soul-1M (see [Sec. 4](https://arxiv.org/html/2512.13495v1#S4 "4 Curating Soul-1M Dataset ‣ Soul: Breathe Life into Digital Human for High-fidelity Long-term Multimodal Animation")) with diverse scenarios and fine-grained multimodal annotations to enhance model generalization; 2) design a long-term generation pipeline (see [Sec. 3.2](https://arxiv.org/html/2512.13495v1#S3.SS2 "3.2 Long-term Human Video Generation ‣ 3 Soul: Multimodal Humam Animation ‣ Soul: Breathe Life into Digital Human for High-fidelity Long-term Multimodal Animation")) with pivotal frames, intra-clip overlap, and threshold-aware codebook replacement to mitigate identity drift and feature shift; 3) integrate audio-attention injection (see [Sec. 3.1](https://arxiv.org/html/2512.13495v1#S3.SS1 "3.1 Model Design ‣ 3 Soul: Multimodal Humam Animation ‣ Soul: Breathe Life into Digital Human for High-fidelity Long-term Multimodal Animation")) for precise lip-sync and text control; 4) optimize deployment for efficient high-resolution generation (see [Sec. 3.3](https://arxiv.org/html/2512.13495v1#S3.SS3 "3.3 Efficient Inference Deployment ‣ 3 Soul: Multimodal Humam Animation ‣ Soul: Breathe Life into Digital Human for High-fidelity Long-term Multimodal Animation")) to enhance its practical and commercial value. In summary, our contributions are threefold:

- •
  To support high-fidelity, long-term multimodal animation, we propose a large-scale and diverse Soul-1M with fine-grained annotations by establishing a principled automated annotation pipeline.
- •
  We introduce a novel multimodal Soul framework with a discrete codebook, enabling long-term, identity-preserving, and high-fidelity human animation. By incorporating distillation and an efficient eVAE, we significantly improve model efficiency with negligible performance degradation.
- •
  We also construct a high-quality Soul-Bench to comprehensively and fairly evaluate audio-driven human animation methods. Results show that our approach not only produces high-quality 1080P generation with superior performance, but also achieves higher efficiency.

## 2 Related Work

### 2.1 Video Generation Foundation Models

The development of diffusion techniques has substantially improved image generation quality, exemplified by the SD \[[5](https://arxiv.org/html/2512.13495v1#bib.bib5), [6](https://arxiv.org/html/2512.13495v1#bib.bib6), [7](https://arxiv.org/html/2512.13495v1#bib.bib7)\] and FLUX \[[8](https://arxiv.org/html/2512.13495v1#bib.bib8), [9](https://arxiv.org/html/2512.13495v1#bib.bib9)\] series. This progress was soon extended to video generation, giving rise to works such as AnimateDiff \[[10](https://arxiv.org/html/2512.13495v1#bib.bib10)\] and SVD \[[11](https://arxiv.org/html/2512.13495v1#bib.bib11)\], which advance video synthesis by expanding temporal modules. Transformer-based DiT architectures have become a standard component in subsequent video generation models; notably, Sora demonstrated a breakthrough in photorealistic generation, after which both open-source \[[12](https://arxiv.org/html/2512.13495v1#bib.bib12), [13](https://arxiv.org/html/2512.13495v1#bib.bib13), [14](https://arxiv.org/html/2512.13495v1#bib.bib14), [15](https://arxiv.org/html/2512.13495v1#bib.bib15), [16](https://arxiv.org/html/2512.13495v1#bib.bib16), [17](https://arxiv.org/html/2512.13495v1#bib.bib17), [18](https://arxiv.org/html/2512.13495v1#bib.bib18), [19](https://arxiv.org/html/2512.13495v1#bib.bib19)\] and closed-source \[[20](https://arxiv.org/html/2512.13495v1#bib.bib20), [21](https://arxiv.org/html/2512.13495v1#bib.bib21), [22](https://arxiv.org/html/2512.13495v1#bib.bib22), [23](https://arxiv.org/html/2512.13495v1#bib.bib23), [24](https://arxiv.org/html/2512.13495v1#bib.bib24), [25](https://arxiv.org/html/2512.13495v1#bib.bib25), [26](https://arxiv.org/html/2512.13495v1#bib.bib26), [27](https://arxiv.org/html/2512.13495v1#bib.bib27), [28](https://arxiv.org/html/2512.13495v1#bib.bib28), [29](https://arxiv.org/html/2512.13495v1#bib.bib29)\] models continued to improve generation quality. Wan2.1/2.2 \[[17](https://arxiv.org/html/2512.13495v1#bib.bib17)\] and HunyuanVideo \[[16](https://arxiv.org/html/2512.13495v1#bib.bib16)\] have attracted particular attention for their strong results and well-maintained open ecosystems, and have been applied across many downstream tasks. Other work has focused on high-resolution and long-term video generation by introducing autoregressive modeling or improved attention mechanisms. Considering both performance and efficiency, we adopt Wan2.2-5B \[[17](https://arxiv.org/html/2512.13495v1#bib.bib17)\] as our base video model.

### 2.2 Audio-Driven Talking Human Generation

Early audio-driven animation methods, such as SadTalker \[[30](https://arxiv.org/html/2512.13495v1#bib.bib30)\], mainly targeted facial motion, typically using 3D Morphable Models (3DMM) \[[31](https://arxiv.org/html/2512.13495v1#bib.bib31)\] to create animations and a GAN \[[32](https://arxiv.org/html/2512.13495v1#bib.bib32)\]-based training pipeline, but they suffered from limited lip-sync accuracy and lackluster expression vividness. More recently, diffusion-based approaches have become dominant, and the animated subject has moved beyond the face to full-body and even multi-person scenarios, which offer greater practical value. Some U-Net-based methods \[[33](https://arxiv.org/html/2512.13495v1#bib.bib33), [34](https://arxiv.org/html/2512.13495v1#bib.bib34), [35](https://arxiv.org/html/2512.13495v1#bib.bib35), [36](https://arxiv.org/html/2512.13495v1#bib.bib36), [37](https://arxiv.org/html/2512.13495v1#bib.bib37), [38](https://arxiv.org/html/2512.13495v1#bib.bib38)\] exploit pretrained denoising models to improve temporal consistency, yet they remain limited in identity preservation, audio conditioning control, and video extrapolation \[[1](https://arxiv.org/html/2512.13495v1#bib.bib1)\]. Hallo3 \[[39](https://arxiv.org/html/2512.13495v1#bib.bib39)\] was the first to leverage DiT \[[40](https://arxiv.org/html/2512.13495v1#bib.bib40)\]’s strong spatiotemporal modeling to handle more complex generation scenarios, a choice later adopted by AlignHuman \[[41](https://arxiv.org/html/2512.13495v1#bib.bib41)\], Lookahead Anchoring \[[42](https://arxiv.org/html/2512.13495v1#bib.bib42)\], and others. Subsequent works, e.g., FantasyID \[[43](https://arxiv.org/html/2512.13495v1#bib.bib43)\], FantasyTalking \[[44](https://arxiv.org/html/2512.13495v1#bib.bib44)\], HunyuanVideo-Avatar \[[45](https://arxiv.org/html/2512.13495v1#bib.bib45)\], InfinityHuman \[[46](https://arxiv.org/html/2512.13495v1#bib.bib46)\], build on powerful video foundation models such as CogVideoX \[[47](https://arxiv.org/html/2512.13495v1#bib.bib47)\], Wan \[[17](https://arxiv.org/html/2512.13495v1#bib.bib17)\], HunyuanVideo \[[16](https://arxiv.org/html/2512.13495v1#bib.bib16)\], and Goku \[[12](https://arxiv.org/html/2512.13495v1#bib.bib12)\]; by incorporating identity-reference networks or emotion control, these methods have achieved notable gains in consistency and expressiveness \[[4](https://arxiv.org/html/2512.13495v1#bib.bib4), [48](https://arxiv.org/html/2512.13495v1#bib.bib48), [49](https://arxiv.org/html/2512.13495v1#bib.bib49), [50](https://arxiv.org/html/2512.13495v1#bib.bib50), [2](https://arxiv.org/html/2512.13495v1#bib.bib2), [51](https://arxiv.org/html/2512.13495v1#bib.bib51)\]. Other works \[[52](https://arxiv.org/html/2512.13495v1#bib.bib52), [53](https://arxiv.org/html/2512.13495v1#bib.bib53), [54](https://arxiv.org/html/2512.13495v1#bib.bib54), [55](https://arxiv.org/html/2512.13495v1#bib.bib55), [56](https://arxiv.org/html/2512.13495v1#bib.bib56)\] introduce additional pose sequences or keypoint inputs to enable finer and more vivid control of the body, at the cost of reduced applicability breadth. There are also promising results from approaches using 3DGS \[[57](https://arxiv.org/html/2512.13495v1#bib.bib57)\], Mixture-of-Experts (MoE) \[[58](https://arxiv.org/html/2512.13495v1#bib.bib58)\], and autoregressive (AR) paradigms \[[59](https://arxiv.org/html/2512.13495v1#bib.bib59)\]. Beyond single-person animation, several methods have been extended to multi-person or more general scenarios \[[60](https://arxiv.org/html/2512.13495v1#bib.bib60), [61](https://arxiv.org/html/2512.13495v1#bib.bib61), [62](https://arxiv.org/html/2512.13495v1#bib.bib62)\]. Despite these advances, existing work still faces substantial challenges in achieving fine-grained text/audio control for human-centric videos, robust audio-lip synchronization, large-range motion synthesis, and consistent identity preservation.

### 2.3 Efficient Deployment

For virtual-human generation applications, inference speed is critical for both user experience and operational cost. In the single-GPU end-to-end video inference setting, training-free attention implementations such as FlashAttention \[[63](https://arxiv.org/html/2512.13495v1#bib.bib63), [64](https://arxiv.org/html/2512.13495v1#bib.bib64), [65](https://arxiv.org/html/2512.13495v1#bib.bib65)\] and the SageAttention family \[[66](https://arxiv.org/html/2512.13495v1#bib.bib66), [67](https://arxiv.org/html/2512.13495v1#bib.bib67), [68](https://arxiv.org/html/2512.13495v1#bib.bib68)\] are widely adopted, while SpargeAttention \[[69](https://arxiv.org/html/2512.13495v1#bib.bib69)\] introduces sparse-attention mechanisms at the cost of higher hardware adaptation requirements. Some trainable methods address the performance drop caused by sparse attention and quantization through finetuning, e.g., VSA \[[70](https://arxiv.org/html/2512.13495v1#bib.bib70)\] and FPSAttention \[[71](https://arxiv.org/html/2512.13495v1#bib.bib71)\]. We investigate deployment from three angles: 1) seamless, training-free replacements for attention modules; 2) step and CFG distillation to fundamentally reduce the runtime cost of multi-step inference; and 3) a lightweight decoder design to achieve substantially lower end-to-end inference latency compared with mainstream approaches.

## 3 Soul: Multimodal Humam Animation

![Refer to caption](https://arxiv.org/html/x2.png)

Figure 2: Overview of Soul for semantic-consistent and long-term multimodal-driven human video animation.

Soul aims to generate dynamic animated videos whose actions and scenes are semantically consistent with text prompts and whose lip-sync and facial expressions match the audio-from a single-frame human image (which supplies the subject, background, and style) under multimodal guidance from text and audio, as illustrated in [Fig. 2](https://arxiv.org/html/2512.13495v1#S3.F2 "In 3 Soul: Multimodal Humam Animation ‣ Soul: Breathe Life into Digital Human for High-fidelity Long-term Multimodal Animation"). In particular, the audio modality supports various vocal types including general talking and singing, while the textual modality enables fine-grained control over the generated video by specifying task behaviors, gestures, scene details, shot scale, and camera motion. The system also supports prompt-driven specification of single- or multi-character scenarios for generalized inference. Moreover, we investigate long-term identity-preserving video generation and efficient deployment, and we construct a large-scale Soul-1M to support training of the proposed algorithms.

### 3.1 Model Design

Video foundation model selection. We adopt the recent Wan2.2-5B \[[17](https://arxiv.org/html/2512.13495v1#bib.bib17)\] as our base video model. This model offers strong fidelity and robust general-scene generation capabilities, and its architecture is naturally advantageous for modeling human-world interactions. In addition, its larger downsampling factor enables direct generation of 1080P-resolution video, yielding substantially higher efficiency compared to open-source alternatives such as Wan2.2-1.3B \[[17](https://arxiv.org/html/2512.13495v1#bib.bib17)\] and HunyuanVideo \[[16](https://arxiv.org/html/2512.13495v1#bib.bib16)\].

Advancing DiT block for audio injection. For the audio input, we pre-extract features using Whisper \[[72](https://arxiv.org/html/2512.13495v1#bib.bib72)\] and inject them by adding an Audio-Attention layer into the DiT blocks. Notably, this module is initialized from the original text-attention weights to accelerates model convergence.

### 3.2 Long-term Human Video Generation

Pivotal representation. By default we generate a single clip\_(n) containing 109 frames in pixel space, which corresponds to 28 frames in the latent space. As shown in [Fig. 2](https://arxiv.org/html/2512.13495v1#S3.F2 "In 3 Soul: Multimodal Humam Animation ‣ Soul: Breathe Life into Digital Human for High-fidelity Long-term Multimodal Animation"), we treat the first frame as a pivotal representation that is padded/duplicated to serve as the first frame of each clip; this pivotal frame provides consistent reference information for the clip-namely the subject identity, background, and style. During training we randomly sample one frame from the source video to serve as the reference frame to improve the model’s generalization to different human identities and backgrounds; this reference is used only as conditioning input and is not involved in the loss computation.

Intra-clip overlap. Consistency and plausibility between consecutive clips during long-term inference are crucial, and relying solely on a reference human image cannot ensure them. In particular, person motion driven by text or camera motion can make a clip’s ending diverge substantially from the pivotal representation. Therefore, when generating the next clip we copy the last few frames of the previous clip into the beginning of the current clip in the latent space (default: 2 frames). This overlap markedly improves semantic coherence across frames. During training, this strategy is applied with a probability of 50%.

Threshold-aware codebook replacement. The two strategies above improve identity preservation and inter-frame consistency respectively, but we find that as inference length increases the generated video quality and scene coherence gradually degrade and can even become unusable (see [Fig. 7](https://arxiv.org/html/2512.13495v1#S5.F7 "In 5.1 Experimental Setup ‣ 5 Experiments ‣ Soul: Breathe Life into Digital Human for High-fidelity Long-term Multimodal Animation")). We attribute this failure to a “latent feature shift”: during training the model only sees short-term (single) clips, whereas at inference it operates in a long-term (multiple-clip) regime. The core problem is that the preceding last few features used as conditioning are generated at inference time and therefore have a distributional mismatch with the real latent features of the first clip.

To mitigate this, we use all examples from Soul-1M (see [Sec. 4](https://arxiv.org/html/2512.13495v1#S4 "4 Curating Soul-1M Dataset ‣ Soul: Breathe Life into Digital Human for High-fidelity Long-term Multimodal Animation")), pre-extract their latent features, and apply K-Means to form a set of clusters (we use 40K clusters by default) to construct a codebook that better matches the training distribution. We further introduce a thresholded cluster-assignment mechanism to replace each feature in the preceding frames via thresholded clipping. Concretely, for each feature we find its nearest cluster centroid: if the distance to that centroid is below a threshold, we keep the feature; if the distance exceeds the threshold, we move the feature toward the centroid and truncate its offset so that its distance to the centroid equals the threshold. This procedure keeps the preceding latent features close to the original training distribution while avoiding abrupt, wholesale replacements.

Hybrid-modality training with negative guidance. During training, we randomly mix in general videos without audio with a probability of 20% to improve the model’s ability to maintain diverse scene types. These auxiliary samples include cartoons, animals, natural scenery and urban architecture; for them the audio channel is set to all zeros. In addition, we synthesize human-related failure cases using negative prompts and include these examples in training. We observe that this augmentation further enhances the model’s temporal consistency.

### 3.3 Efficient Inference Deployment

To further improve user experience and reduce model cost, we optimize inference latency along two mutually compatible axes and ultimately achieve an 11.4-fold efficiency improvement compared with a naive inference implementation.

Step and CFG Distillation. Denoising steps have a large effect on output quality and by default we use 25 steps. Inspired by DMD2’s distribution-matching distillation \[[73](https://arxiv.org/html/2512.13495v1#bib.bib73)\], we extend Soul with simultaneous step and CFG (classifier-free guidance) distillation and remove the GAN loss. This modification substantially increases runtime speed while incurring only an acceptable accuracy degradation. As shown in [Tab. 3](https://arxiv.org/html/2512.13495v1#S5.T3 "In 5.1 Experimental Setup ‣ 5 Experiments ‣ Soul: Breathe Life into Digital Human for High-fidelity Long-term Multimodal Animation"), relative to the immediately preceding configuration, this yields a overall 7.5$`\times`$ improvement over the baseline model.

Advancing Efficient VAE. Beyond attention-centric optimizations, we observed that the decoder of Wan2.2-5B is relatively large: its parameter count and inference latency comprise a substantial fraction of the KD model’s footprint. To address this, we designed a lightweight encoder/decoder variant, as summarized in [Tab. 1](https://arxiv.org/html/2512.13495v1#S3.T1 "In 3.3 Efficient Inference Deployment ‣ 3 Soul: Multimodal Humam Animation ‣ Soul: Breathe Life into Digital Human for High-fidelity Long-term Multimodal Animation"). eVAE-Wan2.2-5B-35M reduces decoder parameters and MACs from 550.05M/688.58T to 34.97M/43.34T while keeping reconstructed video quality within an acceptable range, e.g., LPIPS only increases from 0.0324 to 0.052.

## 4 Curating Soul-1M Dataset

### 4.1 Soul-1M for Training

Human video data source. To cover diverse application scenarios, we construct the large-scale Soul-1M from the following complementary perspectives: 1) Portraits: We leverage open-source datasets including VoxCeleb2 \[[74](https://arxiv.org/html/2512.13495v1#bib.bib74)\], CelebV-Text \[[74](https://arxiv.org/html/2512.13495v1#bib.bib74)\], and VFHQ \[[75](https://arxiv.org/html/2512.13495v1#bib.bib75)\]. These collections encompass celebrities, general-purpose footage, and interview-style recordings with varied duration distributions. Because faces occupy a large portion of the frame in these samples, they help strengthen the model’s ability to preserve lip motion and identity consistency. 2) Upper-body: To support use cases that focus on torso-level content, we gather indoor and interview-style videos that emphasize the upper body and hands, thereby improving consistency of generated upper-body poses and hand movements. 3) Full-body: To enable realistic full-body human animation, we collect large-scale full-body videos from general scenes covering diverse subjects, interactions, environments, lighting conditions, and atmospheres. These data target real-world, high-fidelity human animation. In addition, we augment this collection with manually annotated action data for specific gestures to enhance the expressiveness of intrinsic human motions under text control. 4) Multi-person scenes: We also include multi-person general-scene videos for mixed training to improve the model’s generalization and robustness in multi-person scenarios.

Data filtering. Considering the variable quality of raw videos, we design an automated filtering pipeline consisting of: 1) Video resolution filtering: for high-definition target applications, we first remove clips below 480p at the source by retaining only videos whose short edge is greater than 480 pixels. 2) General-purpose model-based filtering: we detect scene cuts with PySceneDetect \[[76](https://arxiv.org/html/2512.13495v1#bib.bib76)\] in conjunction with DINOv2 \[[77](https://arxiv.org/html/2512.13495v1#bib.bib77)\], remove clips that do not contain faces or have low face-detection confidence using RetinaFace \[[78](https://arxiv.org/html/2512.13495v1#bib.bib78)\], assess aesthetic quality with FineVQ \[[79](https://arxiv.org/html/2512.13495v1#bib.bib79)\], delete clips containing heavy subtitles via PaddleOCR \[[80](https://arxiv.org/html/2512.13495v1#bib.bib80)\], and finally run a multimodal large language model (MLLM) \[[81](https://arxiv.org/html/2512.13495v1#bib.bib81)\] to comprehensively flag defects such as poor image quality, visible logos, or remaining subtitles. 3) Data-pool augmentation: we detect and temporally track each person’s keypoints with MMPose \[[82](https://arxiv.org/html/2512.13495v1#bib.bib82)\]. For full-body videos, we generate upper-body crops and add them to the upper-body pool when the resulting crops still satisfy the resolution requirement. We also augment the pool with a subset of animation and pet videos. 4) Audio-video alignment: for each human video we track the largest face and apply SyncNet \[[83](https://arxiv.org/html/2512.13495v1#bib.bib83)\] to evaluate audio-visual synchronization. Clips with persistent mismatch have their audio modality dropped while the video and its caption are retained for training. After this pipeline we obtain 300K portrait, 400K upper-body, and 100K full-body videos for training with totally 8.5K Hours.

Automatic fine-grained labeling. 1) We adopt the open-sourced Qwen3-VL \[[84](https://arxiv.org/html/2512.13495v1#bib.bib84), [81](https://arxiv.org/html/2512.13495v1#bib.bib81)\] as the base MLLM. We first determine human-centric event intervals based on “presence/absence of people” and “scene changes” (including scene changes caused by camera motion and transitions between stable scenes), and split each video into 4-5s sub-clips to ensure uniform annotation granularity and complete intervals. 2) For each sub-clip we design multi-type specialized prompts to comprehensively cover core attributes such as scene, shot type, human action, hand gesture, movement direction, camera movement, scene style, and lighting; multiple fine-grained templates are used to guide the model to output structured, detailed information. 3) In particular, for human action we decompose actions into body, head, face, hand, leg, etc., and design specialized prompts for these parts to ensure fine-grained action descriptions; we explicitly define object interaction to mean only physical-contact interactions, label any unconfirmable attributes as “none”, and employ both brief/caption-style prompts and detailed prompts to produce condensed and detailed outputs, balancing annotation efficiency and completeness. 4) Finally, we perform a secondary consistency check of the annotations using Qwen2.5-VL-72B \[[81](https://arxiv.org/html/2512.13495v1#bib.bib81)\], and remove examples where the video content contradicts the annotations (e.g., annotated “camera panning” but the shot is static, annotated “person walking” but the person shows no displacement), ensuring annotation accuracy.

### 4.2 Soul-Bench

Manual dataset creation and selection. To comprehensively evaluate the performance and generalization of audio-guided human animation methods, we construct the Soul-Bench benchmark from practical application requirements using an automated-generation plus manual-verification pipeline. The benchmark contains 226 samples spanning portraits, upper-body, full-body, animation, and animal subjects, covering diverse durations, styles, genders, ages, accessories, environments, and interacting objects. The construction proceeds as follows: 1) Prompt and asset generation: we use an LLM \[[84](https://arxiv.org/html/2512.13495v1#bib.bib84)\] to generate image prompts for HunyuanImage 3.0 \[[87](https://arxiv.org/html/2512.13495v1#bib.bib87)\], produce talking text which is converted to audio by a TTS system \[[88](https://arxiv.org/html/2512.13495v1#bib.bib88)\], and generate scene text to guide the visual synthesis process. 2) Automatic plausibility filtering: a second LLM \[[89](https://arxiv.org/html/2512.13495v1#bib.bib89)\] evaluates the realism and consistency of the synthesized assets. We iterate step 1 until the automated evaluator approves a target set size (500 entries in the automated stage). 3) Human verification: human annotators perform a further quality check on image fidelity, audio quality, and the reasonableness of the scene text, selecting only high-quality test samples. 4) Manual augmentation: we additionally curate and add real-world singing cases that are important in practice. After this pipeline we obtain 226 test samples (partially visualized in [Fig. 6](https://arxiv.org/html/2512.13495v1#S4.F6 "In 4.2 Soul-Bench ‣ 4 Curating Soul-1M Dataset ‣ Soul: Breathe Life into Digital Human for High-fidelity Long-term Multimodal Animation")-Bottom) and the scene distribution statistics are shown in  [Fig. 4](https://arxiv.org/html/2512.13495v1#S4.F4 "In 4.1 Soul-1M for Training ‣ 4 Curating Soul-1M Dataset ‣ Soul: Breathe Life into Digital Human for High-fidelity Long-term Multimodal Animation").

## 5 Experiments

### 5.1 Experimental Setup

Baselines. We compare against the latest open-source Sonic \[[36](https://arxiv.org/html/2512.13495v1#bib.bib36)\], Wan-S2V \[[2](https://arxiv.org/html/2512.13495v1#bib.bib2)\], InfiniteTalk \[[86](https://arxiv.org/html/2512.13495v1#bib.bib86)\], StableAvatar \[[49](https://arxiv.org/html/2512.13495v1#bib.bib49)\], and OmniAvatar \[[4](https://arxiv.org/html/2512.13495v1#bib.bib4)\], as well as the closed-source HeyGen \[[91](https://arxiv.org/html/2512.13495v1#bib.bib91)\] and Kling-Avatar \[[3](https://arxiv.org/html/2512.13495v1#bib.bib3)\].

Implementation detail. We train on Soul-1M, which contains 80W synchronized text-audio-video samples, with an additional 20W general videos without audio, covering portrait, half-body, and full-body shots. Soul is built on Wan2.2-5B \[[17](https://arxiv.org/html/2512.13495v1#bib.bib17)\]. A single inference by default generates a video clip of 109$`\times`$1088$`\times`$1920 resolution (all videos are scaled to an equivalent resolution of 1088$`\times`$1920 while maintaining the aspect ratio), and we employ the design in [Sec. 3.2](https://arxiv.org/html/2512.13495v1#S3.SS2 "3.2 Long-term Human Video Generation ‣ 3 Soul: Multimodal Humam Animation ‣ Soul: Breathe Life into Digital Human for High-fidelity Long-term Multimodal Animation") for long-term video generation. We fully fine-tune the model on 64 GPUs using AdamW \[[92](https://arxiv.org/html/2512.13495v1#bib.bib92)\] with a learning rate of $`2\times 10^{-5}`$. To improve training efficiency, we first train for 2 epochs at a resolution of 109$`\times`$720$`\times`$1280, and then fine-tune for 1 epoch at 1088$`\times`$1920 resolution.

Video assessment. Soul-Bench uses the following metrics to evaluate different methods: 1) Video-Text Consistence evaluated by Qwen3-VL-235B-A22B-Instruct \[[84](https://arxiv.org/html/2512.13495v1#bib.bib84), [81](https://arxiv.org/html/2512.13495v1#bib.bib81)\]; 2) LSE-D (Lip-Sync Error-Confidence) and LSE-D (Lip-Sync Error-Distance) to assess lip-audio synchronization using SyncNet-v2 \[[93](https://arxiv.org/html/2512.13495v1#bib.bib93)\]; 3) Identity Consistence by computing cosine similarity using ArcFace \[[90](https://arxiv.org/html/2512.13495v1#bib.bib90)\]; 4) Video Quality measured by the overall score of FineVQ \[[79](https://arxiv.org/html/2512.13495v1#bib.bib79)\]; 5) Audio-Video Alignment following the method in \[[85](https://arxiv.org/html/2512.13495v1#bib.bib85)\]. Additionally, we conduct a human study to evaluate overall video effectiveness: aesthetic visual quality, consistency with textual instructions, and lip-sync consistency.

## 6 Conclusion

This paper presents Soul, a framework for high-fidelity long-term digital human animation. It addresses challenges in datasets, long-term consistency, and efficient high-resolution generation. Leveraging the Soul-1M dataset and Soul-Bench, Soul outperforms open-source and commercial models. With 11.4$`\times`$ inference speedup enabling real-time deployment, it helps bridge the gap between academia and digital human applications.

Limitation and future work. Soul may produce artifacts when handling highly complex full-body motions, which remain a challenging problem in the field. We plan to expand the Soul-1M dataset to increase the representation of rare motion types and to include cross-lingual audio samples to improve model robustness. We will also investigate incorporating 3D geometric priors to enhance the naturalness and spatial consistency of full-body motions.
