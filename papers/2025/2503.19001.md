###### Abstract

Recent advances in talking face generation have significantly improved facial animation synthesis. However, existing approaches face fundamental limitations: 3DMM-based methods maintain temporal consistency but lack fine-grained regional control, while Stable Diffusion-based methods enable spatial manipulation but suffer from temporal inconsistencies. The integration of these approaches is hindered by incompatible control mechanisms and semantic entanglement of facial representations. This paper presents DisentTalk, introducing a data-driven semantic disentanglement framework that decomposes 3DMM expression parameters into meaningful subspaces for fine-grained facial control. Building upon this disentangled representation, we develop a hierarchical latent diffusion architecture that operates in 3DMM parameter space, integrating region-aware attention mechanisms to ensure both spatial precision and temporal coherence. To address the scarcity of high-quality Chinese training data, we introduce CHDTF, a Chinese high-definition talking face dataset. Extensive experiments show superior performance over existing methods across multiple metrics, including lip synchronization, expression quality, and temporal consistency. ## I Introduction

Recent advancements in talking face generation models \[[1](https://arxiv.org/html/2503.19001v1#bib.bib1), [2](https://arxiv.org/html/2503.19001v1#bib.bib2), [3](https://arxiv.org/html/2503.19001v1#bib.bib3), [4](https://arxiv.org/html/2503.19001v1#bib.bib4), [5](https://arxiv.org/html/2503.19001v1#bib.bib5), [6](https://arxiv.org/html/2503.19001v1#bib.bib6), [7](https://arxiv.org/html/2503.19001v1#bib.bib7), [8](https://arxiv.org/html/2503.19001v1#bib.bib8), [9](https://arxiv.org/html/2503.19001v1#bib.bib9), [10](https://arxiv.org/html/2503.19001v1#bib.bib10), [11](https://arxiv.org/html/2503.19001v1#bib.bib11), [12](https://arxiv.org/html/2503.19001v1#bib.bib12), [13](https://arxiv.org/html/2503.19001v1#bib.bib13), [14](https://arxiv.org/html/2503.19001v1#bib.bib14), [15](https://arxiv.org/html/2503.19001v1#bib.bib15)\] have significantly enhanced the quality of facial animation synthesis. However, achieving natural and temporally coherent talking face videos remains challenging, particularly in cross-lingual scenarios \[[5](https://arxiv.org/html/2503.19001v1#bib.bib5)\].

Current approaches primarily fall into two categories, each with fundamental limitations. ## II Related Work

Deterministic Methods. Early approaches can be categorized into end-to-end GAN-based methods (e.g., Wav2Lip \[[7](https://arxiv.org/html/2503.19001v1#bib.bib7)\], PCAVS \[[8](https://arxiv.org/html/2503.19001v1#bib.bib8)\], \[[18](https://arxiv.org/html/2503.19001v1#bib.bib18)\]) and intermediate representation-based methods using 3DMM parameters \[[4](https://arxiv.org/html/2503.19001v1#bib.bib4), [5](https://arxiv.org/html/2503.19001v1#bib.bib5)\] or landmarks \[[1](https://arxiv.org/html/2503.19001v1#bib.bib1), [6](https://arxiv.org/html/2503.19001v1#bib.bib6)\]. While these methods achieve reasonable lip synchronization and temporal consistency, they struggle to capture the probabilistic nature of speech-driven facial expressions, resulting in limited expressiveness.

Diffusion-based Methods. Recent diffusion-based methods have shown promising results in enhancing generation quality. Stable Diffusion-based approaches (EMO \[[12](https://arxiv.org/html/2503.19001v1#bib.bib12)\], DiffTalk \[[11](https://arxiv.org/html/2503.19001v1#bib.bib11)\]) excel at spatial detail modeling but suffer from temporal inconsistency and high computational costs. DreamTalk \[[15](https://arxiv.org/html/2503.19001v1#bib.bib15)\] combines 3DMM with diffusion models to improve temporal coherence, yet its holistic parameter modeling limits fine-grained facial control.

Cross-lingual Generation. Current datasets and methods primarily focus on English content \[[19](https://arxiv.org/html/2503.19001v1#bib.bib19), [20](https://arxiv.org/html/2503.19001v1#bib.bib20)\], with limited success in cross-lingual scenarios. This highlights the need for Chinese high-definition talking face datasets to advance cross-lingual talking face generation.

## III Method

### III-A 3DMM Expression Parameter Disentanglement

While 3DMM effectively encodes facial expressions through PCA of 3D scans \[[16](https://arxiv.org/html/2503.19001v1#bib.bib16), [17](https://arxiv.org/html/2503.19001v1#bib.bib17)\], its mathematically orthogonal dimensions often entangle semantic facial movements, limiting precise control in audio-driven animation. To address this, we propose a data-driven disentanglement framework that decomposes 3DMM parameters into three semantically meaningful subspaces: lip articulation, eye dynamics, and global expressions (Fig. [3](https://arxiv.org/html/2503.19001v1#S3.F3 "Figure 3 ‣ III-A 3DMM Expression Parameter Disentanglement ‣ III Method ‣ DisentTalk: Cross-lingual Talking Face Generation via Semantic Disentangled Diffusion Model⋆ Corresponding authors. This work was supported by NSFC under 62272456. We extend our special thanks to Ruoyu Chen for helping revise Figures 1, the Introduction, and the Abstract.")).

To identify region-specific dimensions, we transform source videos into states with closed lips and eyes using a facial editing model \[[21](https://arxiv.org/html/2503.19001v1#bib.bib21)\]. By analyzing the parameter differences between original and modified states, we select dimensions that are most sensitive to each regional movement:

```math
D_{r} = \arg\max\limits_{K_{r}}{\{\frac{|\Delta_{r}^{i}|}{\sigma_{i}}\}}_{i = 1}^{N},r \in {\{ lip,eye\}},
```

where $`\Delta_{r}^{i}`$ denotes the parameter difference in dimension $`i`$ for region $`r`$, $`\sigma_{i}`$ normalizes the variation scale, and $`K_{r}`$ represents the number of selected dimensions. The remaining dimensions form the global expression subspace:

```math
{D_{global} = {{\{ 1,\ldots,N\}} \smallsetminus {({D_{lip} \cup D_{eye}})}}}.
```

This decomposition preserves 3DMM’s mathematical properties while introducing semantic interpretability, enabling fine-grained control over facial regions. The disentangled representation serves as the foundation for our attention-based architecture, facilitating more natural facial animations.

### III-B Spatio-temporal Aware Diffusion Model

Building upon our disentangled 3DMM representation, we propose a spatio-temporal aware denoising architecture for audio-driven facial animation. Unlike previous methods that process spatial and temporal features in isolation \[[15](https://arxiv.org/html/2503.19001v1#bib.bib15), [14](https://arxiv.org/html/2503.19001v1#bib.bib14)\], our approach maintains joint awareness of both domains throughout the generation process. The model takes the 3DMM expression parameters $`\mathbf{E}`$, diffusion timestep $`t`$, and hubert-based \[[22](https://arxiv.org/html/2503.19001v1#bib.bib22)\] audio features $`\mathbf{A}`$ as input.

Latent Diffusion Model. We adopt a latent diffusion model \[[10](https://arxiv.org/html/2503.19001v1#bib.bib10)\] operating in the 3DMM motion space. Given a latent facial sequence $`\mathbf{Z}_{0}`$ from $`\mathbf{E}`$, the forward process gradually adds Gaussian noise over $`T`$ timesteps:

```math
{{q{({\mathbf{Z}_{t} \mid \mathbf{Z}_{0}})}} = {\mathcal{N}{({\sqrt{1 - \beta_{t}}\mathbf{Z}_{0}},{\beta_{t}\mathbf{I}})}}},
```

where $`\beta_{t}`$ controls the noise schedule. The denoising process leverages our spatio-temporal aware network to progressively recover the original motion $`\mathbf{Z}_{0}`$ from a Gaussian noise $`\mathbf{Z}_{N} \sim {\mathcal{N}{(0,\mathbf{I})}}`$.

Denoising Network Architecture. Our denoising network comprises three key components that jointly process spatial and temporal information: (1) hierarchical region-aware spatial attention for region-specific feature extraction, (2) multi-scale temporal convolution for temporal dynamics modeling, and (3) enhanced audio-expression cross-attention for audio-visual synchronization.

Hierarchical Region-aware Spatial Attention. Leveraging our semantic decomposition of 3DMM parameters, we first introduce a region-aware spatial attention mechanism that maintains distinct facial dynamics while preserving their temporal relationships. This mechanism extends beyond conventional spatial attention by incorporating temporal context into region-specific processing:

```math
{\mathbf{F}_{spatial} = {\mathbf{W}_{f}{\lbrack{\text{Attention}{(\mathbf{E}_{r})}}\rbrack}_{r \in \mathcal{R}}}},
```

where $`\mathbf{E}_{r}`$ represents region-specific parameters, $`\mathbf{W}_{f}`$ is the feature fusion matrix, $`\mathcal{R} = {\{{eye},{lip},{global}\}}`$ denotes the set of facial regions, and $`{\lbrack \cdot \rbrack}_{r \in \mathcal{R}}`$ represents concatenation over all regions. This formulation enables the model to capture spatially localized features.

Multi-scale Temporal Convolution. To complement the spatial attention while preserving fine-grained temporal dynamics, we develop a hierarchical temporal processing framework that maintains spatial awareness. Our design employs spatially-aware depth-wise separable convolutions with exponentially increasing temporal receptive fields:

```math
{\mathbf{F}_{temp} = {\mathcal{F}_{pw}{({\text{Concat}{\lbrack{\{{\mathcal{F}_{dw}^{i}{(\mathbf{F}_{spatial})}}\}}_{i = 0}^{2}\rbrack}})}}},
```

where $`\mathcal{F}_{dw}^{i}`$ denotes depth-wise convolution with dilation rate $`2^{i}`$, and $`\mathcal{F}_{pw}`$ represents point-wise convolution for feature aggregation. This multi-scale architecture preserves spatial relationships while modeling temporal dependencies at different scales.

Enhanced Audio-Expression Cross-attention. To achieve unified spatio-temporal synchronization with audio features, we propose a dual-path cross-attention mechanism that simultaneously processes local temporal correlations and global audio-visual context:

```math
{\mathbf{F}_{final} = {{\mathbf{W}_{o}{\lbrack{\mathcal{F}_{conv}{(\mathbf{F}_{temp})}};{\mathcal{F}_{attn}{(\mathbf{F}_{temp},\mathbf{A})}}\rbrack}} + b_{o}}},
```

where $`\mathcal{F}_{conv}`$ is a local convolution with window size $`k`$ maintaining temporal correlations, $`\mathcal{F}_{attn}`$ captures global audio-visual relationships through causal cross-attention, and $`\mathbf{W}_{o}`$ is the multi-level feature fusion matrix. The framework is guided by time-dependent FiLM \[[23](https://arxiv.org/html/2503.19001v1#bib.bib23)\] conditioning:

```math
{\mathbf{F}_{out} = {{\gamma{(t)}\mathbf{F}_{final}} + {\beta{(t)}}}},
```

where $`\gamma{(t)}`$ and $`\beta{(t)}`$ modulate features throughout the diffusion process, ensuring coherent spatio-temporal generation.

Training Strategy. Our training framework combines classifier-free guidance \[[24](https://arxiv.org/html/2503.19001v1#bib.bib24)\] with a novel 3DMM-based lip synchronization constraint. During training, audio features are randomly masked (p=0.1) for conditional and unconditional generation, with noise estimation loss:

```math
{\mathcal{L}_{\text{noise}} = {{\mathbb{E}}_{\mathbf{Z}_{0},t,\epsilon}\left\lbrack \left\| {\epsilon - {\epsilon_{\theta}{(\mathbf{Z}_{t},t,\mathbf{A})}}} \right\|^{2} \right\rbrack}},
```

and inference guided by:

```math
{\epsilon_{n}^{\ast} = {{s\epsilon_{\theta}{(\mathbf{Z}_{n},n,\mathbf{A})}} + {{({1 - s})}\epsilon_{\theta}{(\mathbf{Z}_{n},n,\varnothing)}}}}.
```

3DMM-based Lip Sync Loss. Unlike previous SyncNet-based \[[7](https://arxiv.org/html/2503.19001v1#bib.bib7)\] approaches using image-level features, we propose a parameter-level synchronization loss operating directly on disentangled lip parameters. Given HuBERT features $`\mathbf{A}`$ and lip motion parameters $`\mathbf{E}_{m}`$, we employ two parallel encoders (details in our appendix) to extract normalized embeddings for synchronization assessment:

```math
{\mathcal{L}_{sync} = {\text{BCELoss}{({\text{cos}{(\frac{f_{a}{(\mathbf{A})}}{{\|{f_{a}{(\mathbf{A})}}\|}_{2}},\frac{f_{m}{(\mathbf{E}_{m})}}{{\|{f_{m}{(\mathbf{E}_{m})}}\|}_{2}})}},y)}}},
```

where $`f_{a}`$ and $`f_{m}`$ are audio and mouth parameter encoders respectively, and $`y \in {\{ 0,1\}}`$ indicates synchronization. The final objective is $`\mathcal{L}_{\text{total}} = {\mathcal{L}_{\text{noise}} + {\lambda\mathcal{L}_{\text{sync}}}}`$. After getting the denoised 3DMM parameters, we use a 3DMM-based face render \[[25](https://arxiv.org/html/2503.19001v1#bib.bib25)\] to generate the final talking face video.

### IV-A Implementation Details and Metrics

Datasets. Existing audio-visual datasets \[[19](https://arxiv.org/html/2503.19001v1#bib.bib19), [20](https://arxiv.org/html/2503.19001v1#bib.bib20)\] predominantly focus on English content, limiting the generalization capability to other languages, particularly those with distinct phonetic characteristics. Mandarin Chinese, as one of the most widely used languages on the Internet, contains unique phonemes absent in English, such as retroflex consonants (e.g., ”zh, ch, sh”) and tonal variations (e.g., ”ma” with four different tones representing ”mother”, ”hemp”, ”horse”, and ”scold”). To address this limitation, we introduce CHDTF (Chinese High-Definition Talking Face Dataset), comprising 1,537 high-quality videos (39.1 hours) and most videos anchored at 1080p resolution. The dataset’s phonetic diversity and professional recording quality make it valuable for both Chinese-specific applications and cross-lingual generalization. For comprehensive evaluation, we utilize HDTF \[[19](https://arxiv.org/html/2503.19001v1#bib.bib19)\] (300+ videos, 15.8 hours) for English content and Voxceleb2 \[[20](https://arxiv.org/html/2503.19001v1#bib.bib20)\] for in-the-wild cross-lingual testing. All videos are preprocessed to 256×256 resolution and audio standardized to 16kHz mono channel format.

Implementation Details. We implement our framework in PyTorch and train it using the Adam optimizer with a learning rate of 1e-4 and batch size of 32. The region-specific dimension selection is determined by the ratio of the maximum temporal variation to the standard deviation, resulting in dimensions $`K_{lip} = 13`$ and $`K_{eye} = 8`$. The diffusion process uses T = 400 timesteps, and the local convolution window size $`k`$ is set to 5. The sync loss weight $`\lambda`$ is set to 0.01 to balance different optimization objectives. ¹¹1Detailed experimental settings and datasets are provided in the supplementary material.

Metrics. We adopt peak signal-to-noise ratio (PSNR)  \[[26](https://arxiv.org/html/2503.19001v1#bib.bib26)\] and cumulative probability of blur detection (CPBD) \[[27](https://arxiv.org/html/2503.19001v1#bib.bib27)\] to assess the image quality and clarity. To evaluate lip synchronization, we use the lip-sync error confidence score (LSE-C) from \[[28](https://arxiv.org/html/2503.19001v1#bib.bib28)\], and the landmark distance of the mouth (LMD) \[[29](https://arxiv.org/html/2503.19001v1#bib.bib29)\]. To assess overall facial naturalness, we measure the blinking frequency. Following human physiological standards where normal blinking rates range from 0.28 to 0.45 blinks/s \[[30](https://arxiv.org/html/2503.19001v1#bib.bib30)\], we use blink rate (Blink) as a naturalness metric, which provides insight into the realism of generated facial movements.

### IV-B Experimental Results

Baselines. We compare our method with ten state-of-the-art approaches, categorized into two groups: (1) Deterministic methods: MakeItTalk \[[1](https://arxiv.org/html/2503.19001v1#bib.bib1)\], Audio2Head \[[3](https://arxiv.org/html/2503.19001v1#bib.bib3)\], AVCT \[[2](https://arxiv.org/html/2503.19001v1#bib.bib2)\], IPLAP \[[6](https://arxiv.org/html/2503.19001v1#bib.bib6)\], SadTalker \[[4](https://arxiv.org/html/2503.19001v1#bib.bib4)\], Styletalk \[[5](https://arxiv.org/html/2503.19001v1#bib.bib5)\], and PDFGC \[[9](https://arxiv.org/html/2503.19001v1#bib.bib9)\]. (2) Diffusion-based methods: Dreamtalk \[[15](https://arxiv.org/html/2503.19001v1#bib.bib15)\], Aniportrait \[[13](https://arxiv.org/html/2503.19001v1#bib.bib13)\], and HALLO \[[14](https://arxiv.org/html/2503.19001v1#bib.bib14)\]. The reported results were obtained using the publicly available code of these methods. (note: the input speaker of ours-video, IPLAP, and PDFGC are videos, while the other methods are a single image.)

Quantitative Results. We conduct comprehensive evaluations on HDTF \[[19](https://arxiv.org/html/2503.19001v1#bib.bib19)\] (English), CHDTF (Chinese), and Voxceleb2 \[[20](https://arxiv.org/html/2503.19001v1#bib.bib20)\] (multi-lingual) datasets to assess the effectiveness of our method in cross-lingual talking face generation. Our approach demonstrates superior performance across multiple aspects: achieving state-of-the-art LSE-C scores (6.299 on Voxceleb2 vs. baseline average 4.5) in cross-lingual scenarios, maintaining physiologically plausible blink rates (0.165 on Voxceleb2) through semantic disentanglement, and exhibiting consistent lip synchronization across languages (LSE-C: 7.289/6.419/6.299 on HDTF/CHDTF/Voxceleb2). Notably, while Aniportrait achieves high CPBD (0.895) through its Stable Diffusion backbone, our method maintains competitive image clarity (CPBD: 0.672) while significantly outperforming in temporal coherence and lip synchronization, demonstrating a better balance between spatial fidelity and motion dynamics. These comprehensive results validate our approach’s effectiveness in addressing the core challenges of cross-lingual talking face generation through semantic disentanglement and spatio-temporal aware modeling.

## V Conclusion

In this paper, we presented a novel framework for cross-lingual talking face generation that effectively addresses two fundamental challenges: semantic entanglement in facial representations and isolated processing of spatial-temporal features. Through our semantic disentanglement approach and hierarchical diffusion architecture, we achieve precise control over distinct facial regions while maintaining temporal coherence. The CHDTF dataset enriches the training data and enables cross-lingual evaluation. Extensive experiments show superior performance over existing methods in lip synchronization, expression naturalness, and temporal stability.
