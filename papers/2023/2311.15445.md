As a subcategory of the general image and video restoration , face restoration is an active research area in computer vision . Image and video restoration is usually *ill-posed* due to the information loss induced by degradation (_e.g.,_ resolution loss, blur, encoding artifacts, and noise), with multiple plausible high-quality (HQ) objects leading to the same low-quality (LQ) observation. Face restoration has recently been greatly improved by using generative priors  and pre-trained face dictionary priors  . While SOTA methods—such as Codeformer , VQFR , and RestoreFormer —can restore high-quality results with fine details, they usually hallucinate HQ faces that diverge from the original subjects in the presence of severe degradation , leading to large distortion, as can be seen in Fig. <a href="#Fig:fig0" data-reference-type="ref" data-reference="Fig:fig0">1</a> (_Top_).

Diffusion probabilistic models (DPMs)  have attracted significant attention as an alternative to traditional generative models due to their excellent performance in image and video generation . DPMs have been applied to a range of imaging problems, showing impressive results for face restoration. These methods generally fall into two categories: model-based unsupervised methods  and conditional training methods . Despite recent activity in the area, there are very few DPM-based frameworks for video restoration, especially in the context of face video restoration (FVR). The key challenges are the significant computational cost of training on video data and the lack of large-scale, publicly available HQ face video datasets. Given the stochasticity of the generative process in DPMs, another challenge is the effective use of nearby, similar but misaligned frames for reconstructing temporally aligned HQ reference frames . For instance, as shown in Fig. <a href="#Fig:fig0" data-reference-type="ref" data-reference="Fig:fig0">1</a> (_Middle_), one of the latest conditional image DPM, DDNM , fails to produce a consistent facial restoration across frames.

**Proposed Work:** We present _Di**f**fusion Probabi**l**istic F**a**ce V**i**deo **R**estoration (FLAIR)_, a conditional generative model for FVR, that can generate multiple distinct, high-quality, enhanced face videos from a given degraded sequential data. We design FLAIR as a “repeated-refinement” conditional DPM. Instead of directly training on high-resolution videos, we first pre-train our conditional DPMs on images only, which allows us to use large-scale HQ image datasets very efficiently. The image DPMs are trained to take the degraded estimation as an auxiliary input for conditional restoration similar to . Given a pre-trained image DPM backbone based on UNet , we then modify it into a video restoration model by introducing a temporal dimension into the feature space of the neural network and only train these temporal layers on video sequences. Specifically, we propose a flow-guided video enhancement layer with a multi-scale recurrent module at the high-resolution scales of the UNet backbone, along with several temporal self-attention blocks that process the low resolution features in a sliding-window fashion. FLAIR is thus designed to capture long-range temporal dependencies, using information from multiple neighbouring frames for the restoration of each frame during inference.

To better balance the perceptual quality and data-fidelity , we propose a two-stage refinement process at every reverse diffusion step. The first stage involves an interpretable data-consistency (DC) module to analytically ensure that the generated coarse, clean intermediate results precisely match their LQ counterparts, even amid a range of mixed real-world degradations (_e.g.,_ a mix of resolution loss, blur, and JPEG). In the second stage, the DC outputs are further processed by a enhancement module for high-quality details specified for facial regions (see Fig. <a href="#Fig:fig1" data-reference-type="ref" data-reference="Fig:fig1">2</a>). This design ensures that the enhancement module is compatible with various choices of restoration methods, enabling FLAIR to produce both perceptually realistic and data-consistent results.

Our main contributions can be summarized as follows: (1) We propose FLAIR as the first conditional diffusion framework for the recovery of long-term consistent, high-quality face videos from their LQ observations. Our key insight is to convert pre-trained image DPMs into video restoration models by inserting temporal layers that learn to align images in a temporally consistent manner (Fig. <a href="#Fig:fig2" data-reference-type="ref" data-reference="Fig:fig2">3</a>). (2) Together with a data-consistency module and an enhancement module, we employ FLAIR in a two-stage conditional refinement process at each iteration of the reverse diffusion to further improve the perception and fidelity simultaneously. (3) We show through extensive experiments that FLAIR outperforms SOTA methods for composite noisy degradation on two high-quality face video datasets both quantitatively and qualitatively, showing great potential for practical applications.

# Related Work

**Face Restoration.** Traditional approaches for face restoration are based on the incorporation of prior knowledge and degradation models . The quality of restored faces has been progressively improving after adoption of convolutional neural networks (CNNs) . Recent work has investigated various deep priors for face image restoration, including geometric and reference priors . The restoration quality has been further improved by adapting pre-trained GANs, such as StyleGAN , as generative priors . This line of works treats face restoration as a conditional image generation problem by projecting the LQ faces into a compact, low-dimension space of the pre-trained generator. Another line of works, *e.g.,* VQFR , CodeFormer , ResotreFormer  and its variant , leverages pre-trained Vector-Quantization (VQ) codebooks  as dictionaries learned on facial regions, achieving SOTA results in blind face restoration.

**Diffusion Models.** Denoising diffusion models  and score-based models  are two related classes of generative models that were shown to achieve SOTA performance for unconditional image and video generation. Apart from unconditional image generation, diffusion models have been extensively investigated in various imaging restoration tasks. One line of works has focused on designing conditional training methods in a supervised fashion . Another line of work has focused on keeping the training of an unconditional image DPM intact, and only modify the inference procedure to enable sampling from a conditional distribution . However, only few DPMs methods  have been tried for image video enhancement and restoration. Notably, none of these methods have directly addressed video restoration tasks with a focus on FVR.

# Preliminaries

**Diffusion Probabilistic Models.** The forward process of DPMs  is a Markov Chain that gradually adds noise $`{\bm{\epsilon}}\sim{\mathcal{N}}(\bm{0},{\mathbf{I}})`$ to data $`{\bm{x}}_0\sim q({\bm{x}}_0), {\bm{x}}_0\in\mathbb{R}^d`$ according to the variance schedule $`\beta_t \in (0,1)`$ for all $`t = 1,\cdots,T`$. The Markov chain sequentially samples the noisy latent variables $`{\bm{x}}_{1:T}`$ with the same dimensionality as $`{\bm{x}}_0`$. Using the notation $`\alpha_t \,\coloneqq\,1- \beta_t`$ and $`\Bar{\alpha}_t \,\coloneqq\,\Pi_{s=1}^t \alpha_s`$, sampling of $`{\bm{x}}_t`$ given $`{\bm{x}}_0`$ can be expressed in a closed form

```math
\begin{equation}
    \label{eq:sampleNoisfwd}
    q({\bm{x}}_t|{\bm{x}}_{0}) := {\mathcal{N}}({\bm{x}}_t; \sqrt{\Bar{\alpha}_t} {\bm{x}}_{0}, ( 1- \Bar{\alpha}_t) {\mathbf{I}}).
\end{equation}
```

The unconditional generative reverse process is a Gaussian transition that samples from $`{\bm{x}}_T\sim{\mathcal{N}}(\bm{0},{\mathbf{I}})`$ to $`{\bm{x}}_0`$ as

```math
\begin{equation}
    \label{eq:sampleNoisbpd}
    q({\bm{x}}_{t-1}|{\bm{x}}_t, {\bm{x}}_{0}) := {\mathcal{N}}({\bm{x}}_{t-1}; \mu_t({\bm{x}}_t, {\bm{x}}_0), \sigma_{t}^2{\mathbf{I}}),
\end{equation}
```

where $`\mu_t({\bm{x}}_t, {\bm{x}}_0)`$ and $`\sigma_{t}`$ depend on $`{\bm{x}}_t, {\bm{x}}_0`$, and $`\beta_t`$. DPMs train $`\epsilon_{\bm{\theta}}`$ to learn the Gaussian transition $`p_{\bm{\theta}}({\bm{x}}_{t-1}|{\bm{x}}_t)`$ as an approximation of reverse diffusion $`q({\bm{x}}_{t-1}|{\bm{x}}_t, {\bm{x}}_{0})`$. By training the residual denoiser network $`\epsilon_{{\bm{\theta}}}({\bm{x}}_t,t)`$ to predict the total noise $`\bm\epsilon_t`$, one can estimate $`{{\bm{x}}_{0t}}`$ through

```math
\begin{equation}
    \label{eq:x0t}
     {\bm{x}}_{0t}=({\bm{x}}_t-\sqrt{1-\bar\alpha_t}\epsilon_{{\bm{\theta}}}({\bm{x}}_t,t))/\sqrt{\bar\alpha_t},
\end{equation}
```

where $`{{\bm{x}}_{0t}}`$ denotes the first prediction of $`{\bm{x}}_0`$ given the noisy observation $`{\bm{x}}_t`$. One can use the DDIM  strategy to sample from the generative process more efficiently

```math
\begin{equation}
    \label{eq:reverse1}
    {\bm{x}}_{t-1} = \sqrt{\bar\alpha_{t-1}}{\bm{x}}_{0t} + \sqrt{1-\bar\alpha_{t-1}}(\sqrt{1-\eta_t}{\bm{\epsilon}}_t + \sqrt{\eta_t}{\bm{\epsilon}}),
\end{equation}
```

where the magnitude of $`\eta_{t}=\eta\sigma_t^2/(1-\bar\alpha_{t-1})`$ controlled by $`\eta\in\mathbb{R}^+_{0}`$ determines how stochastic the forward process is (_e.g.,_ when $`\eta=0`$,  <a href="#eq:reverse1" data-reference-type="eqref" data-reference="eq:reverse1">[eq:reverse1]</a> becomes deterministic).

**Inverse Problems.** The FVR can be formulated as an inverse problem involving the recovery of a sequence $`\{{\bm{X}}^{n}\}_{n=1}^N \in \mathbb{R}^{H\times W\times C}`$ of video frames from a series of LQ measurements, where $`N, H, W`$, and $`C`$ are the video length, height, width, and channel, respectively. For $`{\bm{x}}=[{\bm{x}}^{1}, \dots ,{\bm{x}}^{N}] \in \mathbb{R}^{Nd}`$ defined in a vector form, we have $`{\bm{x}}^{n}=\texttt{vec}({\bm{X}}^{n})^{\mathsf{T}}\in \mathbb{R}^d`$. The measurements can be represented as $`{\bm{y}}= {\mathcal{A}}({\bm{x}}) + {\bm{e}}`$, where $`{\mathcal{A}}=[{\mathcal{A}}_1, \dots ,{\mathcal{A}}_N]:\mathbb{R}^{Nm}\rightarrow\mathbb{R}^{Nd}~(m\ll d)`$ is the measurement operator modeling the degradation process, and $`{\bm{e}}=[{\bm{e}}^{1}, \dots,{\bm{e}}^{N}]\in\mathbb{R}^{Nm}`$ denotes the measurement noise. In this paper, we consider the scenario in which video quality suffers from spatial and temporal degradation of images due to factors such as out-of-focus, motion, limited sensor array intensity, and JPEG encoding .

# Proposed Approach: FLAIR

In this section, we describe the training and testing details of FLAIR tailored for FVR. Fig. <a href="#Fig:fig1" data-reference-type="ref" data-reference="Fig:fig1">2</a> illustrates the overview of the proposed method. FLAIR is defined as a generative process over $`T`$ steps conditioned on degraded video sequence $`{\bm{y}}`$,

```math
\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
    \label{eq:cond-reverse1}
    &\quad\quad p_{{\bm{\theta}}}({\bm{x}}_{0:T}|{\bm{y}}) = p({\bm{x}}_T)\prod_{t=1}^{T}p_{{\bm{\theta}}}({\bm{x}}_{t-1}|{\bm{x}}_t,{\bm{y}}),
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}
```

where $`{\bm{x}}_T`$ is sampled from the normal distribution $`p({\bm{x}}_T)\sim{\mathcal{N}}(\bm{0},{\mathbf{I}})`$, and $`{\bm{x}}_0`$ is the final diffusion output. Conditional generative process $`p_{\bm{\theta}}({\bm{x}}_{t-1}|{\bm{x}}_t, {\bm{y}})`$ is learned to approximate the intractable conditional reverse process $`q({\bm{x}}_{t-1}|{\bm{x}}_t, {\bm{x}}_{0}, {\bm{y}})`$ for the inference, similar to unconditional DPMs.

## Diffusion Video Restoration Network

We leverage pre-trained DPMs for images to efficiently train the video diffusion model . Our proposed method extends a DPM designed for image restoration, denoted as $`\epsilon_{\bm{\theta}}`$, into a video diffusion restoration network represented as $`\epsilon_{{\bm{\theta}},{\bm{\phi}}}`$. We introduce additional *temporal* neural network layers parameterized by $`{\bm{\phi}}`$ to $`\epsilon_{\bm{\theta}}`$ and fine-tune them to align individual frames for temporal consistency. We adopt UNet architecture in  for network $`\epsilon_{\bm{\theta}}`$. The training of the conditional model requires concatenation of the input image $`{\bm{x}}_t\in\mathbb{R}^d`$ and condition $`{\bm{c}}\in\mathbb{R}^d`$ along the channel dimension. The condition $`{\bm{c}}`$ represents the up-scaled LQ measurements $`{\bm{y}}\in\mathbb{R}^m`$ to the same dimension as $`{\bm{x}}_{0:T}`$ (see supplements for more details). The objective function for training the $`\epsilon_{\bm{\theta}}`$ is

```math
\begin{equation}
    \label{eq:condlossTheta}
    {\mathcal{L}}_{{\bm{\theta}}} = \mathbb{E}_{{\bm{x}}_0,{\bm{c}},{\bm{\epsilon}}, t\sim[1,T]}\left[\|{\bm{\epsilon}}- \epsilon_{{\bm{\theta}}}({\bm{x}}_t, {\bm{c}}, t)\|^2\right].
\end{equation}
```

**Temporal Layers Implementation.** Input feature maps in the pixel space are processed using the layers of image DPM denoted as *spatial* layers $`\{{\mathcal{H}}_{{\bm{\theta}}}^i\}_{i=1}^L`$, while each interleaved _temporal_ layer is denoted as $`{\mathcal{H}}_{\bm\phi}^i`$. We use three distinct types of temporal layers depicted in Fig. <a href="#Fig:fig2" data-reference-type="ref" data-reference="Fig:fig2">3</a>: recurrent feature enhancement (RFE), 3D convolutional residual blocks, and temporal attention. In practice, the spatial layers $`{\mathcal{H}}_{{\bm{\theta}}}^i`$ process the video as a collection of individual images within a batch by rearranging the temporal dimension into the batch axis, i.e., $`\mathbb{R}^{B\times C\times N \times H \times W} \rightarrow \mathbb{R}^{(BN)\times C \times H \times W}`$, where $`B`$ is the batch size. Subsequently, we reshape it back to the original video dimensions for each temporal layer $`{\mathcal{H}}_{\bm\phi}^i`$.

Directly integrating temporal attention into high-resolution features within image DPMs would notably increase memory complexity. Hence, we propose a method to capture sequential dependencies and synchronize video frame features at high resolutions (e.g., \[512, 256\]) by using recurrent feature refinement. The RFE module is comprised of a 3D convolutional residual block for extracting temporal features $`\tilde{\bm{f}}_i`$ from the spatial output $`{\bm{f}}_i`$ of $`{\mathcal{H}}_{{\bm{\theta}}}^i`$ and a flow-guided deformable feature alignment (DFA) module motivated by . The DFA is designed for bidirectional propagation, aiming to enhance the robustness of the recurrent network against error accumulation and alteration in appearance. Additional details regarding the modified DFA can be found in the supplements.

In addition, we integrate temporal attention following each $`{\mathcal{H}}_{{\bm{\theta}}}^i`$ to concurrently process $`N`$ frames locally in parallel within low-resolution blocks (e.g., \[32, 16, 8\]). To enhance the expressiveness of modeling sequential representation, we include sinusoidal positional embeddings  into the attention blocks. Our video temporal backbone is then trained with the same noise schedule as in <a href="#eq:sampleNoisfwd" data-reference-type="eqref" data-reference="eq:sampleNoisfwd">[eq:sampleNoisfwd]</a>. We optimize the temporal layers’ weights with the objective function

```math
\begin{equation}
    \label{eq:condlossPhi}
    {\mathcal{L}}_{{\bm{\phi}}} = \mathbb{E}_{{\bm{x}}_0,{\bm{c}},{\bm{\epsilon}}, t\sim[1,T]}\left[\|{\bm{\epsilon}}- \epsilon_{{\bm{\theta}}, {\bm{\phi}}}({\bm{x}}_t, {\bm{c}}, t)\|^2\right],
\end{equation}
```

while the spatial layers are frozen.

## Analytical Data Consistency Module

We initially consider a linear forward-model $`{\bm{y}}^{n} = ({\bm{h}}_n*{\bm{x}}^{n})\downarrow_{s}`$ without noise added to individual frames $`\{{\bm{y}}^{n}\}_{n=1}^N\in\mathbb{R}^m`$. In this expression, $`{\bm{h}}_n * {\bm{x}}^{n}`$ denotes two-dimensional convolution of clean image $`{\bm{x}}^{n}`$ and the blur kernel associated with the point-spread function (PSF) of the camera at frame $`n`$ and $`\downarrow_s`$ represents a $`s`$-fold down-sampler. For convenience, we denote the forward-model as $`{\bm{y}}={\mathcal{A}}{\bm{x}}`$. We enforce consistency of reconstructed $`\tilde{\bm{x}}`$ (e.g., $`{\bm{x}}_{0t}`$ in <a href="#eq:x0t" data-reference-type="eqref" data-reference="eq:x0t">[eq:x0t]</a>) by using a projection onto the subspace spanned by $`{\mathcal{A}}{\bm{x}}`$, where $`\text{rank}({\mathcal{A}})=Nm\leq Nd`$. We recover the consistent reconstruction by solving the following minimization problem

```math
\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
    \label{eq:FVRFWD1}
    &\tilde{\bm{x}}_{0t} = \mathop{\mathrm{arg\,min}}_{\tilde{\bm{x}}}{\|\tilde{\bm{x}}- {\bm{x}}_{0t}\|_2^2}\quad\text{s.t.}\quad{\mathcal{A}}\tilde{\bm{x}}={\bm{y}},
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}
```

corresponding to least-norm problem with equality constraints. This problem can be solved analytically  as

```math
\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
    \label{eq:rangenull}
    &\tilde{\bm{x}}_{0t} = {\bm{x}}_{0t} - {\mathcal{A}}^+({\mathcal{A}}{\bm{x}}_{0t} - {\bm{y}}),
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}
```

where $`{\mathcal{A}}^+={\mathcal{A}}^{T}({\mathcal{A}}{\mathcal{A}}^{T})^{-1}\in\mathbb{R}^{Nd\times Nm}`$ is the Moore-Penrose pseudo-inverse of $`{\mathcal{A}}`$ and satisfies $`{\mathcal{A}}{\mathcal{A}}^+={\mathbf{I}}_{Nm}`$. By substituting the estimated $`{\bm{x}}_{0t}`$ with $`\tilde{\bm{x}}_{0t}`$ in <a href="#eq:reverse1" data-reference-type="eqref" data-reference="eq:reverse1">[eq:reverse1]</a>, we enforce the low-frequency content of $`\tilde{\bm{x}}_{0t}`$ to align with that of the ground-truth video sequence $`{\bm{x}}`$ (_i.e.,_ $`{\mathcal{A}}\tilde{\bm{x}}_{0t}={\mathcal{A}}{\bm{x}}={\bm{y}}`$), while allowing the reverse diffusion process to recover the high-frequency components. We reformulate  <a href="#eq:rangenull" data-reference-type="eqref" data-reference="eq:rangenull">[eq:rangenull]</a> by calculating $`{\mathcal{A}}^+`$ according to  for each individual frame

```math
\begin{equation*}
\begin{aligned}
    \label{eq:srclosed}
    &\tilde{\bm{x}}_{0t}^{n} = {\bm{x}}_{0t}^{n} - \tilde{\bm{h}}_n * \left({\bm{k}}_n*(({\bm{h}}_n*{\bm{x}}_{0t}^{n})\downarrow_{s}-{\bm{y}}^{n})\right)\uparrow^s,\\
\end{aligned}
\end{equation*}
```

where $`\tilde{\bm{h}}_n`$ is the mirrored version of the blur kernel $`{\bm{h}}_n`$, and $`\uparrow^s`$ denotes spatial upsampling by zero-filling of new entries. $`{\bm{k}}_n`$ is used to replace the multiplication by $`({\mathcal{A}}{\mathcal{A}}^{T})^{-1}`$ and corresponds to the inverse of filter $`({\bm{h}}_n*\tilde{\bm{h}}_n)\downarrow_s`$ in Fourier domain.

**Noisy FVR Degradation.** In the presence of noise, the forward model is $`{\bm{y}}={\mathcal{A}}{\bm{x}}+{\bm{e}}`$, where $`{\bm{e}}=\{{\bm{e}}^{n}\}_{n=1}^N`$ represents additive white Gaussian noise (AWGN) with $`{\bm{e}}^{n}\sim {\mathcal{N}}(\bm{0},\sigma^2_{\bm{e}}{\mathbf{I}}_m)`$. Directly applying  <a href="#eq:rangenull" data-reference-type="eqref" data-reference="eq:rangenull">[eq:rangenull]</a> to noisy measurements $`{\bm{y}}`$ will result in an additional noise term $`{\mathcal{A}}^+{\bm{e}}`$ in $`\tilde{\bm{x}}_{0t}`$, consequently affecting the reverse diffusion $`q({\bm{x}}_{t-1}|{\bm{x}}_t, \tilde{\bm{x}}_{0}, {\bm{y}})`$. We can approximate $`{\mathcal{A}}^+_n{\bm{e}}^{n}`$ as a AWGN $`{\mathcal{N}}(\bm{0}, \sigma^2_{\bm{e}}{\mathbf{I}}_m)`$, given $`{\mathcal{A}}^+`$ in FVR closely resembles a copy operation . Thus <a href="#eq:rangenull" data-reference-type="eqref" data-reference="eq:rangenull">[eq:rangenull]</a> and <a href="#eq:reverse1" data-reference-type="eqref" data-reference="eq:reverse1">[eq:reverse1]</a> can be modified into

```math
\begin{align}
    \label{eq:rangenullscale}
    &\tilde{\bm{x}}_{0t} = {\bm{x}}_{0t} - \gamma_t{\mathcal{A}}^+({\mathcal{A}}{\bm{x}}_{0t} - {\mathcal{A}}{\bm{x}}) + \gamma_t{\mathcal{A}}^+{\bm{e}},\\\nonumber
    &{\bm{x}}_{t-1}=\sqrt{\bar\alpha_{t-1}}\tilde{\bm{x}}_{0t} + \sqrt{1-\bar\alpha_t}(\sqrt{1-\rho_t}\tilde{\bm{\epsilon}}_t + \sqrt{\rho_t}{\bm{\epsilon}}),
\end{align}
```

where $`\gamma_t\geq0`$ and $`\rho_t>0`$ are user-defined hyperparameters such that $`\sigma_t=\sqrt{\bar\alpha_{t-1}\gamma_t^2\sigma_{\bm{e}}^2 + \rho_t}`$, and $`\tilde{\bm{\epsilon}}_t=\frac{1}{\sqrt{1-\bar\alpha_t}}({\bm{x}}_t - \sqrt{\bar\alpha_t}\tilde{\bm{x}}_{0t})\in\mathbb{R}^{Nd}`$ is the recalculated noise estimate. By appropriately setting $`\gamma_t`$ and $`\rho_t`$, we make the total noise variance in $`{\bm{x}}_{t-1}`$ conform to the forward diffusion $`q ({\bm{x}}_{t-1}|{\bm{x}}_0)`$ in <a href="#eq:sampleNoisfwd" data-reference-type="eqref" data-reference="eq:sampleNoisfwd">[eq:sampleNoisfwd]</a>. This allows for an effective estimation of noise by $`\epsilon_{{\bm{\theta}},{\bm{\phi}}}({\bm{x}}_{t-1},{\bm{c}},t)`$ at next step.

**Composite FVR Degradation.** FLAIR is also applicable to more complicated FVR degradation

```math
\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
    \label{eq:complexFVR}
    &{\bm{y}}^{n} = {\mathcal{E}}_n\left(({\bm{h}}_n*{\bm{x}}^{n})\downarrow_{s} + {\bm{e}}^n\right),
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}
```

where $`{\mathcal{E}}=\{{\mathcal{E}}_n\}_{n=1}^{N}`$ denotes the JPEG encoding with quality factors $`Q\geq0`$. While JPEG is non-linear, we can construct JPEG decoding operator $`{\mathcal{D}}`$, such that $`{\mathcal{E}}({\mathcal{D}}({\mathcal{E}}({\bm{x}})))={\mathcal{E}}({\bm{x}}), \forall{\bm{x}}\in\mathbb{R}^{Nn}`$, similar to , which is analogue to the matrix pseudo-inverse $`{\mathcal{A}}{\mathcal{A}}^{+}{\mathcal{A}}{\bm{x}}={\mathcal{A}}{\bm{x}}`$. For composite forward operator $`{\mathcal{A}}={\mathcal{A}}_1\circ...\circ{\mathcal{A}}_k`$, we may approximate $`{\mathcal{A}}^{+}`$ with $`{\mathcal{A}}^{+}={\mathcal{A}}_k^{+}\circ...\circ{\mathcal{A}}_1^{+}`$. Hence, $`\tilde{\bm{x}}_{0t}`$ in <a href="#eq:rangenullscale" data-reference-type="eqref" data-reference="eq:rangenullscale">[eq:rangenullscale]</a> under composite degradation in <a href="#eq:complexFVR" data-reference-type="eqref" data-reference="eq:complexFVR">[eq:complexFVR]</a> can be efficiently solved using

```math
\begin{align}
    \label{eq:compositrangenullscale}
    &\tilde{\bm{x}}_{0t} = {\bm{x}}_{0t} - \gamma_t{\mathcal{A}}^+{\mathcal{D}}({\mathcal{E}}({\mathcal{A}}{\bm{x}}_{0t}) - {\bm{y}}).
\end{align}
```

The full algorithm of FLAIR is detailed in the supplements.

## Efficient Spatial Enhancement Module

Finally, we introduce a coarse-to-fine image enhancement module designed for refinement of estimated $`\tilde{\bm{x}}_{0t}`$, as

```math
\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
    \label{eq:enhance}
    &\tilde{\bm{x}}_{0t}= (\bm{1}-w_t \bm m_t)\odot\tilde{\bm{x}}_{0t} + w_t \bm m_t\odot\mathcal{G}(\tilde{\bm{x}}_{0t}),
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}
```

where $`w_t`$ balances the importance of the facial enhancement region $`\bm m\odot\mathcal{G}(\tilde{\bm{x}}_{0t})\in\mathbb{R}^{Nd}`$ and originally estimated $`\bm{m}_t\odot\tilde{\bm{x}}_{0t}\in\mathbb{R}^{Nd}`$ at each step, and $`(\bm{1}-\bm{m}_t)\odot\tilde{\bm{x}}_{0t}`$ denotes the background scenes. Note that we *do not* impose any specific constraints on the method or architecture of $`\mathcal{G}`$, allowing the enhancement module to be trained independently. For our enhancement module, we consider two well-established backbones: Restorformer++  and Codeformer . This shows the compatibility of FLAIR with a diverse range of existing methods. Both backbones make use of pre-trained high-quality VQ codebooks  specifically designed for face images. We refer to these methods as _FLAIR + RestorFormer++_ and _FLAIR + CodeFormer_, respectively.

# Experiments

## Experimental Setup

**Datasets.** We use FFHQ  for training image DPMs and 7200 clips from CelebV-Text  for fine-tuning video DPMs. We choose 125 short clips and 6 long clips from the unused identities of the CelebV-Text for testing. We also consider 20 clips from CelebV-HQ  and 100 sequences from Obama datasets  for testing. We additionally crawl a real life video clip with 300 frames from the Internet for testing. See supplements for more details.

**Evaluation Metrics.** Our evaluation is based on both perception and distortion of the restored videos. For perception, we choose three different frame-wise perceptual metrics: Frechet Inception Distance (FID) , LPIPS , and Kernel Inception Distance (KID)  as well as Frechet Video Distance (FVD) . We adopt two pixel-wise metrics: PSNR and SSIM  to evaluate data fidelity of our method.

**Training and Inference Details.** We consider three types of degradation models: video super-resolution (SR), deblurring and JPEG restoration. For video SR, we pre-train a conditional image DPM backbone (spatial layers) using downsampling factors $`s=8`$ with bicubic degradation and then fine tune the video DPMs with loss function in <a href="#eq:condlossPhi" data-reference-type="eqref" data-reference="eq:condlossPhi">[eq:condlossPhi]</a> using $`s\in\{4, 8, 16\}`$ separately. Likewise, for video deblurring, we pre-train a conditional image DPM for our video DPM using scale factors $`s=4`$ and AWGN $`\sigma_{\bm{e}}\in[0,25]`$ with anisotropic Gaussian kernels as in  and motion kernels as in . We fix the kernel size to $`25\times25`$. For video JPEG restoration, we use the same settings as for deblurring with additional JPEG quality factor $`Q\in[60, 100]`$.

We use pre-trained SPyNet  as our flow estimation network. At inference, we use $`K\in[1,T)`$ evenly spaced real numbers for the sampling step index, and then round each resulting number to the nearest integer following . For the enhancement module, we employ the original pre-trained models of RestoreFormer  and CodeFormer .

# Conclusion

In this paper, we propose the FLAIR, a novel framework based on diffusion probabilistic models for *face video restoration*. The key idea of FLAIR is to build upon pre-trained image diffusion models specialized in face image restoration and to transform them into video diffusion restoration models by incorporating and fine-tuning temporal alignment layers. We further propose a two-stage refinement process at every reverse sampling step. In the first stage, FLAIR analytically imposes reconstruction fidelity by using a data-consistency module that can handle composed degradation in practice. The subsequent stage involves an enhancement module dedicated to regional improvement. Extensive comparisons show that our FLAIR framework provides temporally aligned, high-quality results in face video restoration.

## Training of conditional Image DPMs

<figcaption>Visual illustration of the impact of equation <a href="#eq:mcondition" data-reference-type="eqref" data-reference="eq:mcondition">[eq:mcondition]</a> on training image DPMs. The zoomed-in regions are shown below the main results. Notably, the image restoration quality is improved by applying data augmentation to the conditional inputs. </figcaption>

In order to improve the generation flexibility and empirical performance of FLAIR, we jointly train a single image diffusion model on conditional and unconditional objectives by randomly dropping $`{\bm{c}}`$ during training (, $`p_{\text{uncond}}=0.2`$), similar to the *classifier free guidance* . Hence, the sampling is performed using the adjusted noise prediction:

```math
\begin{equation}
    \label{eq:reverse4}
    \tilde\epsilon_{{\bm{\theta}}}({\bm{x}}_t,{\bm{c}},t)=\lambda\epsilon_{{\bm{\theta}}}({\bm{x}}_t,{\bm{c}},t) + (1-\lambda)\epsilon_{{\bm{\theta}}}({\bm{x}}_t,t),
\end{equation}
```

where $`\lambda>0`$ is the trade-off parameter, and $`\epsilon_{{\bm{\theta}}}({\bm{x}}_t,t)`$ is the unconditional $`{\bm{\epsilon}}`$-prediction. For example, setting $`\lambda=1`$ disables the unconditional guidance, while increasing $`\lambda > 1`$ strengthens the effect of conditional $`{\bm{\epsilon}}`$-prediction.

Given that our video diffusion restoration models are fine-tuned on pre-trained image DPMs, it is reasonable to assume that a superior pre-trained image DPM would result in an better video DPM in terms of restoration quality. To this end, a data augmentation for training conditional image DPMs is done by constructing the conditional inputs $`{\bm{c}}\in\mathbb{R}^{Nd}`$ as follows

```math
\begin{equation}
    \label{eq:mcondition}
    {\bm{c}}= \bm{m}_c \odot ({\bm{y}})\uparrow^{s}_{bicubic},
\end{equation}
```

where $`\bm{m}_c`$ is a weighted mask that randomly reduces the importance of some pixels, analog to the masked augmentation training proposed in . We have observed that this data augmentation on $`{\bm{c}}`$ can improve the restoration results especially on large motion degradation, as shown in Fig. <a href="#Fig:figwmask" data-reference-type="ref" data-reference="Fig:figwmask">9</a>. The conditional input $`{\bm{c}}`$ is normalized to intensity range of $`[-1,1]`$ for better performance and stable training. We train all image DPMs in half precision (`float16`) with a batch-size of 64. We use the Adam optimizer with a fixed learning rate of $`1.5\times 10^{-4}`$ and a dropout rate of 0.2 for each model. In Fig. <a href="#Fig:figkernels" data-reference-type="ref" data-reference="Fig:figkernels">14</a>, we present samples of synthetically generated random kernels, following , used to generate the image and video deblurring dataset.

## Implementations of Video DPM

We use `einops`  to efficiently rearrange the features between spatial and temporal layers.

**Group Normalization for Sequential Features.** For video DPMs, we observe that directly calculating group normalization to video features as independent images by rearranging the input as $`\mathbb{R}^{B\times N\times C\times H\times W}\rightarrow\mathbb{R}^{(BN)\times C\times H\times W}`$ results in temperature unalignment across frames. When calculating the group normalization, we consider the entire video by rearranging the input from $`\mathbb{R}^{B\times N\times C\times H\times W}`$ to $`\mathbb{R}^{B\times C\times N\times H\times W}`$, Consequently, the group normalization is computed along the $`N`$, $`H`$, $`W`$ axis. We have observed that applying this rearrangement to group normalization layers, which are pre-trained in image DPM, does not result in any performance degradation.

**More details about RFE Module.** As introduced in the main paper, we implement recurrent feature enhancement (RFE) module to capture sequential dependencies and synchronize video frame features at high resolutions (_e.g.,_ \[512, 256\]). Fig <a href="#fig:dcn" data-reference-type="ref" data-reference="fig:dcn">10</a> illustrates one basic block of our RFE module. Given the extracted temporal features $`\{\tilde{\bm{f}}_i^{n}\}_{n=1}^{N}`$ from the 3D residual blocks at $`i`$-th resolution scale, we apply Deformable Feature Alignment (DFA)  to propagate and align the intermediate features $`\widehat{\bm{f}}_{i,j}^{n}`$ as

```math
\begin{align*}
    &\hat{\bm{f}}_{i,j}^{n} = \texttt{DFA}(\tilde{\bm{f}}_i^{n}, {\bm{g}}_{i,j}^{n-1}, {\bm{g}}_{i,j}^{n-2}, {\bm{o}}_i^{n\rightarrow n-1}, {\bm{o}}_i^{n\rightarrow n-2}),
\end{align*}
```

where $`{\bm{g}}^{n-1}_{i,j}`$ and $`{\bm{g}}^{n-2}_{i,j}`$ are the features at the $`(n-1)`$-th and $`(n-2)`$-th sequential step in the $`j`$-th propagation branch, respectively. For example, we have $`{\bm{g}}^{n}_{i,0}=\tilde{\bm{f}}_i^{n}`$. Similarly, the $`{\bm{o}}_i^{n_1\rightarrow n_2}`$ denotes the optical flow estimated from $`n_1`$-th degraded input frame to the $`n_2`$-th counterparts. The features $`\hat{\bm{f}}_{i,j}^{n}`$ are then concatenated (`cat`) and passed into a stack of residual blocks (`ResBlocks`) to fuse $`{\bm{g}}_{i,j}^{n}`$, denoted as

```math
\begin{align}
    \label{eq:annealing}
    &\tilde{\bm{g}}_{i,j}^{n} = \hat{\bm{f}}_{i,j}^{n} + \texttt{ResBlocks}(\texttt{cat}({\bm{g}}_{i,j-1}^{n},\hat{\bm{f}}_{i,j}^{n})),\\
    &{\bm{g}}_{i,j}^{n} = \tilde w*(\bm{1} - \tilde{\bm{m}}_t^n) \odot \tilde{\bm{g}}_{i,j}^{n} + (\tilde{\bm{m}}_t^n) \odot \tilde{\bm{g}}_{i,j}^{n},
\end{align}
```

where $`\tilde w \in [0,1]`$ balances the smoothness of the background scenes of the fused featur, denoted as $`(\bm{1}-\tilde{\bm{m}}_t^n)\odot\tilde{\bm{g}}_{i,j}^n`$. The masks $`\{\tilde{\bm{m}}_t^n\}_{n=1}^N`$ are the downscale version of facial region masks $`\bm{m}_t = \{\bm{m}_t^n\}_{n=1}^N`$ estimated from $`{\bm{x}}_{0t}`$ at the $`t`$-th reverse diffusion step. The main motivation behind the design of propagation annealing is to enhance robustness against appearance changes and error accumulation within the recurrent network. We have observed that this annealing can notably improve the temporal consistency of background scenes across frames while preserving the sharpness of facial region, as shown in Fig <a href="#Fig:anneal" data-reference-type="ref" data-reference="Fig:anneal">11</a>.

## Training of video DPMs

All video DPMs are fine tuned with batch size $`B=4`$ and frame length $`N=10`$. We set schedule $`T=1000`$ and uniformly spaced $`\beta_t`$ for both video deblurring and JPEG restoration, while $`T=2000`$ for video super-resolution tasks. We use the Adam optimizer with a fixed learning rate of $`1\times 10^{-4}`$ and weight-decay of $`0.05`$ for fine-tuning the video DPMs. Similarly, we train all DPMs in half precision ($`\texttt{float16}`$). We do not apply classifier free guidance for fine-tuning video diffusion model. Note that, we do not perform any checkpoint selection on our models and simply select the latest checkpoint of each model. It will take around a week to get a video DPM.

## Baseline Methods

**CodeFormer , VQFR  and RestoreFormer++ ** refer to recently developed conditioning generative methods that use pre-trained Vector-Quantization (VQ) codebooks as dictionaries, achieving SOTA results in blind face restoration. These codebooks are learned on the entire facial region. We employ their original implementations [^1]$`^{,}`$[^2]$`^{,}`$[^3] and pre-trained models for our tasks. For all these three baseline methods, we follow their original implementations of frame background enhancement accordingly.

**VRT ** denotes a recently developed video restoration transformer (VRT) method, characterized by its parallel frame prediction and long-range temporal dependency modeling abilities. VRT has been shown superior performance for general restoration tasks such as video denoising, deblurring, super-resolution, etc. We modify the publicly available implementation [^4] and train the model for each task on the same CelebV-Text  video training dataset as FLAIR.

**BasicVSPP ** is another recent SOTA method based on recurrent refinement structure for video super-resolution. BasicVSPP improves over BasicVSR  by proposing a second-order grid propagation with flow guided deformable alignment. Likewise, we modify the publicly available implementation [^5] and train the model on the same CelebV-Text  training dataset as FLAIR.

**ILVR  and DR2E ** are two recently developed conditioning methods based on unconditionally trained image DPM for solving versatile blind image restoration tasks. Both ILVR and DR2E share the similar conditional sampling implementation, whereas DR2E adapts an additional enhancement module for face regions similar to FLAIR. We modify the publicly available implementation [^6]$`^{,}`$[^7] of both methods for each FVR task. We use the similar grid search to FLAIR for fine-tuning the hyper-parameters within ILVR and DR2E, respectively.

**DDNM  and DiffPIR ** refer to recently developed conditioning methods based on unconditionally trained image DPM for solving general image inverse problems. Unlike ILVR and DR2E, DDNM and DiffPIR rely on the forward-model to impose data-consistency. Similarly, we modify the publicly available implementation [^8]$`^{,}`$[^9] of both methods for each FVR task. We use the similar grid search to FLAIR for fine-tuning the hyper-parameters within DDNM and DiffPIR, respectively.

We pre-train an unconditional image DPM on FFHQ and then fine tune it on the same CelebV-Text images used for video DPMs as additional baseline. All diffusion model based baseline methods, including ILVR, DR2E, DDNM, DiffPIR share the same unconditional image DPM. We train the baseline unconditional diffusion model modified based on the publicly available PyTorch implementation [^10] for around $`1\times 10^7`$ samples in total (pre-training and fine-tuning).

## Face Detection and Processing

We process the images using the tools provided in `facexlib`[^11].

**Face Region Affine Transformation.** We first use `RetinaFace` [^12] to calculate the face landmarks. Then we use `OpenCV`  to estimate affine matrices and transform the images to the head-only version with bicubic interpolation.

**Estimation of Face Mask $`\bm{m}_t`$.** We use `ParseNet`  to get the face parsing map, and convert it to a soft mask $`\bm{m}_t`$ with Gaussian blurring. The above process has been widely adapted for FVR in recent methods, such as .

# Datasets

**CelebV-HQ ** dataset is a large-scale, high-quality video dataset with rich facial attributes for video generation and editing. CelebV-HQ contains $`35, 666`$ video clips with the resolution of $`512 \times 512`$ at least. All data is publicly available [^13]. We randomly select $`20`$ clips, each containing $`25`$ high quality sequences from CelebV-HQ.

**CelebV-Text ** dataset is another large-scale, high-quality, diverse dataset of facial text-video pairs. CelebV-Text comprises $`70,000`$ in-the-wild face video clips with diverse visual content. All data is publicly available [^14]. we select $`7200`$ clips with each containing $`20`$ high quality $`512\times512`$ sequences for training. For video testing datasets, we randomly chose $`125`$ short clips and $`6`$ long clips from the unused portion of the CelebV-Text, ensuring no identity overlap with the fine-tuning datasets. Each short clip contains $`25`$ sequences, and each long clip contains $`100`$ sequences. As highlighted by its original authors, the videos that have appeared in CelebV-HQ are filtered out.

**Obama Clip.** We select the video part C [^15] from the Obama dataset . We extract the first $`100`$ frames from original videos. We crop out the head-only region from the frames using the same processes described in <a href="#Sec:image_processing" data-reference-type="ref" data-reference="Sec:image_processing">8.6</a>.

**Web Video Clip.** We extract a low quality web video of $`300`$ frames from Internet [^16], which suffers from complex unknown degradation. For video deblurring, we present the visual results through Fig.<a href="#Fig:visua65" data-reference-type="ref" data-reference="Fig:visua65">23</a> to <a href="#Fig:visual1" data-reference-type="ref" data-reference="Fig:visual1">26</a>. For real-world web video enhancement task, we assume the LQ inputs $`{\bm{y}}`$ corrupted by mixed degradation. Since our video DPM is trained for multi-variant degradation, we only need to fine-tune the data-consistency module. By fine-tuning the forward-model such that $`{\mathcal{A}}{\mathcal{A}}^{+}{\bm{y}}\approx {\bm{y}}`$, we observe that the degradation of $`4\times`$ SR with Gaussian kernel of width$`=1.6`$, JPEG $`Q=90`$ works the best. In Fig. <a href="#Fig:visual15" data-reference-type="ref" data-reference="Fig:visual15">27</a>, we present more visual results of our FLAIR compared with several baseline methods. One can see from Fig. <a href="#Fig:visual15" data-reference-type="ref" data-reference="Fig:visual15">27</a> that our designed two stage enhancement modules together can improve visual quality while preserving the data-consistency effectively.
