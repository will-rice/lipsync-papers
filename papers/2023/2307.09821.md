# Hierarchical Semantic Perceptual Listener Head Video Generation: A High-performance Pipeline

Zhigang Chang Du Xiaoman FinancialShanghai Jiao Tong UniversityBeijing-ShanghaiChina <changzhigang@duxiaoman.com> <changzig@sjtu.edu.cn> ,  Weitai Hu Du Xiaoman FinancialBeijingChina <huweitai@duxiaoman.com> ,  Qing Yang Du Xiaoman FinancialBeijingChina <yangqing@duxiaoman.com>  and  Shibao Zheng Shanghai Jiao Tong UniversityShanghaiChina <sbzh@sjtu.edu.cn>

\(2023\)

###### Abstract.

In dyadic speaker-listener interactions, the listener’s head reactions along with the speaker’s head movements, constitute an important non-verbal semantic expression together. The listener Head generation task aims to synthesize responsive listener’s head videos based on audios of the speaker and reference images of the listener. Compared to the Talking-head generation, it is more challenging to capture the correlation clues from the speaker’s audio and visual information. Following the ViCo baseline scheme, we propose a high-performance solution by enhancing the hierarchical semantic extraction capability of the audio encoder module and improving the decoder part, renderer and post-processing modules. Our solution gets the first place on the official leaderboard for the track of listening head generation. This paper is a technical report of ViCo@2023 Conversational Head Generation Challenge in ACM Multimedia 2023 conference.

## 1. Introduction

In interactive conversational scenarios, facial expressions, head movements and micro-expressive reactions of both the speaker and the listener are capable of conveying important non-verbal semantics. Humans are able to acutely capture these non-verbal semantics while communicating verbally and respond in the same way. In dyadic speaker-listener interactions, we similarly expect machines to have the ability to perceive the speaker’s verbal and non-verbal semantics in multiple dimensions and simulate and generate the listener’s head response. This task is called “Responsive Listener Head Generation” which has great potential for applications in areas such as human-computer interaction, sentiment analysis, animation and metaverse.

Compared to the Talking-head generation (Wang et al., 2020; Zhu et al., 2021), there is much less research on listener head generation. This is because the mapping relationship between speech and speaker’s facial expressions and lip movements can be learned more strictly, while the listener’s reaction to the speaker’s audio and visual input is relatively ambiguous, that is, different head reactions can be appropriate and reasonable. However, when carefully comparing the videos of the speaker and the listener in an interaction, we can still find that the non-verbal semantic behaviors of the listener (such as nodding, smiling and head shaking) are feedback on the hierarchical multi-modal semantics of the speaker (such as linguistic emotions, speech rhythms, expressions and eyes contact), and carry personal behavioral styles. So the question now is how to extract the correlation clues between the speaker’s audio and video information and the listener’s non-verbal semantic behaviors.

Inspired by (Liu et al., 2022), we adopt a Hierarchical Audio Encoder to fully exploit the semantics of different levels. Through this encoder, we believe that both high-level speech semantics (such as the emotions and tone conveyed by the content) and low-level speech semantics (such as the rhythm and pitch of the speech) can be captured and matched to the listener’s responsive behaviors. Similar to the ViCo baseline (Zhou et al., 2022), we also use the face reconstruction model (Guo et al., 2020) to extract the speaker’s three-dimensional morphable model (3DMM) coefficients (Blanz and Vetter, 1999; Paysan et al., 2009) as a visual signal encoder. A cascaded bi-directional GRU network acts as a decoder to predict the sequential outputs of listener head coefficients. In the final rendering and post-processing stage, we employ an enhanced renderer (Tang et al., 2022) and a video restoration module (Liang et al., 2022) to enhance the quality of the generated videos.

In general, this paper reports a high-performance solution to the Responsive Listener Head Generation task. Our main techniques include:

- •
  We adopt a Hierarchical Audio Encoder to capture hierarchical speech semantics, and design a audio feature fusion module to fuse audio features.
- •
  We adopt a cascaded bi-directional GRU as a decoder to predict the sequential outputs. Several training techniques are applied on the limited training data.
- •
  We adopt an enhanced renderer and a video restoration module to improve the quality of the generated videos significantly.

## 2. Related Work

Audio-Driven human-centric synthesis tasks (Prajwal et al., 2020; Wang et al., 2020; Zhu et al., 2021; Ginosar et al., 2019; Liu et al., 2022) have attracted extensive research interest in recent years. The Audio-to-lip task (Prajwal et al., 2020) aims to generate a lip-synchronization video clip with a initial frame input. The Talking-head generation task (Wang et al., 2020; Zhu et al., 2021) tends to output a vivid talking video of the speaker with head movements from a speaker’s initial frame and a clip of audio. The Audio-to-gesture task (Ginosar et al., 2019; Liu et al., 2022) tries to learn mappings between audios and body languages. The commonality of the above generation tasks are that they are all based on the role of the speaker, and they all need to build the associations between speech semantics and target behaviors. Listening head generation task (Zhou et al., 2023, 2022; Luo et al., 2023) is a non-verbal facial reaction prediction task. Recent facial reaction studies (Ng et al., 2022; Zhou et al., 2022) frequently represent facial motions with 3DMM coefficients (e.g., expression and pose coefficients) to better visualize the facial muscle movements. Meanwhile, speaker’s speech and audio behaviours were also additionally employed as the input to deliver richer verbal and non-verbal speaker behavioural cues (Ng et al., 2022; Song et al., 2022; Shao et al., 2021).

## 3. Task Overview

### 3.1. Definition

We first briefly introduce the task definition of Responsive Listening Head Generation. Given an input video sequence $`\mathcal{V}_{t}^{s} = \left\{ v_{1}^{s},\ldots,v_{t}^{s} \right\}`$ of the speaker head in time stamps ranging from $`\left\{ 1,\ldots,t \right\}`$, and the corresponding audio signal sequence $`\mathcal{A}_{t}^{s} = \left\{ a_{1},\ldots,a_{t} \right\}`$, the goal of this task is to generate the whole listener’s head video sequence $`\mathcal{V}_{t}^{l} = \left\{ v_{2}^{l},\ldots,v_{t + 1} \right\}`$, where output of the next time stamp can be denoted as Eq. 1 and $`\mathcal{V}_{1}^{l}`$ is the initial reference frame.

```math
\mathcal{V}_{t + 1}^{l} = {\mathbf{G}\hspace{0pt}\left( \mathcal{V}_{t}^{s},\mathcal{A}_{t}^{s},\mathcal{V}_{1}^{l} \right)}
```

### 3.2. Dataset Description and Evaluation Metrics

This ViCo@2023 challenge is based on the extended ViCo dataset. The training set contains 440 pairs of speaker-listener videos with 83 listener IDs and 65 speaker IDs. The listening head test set contains 45 audio clips, speaker video clips and several listener reference images.

The quality of generated videos will be quantitative evaluated from the prespectives of visual quality, naturalness, and task-specified goals. The evaluation meaning of each indicator is as follows:

The PSNR (Peak Signal to Noise Ratio) and SSIM (Structure Similarity Index Measure) can evaluate the quality of generated images by comparing with GT images.

The CPBD (perceptual-based no-reference objective image sharpness metric) can evaluate the sharpness of the generated images without GT images.

The FID (Fréchet Inception Distance) evaluates the similarity of the generated images and GT in feature level.

The CSIM is evaluated by extracting identity features predicted by ArcFace and calculate the cosine similarity.

The ExpL1 and PoseL1 evaluate L1 distances of expression and pose coefficients from 3DMM reconstruction.

The ExpFD and PoseFD evaluate Fréchet distances of expression and pose coefficients from 3DMM reconstruction.

## 4. Method

### 4.1. Framework

The overall pipeline is illustrated in Fig. 1 The pipeline is composed of an audio encoder, a visual encoder, a sequential decoder, a video renderer and a post-processing module. Then we will introduce each component.

### 4.2. Visual Encoder

Following the ViCo baseline scheme (Zhou et al., 2022), 3DMM coefficients are extracted by a face reconstruction model (Guo et al., 2020) for each frame. We get 3DMM coefficients $`{\mathcal{x}}_{t} = \left( \alpha_{t},\beta_{t},\epsilon_{t},\gamma_{t},p_{t} \right)`$ for each time stamp where the identity-independent features can be represented parametrically by expression and pose coefficients $`\left\{ {{\beta \in {\mathbb{R}}^{64}},{p \in {\mathbb{R}}^{6}}} \right\}`$.

### 4.3. Audio Encoder

We adopt the Hierarchical Audio Encoder $`E_{a}`$ in HA2G (Liu et al., 2022) to extract multi-level semantic features. Transcript can be directly recognized by Automatic Speech Recognition (ASR) models from audio$`\mathcal{A}_{t}^{s}`$. High-level linguistic features extracted by text encoder from transcripts $`t_{t\hspace{0pt}e\hspace{0pt}x\hspace{0pt}t}`$ as $`f_{t} = {E_{t\hspace{0pt}e\hspace{0pt}x\hspace{0pt}t}\hspace{0pt}{(t_{t\hspace{0pt}e\hspace{0pt}x\hspace{0pt}t})}}`$. $`E_{a}`$ is a ResNetSE34 borrowed from (Chung et al., 2020). Specifically, we define the features output from ResNet Stage-2 as low-level features $`f_{a}^{l\hspace{0pt}o\hspace{0pt}w}`$, features output from ResNet Stage-3 as middle-level audio features $`f_{a}^{m\hspace{0pt}i\hspace{0pt}d}`$, features output from ResNet Stage-4 as high-level audio features $`f_{a}^{h\hspace{0pt}i\hspace{0pt}g\hspace{0pt}h}`$. We propose to learn the association between provided transcripts and audios with contrastive learning. The training strategy is to leverage the natural synchronization between text and audio. The multi-level contrastive loss are computed by Eq. 2 to enforce the high-level audio features $`f_{a}^{h\hspace{0pt}i\hspace{0pt}g\hspace{0pt}h}`$ aligned with the text features $`f_{t}`$.

```math
L_{c\hspace{0pt}o\hspace{0pt}n\hspace{0pt}s\hspace{0pt}t} = {- {\log\frac{\exp\left( {{s\hspace{0pt}i\hspace{0pt}m\hspace{0pt}\left( f_{t},f_{a +}^{h\hspace{0pt}i\hspace{0pt}g\hspace{0pt}h} \right)}/\tau} \right)}{\sum_{i = 1}^{K}{\sum_{l \in L}{\exp\left( {{s\hspace{0pt}i\hspace{0pt}m\hspace{0pt}\left( f_{t},f_{a\hspace{0pt}{(i)}}^{l} \right)}/\tau} \right)}}}}}
```

where $`L = \left\{ {low},{mid},{high} \right\}`$ and $`{s\hspace{0pt}i\hspace{0pt}m\hspace{0pt}{(f_{1},f_{2})}} = \frac{f_{1} \cdot f_{2}}{\left| f_{1} \right|\hspace{0pt}\left| f_{2} \right|}`$ is cosine similarity. $`f_{a +}^{h\hspace{0pt}i\hspace{0pt}g\hspace{0pt}h}`$ is the high-level audio features aligned to the transcript as the positive samples. Negative samples are collected from high-level audio features of other time stamps or non-high-level semantic audio features. $`\tau`$ is the temperature parameter.

We also implement the pre-trained language model BERT (Devlin et al., 2018) on transcripts to get the content semantics $`S_{T} = \left\{ s_{1},\ldots,s_{t} \right\}`$. Mel-frequency cepstral coefficients (MFCC) features with the corresponding MFCC Delta and Delta-Delta (second-order difference) features are extracted from the original speech as well, denoted as $`M_{T} = \left\{ m_{1},\ldots,m_{t} \right\}`$.

An audio feature fusion module $`f_{A - {F\hspace{0pt}F\hspace{0pt}M}}`$ is designed to obtain a fusion of multiple speech features $`f_{a}^{h\hspace{0pt}i\hspace{0pt}g\hspace{0pt}h}`$, $`f_{a}^{m\hspace{0pt}i\hspace{0pt}d}`$, $`f_{a}^{l\hspace{0pt}o\hspace{0pt}w}`$, $`S_{T}`$ and $`M_{T}`$. We adopt a SE-ResNet Module (Hu et al., 2018) as the audio feature fusion module to capture the channel attention. The output of the audio encoder can be denoted as follows:

```math
A_{t}^{A\hspace{0pt}E} = {f_{A - {F\hspace{0pt}F\hspace{0pt}M}}\hspace{0pt}\left( f_{a\hspace{0pt}t}^{h\hspace{0pt}i\hspace{0pt}g\hspace{0pt}h},f_{a\hspace{0pt}t}^{m\hspace{0pt}i\hspace{0pt}d},f_{a\hspace{0pt}t}^{l\hspace{0pt}o\hspace{0pt}w},S_{t},M_{t} \right)}
```

### 4.4. Sequential Decoder

After acquiring audio and visual representations of the speaker, a cross-modal fusion function $`f_{X\hspace{0pt}M}`$ is implemented to get the cross-modal comprehensive representations of the speaker:

```math
e_{t}^{x\hspace{0pt}m} = {f_{X\hspace{0pt}M}\hspace{0pt}\left( \beta_{t},p_{t},A_{t}^{A\hspace{0pt}E} \right)}
```

At each time stamp $`t`$, the decoder module takes the he cross-modal comprehensive representation $`e_{t}^{x\hspace{0pt}m}`$ as input and generate the listener’s motion parameters $`\beta_{t}`$ and $`p_{t}`$. The architecture of sequential decoder contains a six-layer bi-directional GRU. The decoding procedure can be formulated as:

```math
{\beta_{t + 1},p_{t + 1}} = {\mathbf{G}_{GRU}\hspace{0pt}\left( h_{t},e_{t}^{x\hspace{0pt}m} \right)}
```

where $`h_{t}`$ is the $`t`$-th hidden state.

For the decoder model optimization, we supervise each prediction $`\beta_{t},p_{t}`$ with the groud truth (GT) of 3DMM coefficients in training set. The regression loss of the decoder is calculated as:

```math
\begin{array}{cl}
L_{d\hspace{0pt}e\hspace{0pt}c} & {= {{\sum\limits_{t = 2}^{T}{\|{\beta_{t} - \hat{\beta_{t}}}\|}_{2}} + {\|{p_{t} - \hat{p_{t}}}\|}_{2}}} \\
 & {{+ {\sum\limits_{t = 2}^{T}{w_{1}\hspace{0pt}{\|{{\mu\hspace{0pt}{(\beta_{t})}} - {\mu\hspace{0pt}{(\hat{\beta_{t}})}}}\|}_{2}}}} + {\sum\limits_{t = 2}^{T}{w_{2}\hspace{0pt}{\|{{\mu\hspace{0pt}{(p_{t})}} - {\mu\hspace{0pt}{(\hat{p_{t}})}}}\|}_{2}}}}
\end{array}
```

where $`\hat{\beta_{t}}`$, $`\hat{p_{t}}`$ denote the groud truth. The last two terms of Eq. 6 apply motion constraints to guarantee the inter-frame continuity, where $`\mu\hspace{0pt}( \cdot )`$ measures the inter-frame changes.

### 4.5. Video Renderer and Post-processing Module

The video renderer module in our pipeline refers to a controllable 3D-aware portrait generation model (Ren et al., 2021; Tang et al., 2022), which is able to convert the predicted sequential 3DMM coefficients (expressions and head poses) to video based on a reference image. The ViCo baseline adopts PIRenderer (Ren et al., 2021) as video renderer. Through observations, it can be found that PIRenderer performs well in general, but has several defects, including background distortion and poor performance on preserving the identity when large angles. Thus we have adopted a novel state-of-the-art renderer called 3DFaceShop (Tang et al., 2022). 3DFaceShop proposes a volume blending strategy where the renderer can form a composite output by blending dynamic and static areas, with two parts segmented from the jointly learned semantic field. With this blending strategy, 3DFaceShop could solve non-face area distortion and produce realistic portraits when viewed from free viewpoints. Experiments have demonstrated that the adoption of 3DFaceShop can lead to significant performance gains.

The final post-processing module is a video restoration method application, i.e., RVRT (Liang et al., 2022), to overcome the bottleneck due to the low-resolution and blurred training data. Specifically, we utilize pre-trained video restoration models to enhance facial details and improve visualization as much as possible.

## 5. Experiments

### 5.1. Overall Evaluation on the leaderboard

As shown in Tab. 1, our proposed solution won five 1st places on the official leaderboard out of nine evaluation metrics, and finally achieved the 1st place in the overall score on the track of listening head generation. Our proposed solution leads the way in SSIM, PSNR, PoseL1, ExpL1 and PoseFD evaluation metrics, which indicates our generated videos are of excellent quality, both from visual prespective and from task-specified goals prespective.

### 5.2. Limited Alation Study of submitted results

In order to validate the effectiveness of the components of our proposed solution, we selected a few of the many results submitted to the online evaluation system for limited ablation experiments. Due to the large number of results submitted, the selected results do not guarantee that the experimental setups are aligned, and the results can only be used as a reference, with more rigorous ablation experiments to be re-organized. As shown in Tab. 2, our solution with PIRenderer has substantial performance gains compared to ViCo baseline, proving that our proposed audio encoder and sequential decoder are more capable of learning the associations between speech semantics and listener head behaviors. The experimental results also proved that the 3DFaceShop (Tang et al., 2022) renderer was able to alleviate some of the problems that existed in the previous PIRenderer, bringing some performance gains.

### 5.3. Qualitative Visualization

We show the visualization results of our solution in Fig. 2. Based on the reference image of the first frame in the leftmost column, it can be observed that our method is able to generate high-quality photo-realistic video clips of the listening heads.

## 6. Conclusion

This paper is a technical report of ViCo@2023 Conversational Head Generation Challenge in ACM Multimedia 2023 conference. Following the ViCo baseline scheme, we propose a high-performance solution by enhancing the herarchical semantic extraction capability of the audio encoder module and improving the decoder part, renderer and post-processing modules. Our solution gets the first place on the official leaderboard for the track of listening head generation.
