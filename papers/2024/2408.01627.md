# JambaTalk: Speech-driven 3D Talking Head Generation based on a Hybrid Transformer-Mamba Model

Farzaneh Jafari [0009-0007-1901-2860](https://orcid.org/0009-0007-1901-2860 "ORCID identifier") University of Alberta, Multimedia Research Center (MRC)Edmonton, ABAlbertaCanada <farzane1@ualberta.ca> , Stefano Berretti University of Florence, Media Integration and Communication Center (MICC)FlorenceItaly <stefano.berretti@unifi.it> and Anup Basu University of Alberta, Multimedia Research Center (MRC)Edmonton, ABAlbertaCanada <basu@ualberta.ca>

\(2025\)

###### Abstract.

In recent years, the talking head generation has become a focal point for researchers. Considerable effort is being made to refine lip-sync motion, capture expressive facial expressions, generate natural head poses, and achieve high-quality video. However, no single model has yet achieved equivalence across all quantitative and qualitative metrics. We introduce JambaTalk, a hybrid Transformer-Mamba model, to animate a 3D face. Mamba, a pioneering Structured State Space Model (SSM) architecture, was developed to overcome the limitations of conventional Transformer architectures, particularly in handling long sequences. This challenge has constrained traditional models. Jamba combines the advantages of both the Transformer and Mamba approaches, offering a comprehensive solution. Based on the foundational Jamba block, we present JambaTalk to enhance motion variety and lip sync through multimodal integration. Extensive experiments reveal that our method achieves performance comparable or superior to state-of-the-art models. Supplementary video and code are available at: [https://JambaTalk.github.io/](https://farzanehjafari1987.github.io/JambaTalk.github.io/).

Facial Animation Synthesis, Virtual Humans, 3D Mesh Animation, Blendshape Animation, Selective State Space Models (S6), Mixture of Experts, Transformers, Mamba, and Low-Rank Learned Rotary Positional Embedding.

## 1. Introduction

Talking-head generation has gained increasing attention for applications in virtual avatars, gaming, online education, and communication. Recent advances have improved lip-sync accuracy, natural facial motion, and high-quality 3D avatar synthesis. However, many existing approaches rely on Transformer-based architectures (Fan et al., [2022](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.01627v3#bib.bib12); Xing et al., [2023](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.01627v3#bib.bib68); Peng et al., [2023a](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.01627v3#bib.bib40)), which face limitations when modeling long sequences due to high computational complexity and memory requirements. This challenge restricts scalability and efficiency, particularly when looking for real-time applications or large-scale datasets.

Many existing methods focus primarily on lip-sync accuracy from audio input (Liu et al., [2023](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.01627v3#bib.bib30); Zhong et al., [2023](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.01627v3#bib.bib76); Xu et al., [2024a](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.01627v3#bib.bib71); Wei et al., [2024](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.01627v3#bib.bib67); Xu et al., [2024b](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.01627v3#bib.bib70); Ye et al., [2023](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.01627v3#bib.bib74)). Most audio-driven talking face generation approaches adopt a two-stage pipeline. In the first stage, an intermediate representation, such as 2D landmarks (Tang et al., [2022](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.01627v3#bib.bib64); Shen et al., [2023](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.01627v3#bib.bib50); Du et al., [2023](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.01627v3#bib.bib11); Wang et al., [2023](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.01627v3#bib.bib66); Guan et al., [2023](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.01627v3#bib.bib19)) or 3D face model blend-shape coefficients (Ko et al., [2024](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.01627v3#bib.bib25); Su et al., [2023](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.01627v3#bib.bib54); Nocentini and Berretti., [2023](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.01627v3#bib.bib33)), is predicted from the audio input. In the second stage, a renderer synthesizes the video portrait based on this representation. Audio-driven talking face generation aims to produce high-quality, lip-synchronized face videos from a given audio clip and target face image. However, this task remains challenging given the inherent differences between audio and visual modalities. The generation of a one-shot 3D talking portrait further increases the complexity for attempting to create a 3D avatar from a single unseen image and animating it using a reference video or audio (Ye et al., [2024](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.01627v3#bib.bib75)). Moreover, face-to-face spoken dialogue models capture audio-visual speech input from the user and generate audio-visual responses, serving as a first step toward avatar-based chatbots that operate without intermediate text (Sun et al., [2022](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.01627v3#bib.bib57); Goyal et al., [2023](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.01627v3#bib.bib16); Tan et al., [2024b](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.01627v3#bib.bib62), [d](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.01627v3#bib.bib60), [c](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.01627v3#bib.bib63)). Another open challenge lies in generalizing talking-face models to multiple languages and diverse text inputs (Anderson et al., [2013](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.01627v3#bib.bib3); Song et al., [2022](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.01627v3#bib.bib51)).

State-Space Models (SSMs) have recently demonstrated significant potential for addressing long-range dependency issues (Gu and Dao, [2023](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.01627v3#bib.bib17)), but they still fall short of the performance achieved by comparably sized Transformer-based models. To overcome these limitations, we introduce JambaTalk, built upon Jamba, a publicly available large language model that employs a hybrid architecture combining Transformer layers with Mamba layers, along with a Mixture-of-Experts (MoE) mechanism. This combination leverages the strengths of attention (for global context modeling) and SSMs (for efficient long-sequence processing), resulting in improved performance, higher throughput, and reduced memory footprint compared to traditional Transformer-only models. The key novelty of Jamba lies in its hybrid design, which balances memory efficiency, long-context handling (up to 256K tokens), and scalability. MoE layers are incorporated into the MLP components, expanding the total model capacity without increasing the computational cost. This design enables Jamba to handle extremely long sequences efficiently, where attention-only models often struggle (Lieber et al., [2024](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.01627v3#bib.bib29)). Our implementation runs efficiently on a single 24GB GPU, demonstrating high throughput, low memory usage, and state-of-the-art performance in long-context evaluations. The main contributions of our work are summarized below:

- •
  We introduce JambaTalk, an innovative framework for speech-driven 3D talking head generation that cooperates with multiple Mamba, MoE_Mamba, and Transformer layers to improve the performance of talking face generation;
- •
  We introduce Low-Rank Learned Rotary Positional Embedding (LRL-RoPE), a learnable and low-memory extension of RoPE, and take advantage of the potential of the Group Query Attention (GQA) algorithm to enhance the performance of the Transformer layer;
- •
  Comprehensive experiments and analyses on the Vocaset and $`BIWI_{\lx@text@underscore}6`$ datasets demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed model.

## 2. Related Work

### 2.1. Speech-Driven 3D Facial Animation

Speech-driven 3D facial animation has seen substantial progress in recent years, with methods focusing on generating expressive and synchronized facial movements from audio (Chen et al., [2023](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.01627v3#bib.bib7)). This research area spans traditional neural architectures, diffusion-based models, codebook-based synthesis, and controllable mesh deformation.

VOCA (Cudeiro et al., [2019](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.01627v3#bib.bib9)) introduced a neural network trained on a unique 4D face dataset, enabling identity-independent facial animation from speech. FaceFormer (Fan et al., [2022](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.01627v3#bib.bib12)) applied a Transformer-based auto-regressive architecture to capture long-term audio context, enhanced by pre-trained speech representations and bias-aware attention mechanisms. MeshTalk (Richard et al., [2021](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.01627v3#bib.bib44)) introduced a categorical latent space that separates audio-driven and audio-independent features, enabling high-quality full-face animations including blinks and eyebrow motion. Xing et al. (Xing et al., [2023](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.01627v3#bib.bib68)) reformulated facial animation as a code query problem over a learned motion codebook, improving the vividness and accuracy of generated expressions.

Diffused Heads (Stypułkowski et al., [2024](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.01627v3#bib.bib53)) demonstrated the effectiveness of diffusion-based generative models in producing realistic talking heads using only audio and a single image. FaceDiffuser (Stan et al., [2023](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.01627v3#bib.bib52)) extended this by supporting both vertex and blendshape datasets, improving compatibility with real-world animation pipelines. VividTalk (Sun et al., [2023](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.01627v3#bib.bib56)) proposed a two-stage mesh-to-video pipeline integrating non-rigid expression and rigid motion learning, with a head pose codebook and dual-branch motion decoding. Zou et al. (Zou et al., [2023](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.01627v3#bib.bib77)) presented a landmark-guided DDPM framework for generating 3D mesh sequences, enabling conditional generation from text, geometry, or partial sequences.

Rai et al. (Rai et al., [2024](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.01627v3#bib.bib42)) incorporated 2D generative models into a controllable 3D face framework for semantic manipulation. Otberdout et al. (Otberdout et al., [2020](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.01627v3#bib.bib36), [2022](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.01627v3#bib.bib37), [2023](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.01627v3#bib.bib38)) introduced a motion transition framework combining temporal GANs (Motion3DGAN) and a decoder (S2D-Dec) to generate dense point clouds from sparse 3D landmark motions between emotional states.

While early work focused primarily on lip-sync, recent efforts address expressive control and emotional realism in talking head generation. These methods incorporate emotion recognition, disentanglement, and continuous expression control. Speech Emotion Recognition (SER) plays a crucial role in realistic animation. Traditional SER approaches include handcrafted audio features (Liang et al., [2022](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.01627v3#bib.bib28); Schuller et al., [2003](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.01627v3#bib.bib46); Kwon et al., [2003](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.01627v3#bib.bib26)), while recent deep learning methods use end-to-end training for emotional feature extraction (Gan et al., [2023](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.01627v3#bib.bib15); Gururani et al., [2023](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.01627v3#bib.bib20); Ji et al., [2022](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.01627v3#bib.bib24); Saunders and Namboodiri, [2023](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.01627v3#bib.bib45); Tan et al., [2024a](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.01627v3#bib.bib61); Tian et al., [2024](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.01627v3#bib.bib65); Xu et al., [2024d](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.01627v3#bib.bib69); Papantoniou et al., [2022](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.01627v3#bib.bib39); Huang et al., [2014](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.01627v3#bib.bib22); Jain et al., [2020](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.01627v3#bib.bib23); Schuller, [2018](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.01627v3#bib.bib47); Hossain and Muhammad, [2019](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.01627v3#bib.bib21); LeCun et al., [1998](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.01627v3#bib.bib27); Mekruksavanich et al., [2020](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.01627v3#bib.bib31)). EmoVOCA (Nocentini et al., [2024b](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.01627v3#bib.bib35)) and DEITalk (Shen et al., [2024](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.01627v3#bib.bib49)) integrated SER into facial animation pipelines. EmoTalk (Peng et al., [2023b](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.01627v3#bib.bib41)) introduced an Emotion Disentangling Encoder (EDE) and a fusion decoder to separate emotional and content cues for enhanced expressivity.

EmoStyle (Azari and Lim, [2024](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.01627v3#bib.bib5)) leveraged valence-arousal coordinates and a pre-trained StyleGAN2 generator to manipulate facial expressions across a wide emotional spectrum, supporting one-shot processing of real portraits. Sung-Bin et al. (Sung-Bin et al., [2024](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.01627v3#bib.bib59)) addressed non-verbal expression by introducing LaughTalk, a model capable of generating both articulated speech and natural laughter. EMOTE (Daněček et al., [2023](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.01627v3#bib.bib10)) proposed a framework for emotion-aware 3D avatars by separating high-frequency lip-sync deformation from low-frequency emotional expressions using decoupled loss functions.

### 2.2. Selective State Space Models

Transformer models are widely used in sequence modeling but struggle with high computational and memory demands, especially for long contexts. To address this, Selective State Space Models (SSMs) have been introduced.

Mamba (Gu and Dao, [2023](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.01627v3#bib.bib17)) introduces a hardware-efficient, attention-free sequence model that adapts its internal parameters dynamically based on the input token stream. It overcomes conventional SSM limitations with a parallelized convolution-based architecture and avoids reliance on attention or MLP blocks. Mamba offers advantages in terms of memory efficiency and recurrent processing, while maintaining long-range dependency modeling.

Xu et al. (Xu et al., [2024c](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.01627v3#bib.bib72)) extended Mamba into MambaTalk, targeting speech-driven full-body gesture synthesis. It employs discrete motion priors and multimodal integration to handle the limitations of diffusion-based and attention-based methods, producing natural and diverse body motions with efficient computation. These insights form a foundation for applying SSMs to expressive facial animation, as explored in our proposed JambaTalk framework.

## 3. Proposed Method

Our primary goal is to generate sequential 3D facial animations from raw audio input and prior facial movement sequences, resulting in realistic and synchronized animations. Suppose we are given a sequence of ground-truth 3D facial motions $`P_{\lx@text@underscore}t=(p_{\lx@text@underscore}1,...,p_{\lx@text@underscore}T)`$, where $`T`$ denotes the number of visual frames sampled at a specific frame-per-second (FPS) rate for the dataset, alongside the corresponding raw audio signals $`\chi`$. The purpose of the model is to generate synthesized facial movements $`\hat{P}_{\lx@text@underscore}T`$ that closely approximate $`P_{\lx@text@underscore}T`$, using the raw audio inputs $`\chi`$ (Fan et al., [2022](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.01627v3#bib.bib12); Xing et al., [2023](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.01627v3#bib.bib68)). In this architecture, the process begins with audio encoders, which convert the raw audio signals into sequential speech representations $`A_{\lx@text@underscore}T=(a_{\lx@text@underscore}1,...,a_{\lx@text@underscore}T)`$, where $`T`$ represents the frame length of the speech representation. This speech encoding captures the temporal and phonetic dynamics required for facial movement synthesis. The motion decoder predicts facial movements $`\hat{P}_{\lx@text@underscore}T=(\hat{p}_{\lx@text@underscore}1,...,\hat{p}_{\lx@text@underscore}T)`$ based on the encoded speech sequences $`A_{\lx@text@underscore}T`$. Generally, we have:

```math
\hat{p}_{\lx@text@underscore}t=JambaTalk(a_{\lx@text@underscore}t;\eta),
```

where $`\eta`$ signifies the model parameters, $`t`$ is the current time-step in the sequence, and $`\hat{p}_{\lx@text@underscore}t\in\hat{P}_{\lx@text@underscore}T`$. Following (Fan et al., [2022](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.01627v3#bib.bib12)), we utilize the pre-trained Wav2Vec 2.0 model (Baevski et al., [2020](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.01627v3#bib.bib6)) with fixed weights. The encoder includes an audio feature extractor and a multi-layer Transformer encoder. The audio feature extractor, consisting of several temporal convolutional layers (TCN), converts the raw waveform input into feature vectors with frequency $`f_{\lx@text@underscore}a`$. A linear interpolation layer is added for resampling the audio components using the captured frequency of facial motion $`f_{\lx@text@underscore}m`$ with the output length $`kT`$, where $`k=\frac{f_{\lx@text@underscore}m}{f_{\lx@text@underscore}a}`$ (e.g., if $`f_{\lx@text@underscore}a=50`$ and $`f_{\lx@text@underscore}m=25`$, then $`k=0.5`$, meaning the audio is sampled twice as fast and should be downsampled to match the facial motion rate). These components are combined with multi-head self-attention and feed-forward layers to transform the audio feature vectors into contextualized speech representations, in which the outputs of the TCN are discretized into a finite set of speech units by a quantization module. The linear projection layer outputs can be demonstrated as $`A_{\lx@text@underscore}{kT}=(a_{\lx@text@underscore}1,...,a_{\lx@text@underscore}{kT})`$. Ultimately, the predicted facial motion $`\hat{p}_{\lx@text@underscore}t`$ is derived by mapping the $`d`$-dimensional hidden state back into the $`V`$-dimensional 3D vertex space using a motion decoder. The motion decoder utilizes multiple layers of masked self-attention and feed-forward neural networks to transform audio features into 3D mesh deformations. In this process, masked self-attention assigns weights to input embeddings based on their relevance, and the feed-forward network refines these context vectors to produce the final outputs. After generating the complete 3D facial motion sequence, the model is trained by minimizing the Mean Squared Error (MSE) between the decoder outputs $`\hat{P}_{\lx@text@underscore}T=(\hat{p}_{\lx@text@underscore}1,...,\hat{p}_{\lx@text@underscore}T)`$ and the corresponding 3D ground-truth $`P_{\lx@text@underscore}T=(p_{\lx@text@underscore}1,...,p_{\lx@text@underscore}T)`$:

```math
L_{\lx@text@underscore}{MSE}=\sum_{\lx@text@underscore}{t=1}^{T}\sum_{\lx@text@underscore}{v=1}^{V}|\hat{p}_{\lx@text@underscore}{t,v}-p_{\lx@text@underscore}{t,v}|^{2},
```

where $`V`$ represents the number of vertices in the 3D facial mesh (Fan et al., [2022](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.01627v3#bib.bib12)). To reduce jitter in the output frames caused by relying solely on the MSE loss, we add a velocity loss that promotes smoother and more realistic lip motion over time (Cudeiro et al., [2019](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.01627v3#bib.bib9)). This loss is formulated as follows:

```math
L_{\lx@text@underscore}{VEL}=\sum_{\lx@text@underscore}{t=1}^{T}\sum_{\lx@text@underscore}{v=1}^{V}|(\hat{p}_{\lx@text@underscore}{t,v}-\hat{p}_{\lx@text@underscore}{t-1,v})-(p_{\lx@text@underscore}{t,v}-p_{\lx@text@underscore}{t-1,v})|^{2}.
```

As depicted in Figure [1](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.01627v3#S3.F1 "Figure 1 ‣ 3. Proposed Method ‣ JambaTalk: Speech-driven 3D Talking Head Generation based on a Hybrid Transformer-Mamba Model"), the lip feature extraction block transforms mesh decoder outputs into lip deformation features by selecting lip vertices using a lip mask. These are processed by a Transformer-based lip encoder to obtain latent lip representations in the lip reader module. In parallel, the pre-trained wav2vec 2.0 model extracts rich latent features from audio and generates text through a pre-trained language decoder, which maps the features to a 32-character vocabulary plus a blank token, selects the highest probability outputs, and applies post-processing to form the transcription. This produces both latent and text features without requiring ground-truth text input. The lip latent features are aligned with audio-derived features using a latent consistency loss, and a text decoder maps the lip features to the same vocabulary space. The resulting outputs are compared with the audio transcriptions using CTC loss to ensure consistency (Peng et al., [2023a](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.01627v3#bib.bib40)). The formula for calculating the latent consistency loss is:

```math
L_{\lx@text@underscore}{LAT}=\frac{1}{T}\sum_{\lx@text@underscore}{t=1}^{T}\frac{1}{N}\sum_{\lx@text@underscore}{i=1}^{N}\frac{(als_{\lx@text@underscore}{t,n}-lls_{\lx@text@underscore}{t,n})^{2}}{2},
```

where $`als_{\lx@text@underscore}{t,n}`$ represents the features extracted from the audio encoder, while $`lls_{\lx@text@underscore}{t,n}`$ represents the features extracted from the lip encoder. $`N`$ denotes the number of samples. The CTC loss can be defined as:

```math
L_{\lx@text@underscore}{\mathrm{CTC}}=-\log p\big(\pi\in\mathcal{B}^{-1}(S)\mid\mathbf{e}_{\lx@text@underscore}{1:T}\big),
```

where $`\mathbf{e}_{\lx@text@underscore}{1:T}=(\mathbf{e}_{\lx@text@underscore}1,\mathbf{e}_{\lx@text@underscore}2,\dots,\mathbf{e}_{\lx@text@underscore}T)`$ represents the sequence of lip features extracted from the text decoder, $`S=(s_{\lx@text@underscore}1,s_{\lx@text@underscore}2,\dots,s_{\lx@text@underscore}T)`$ is the target transcription, $`\pi`$ is a possible alignment sequence between $`\mathbf{e}_{\lx@text@underscore}{1:T}`$ and $`S`$. The operator $`\mathcal{B}`$ removes repeated labels and blanks, and $`\mathcal{B}^{-1}(S)`$ represents all valid alignment sequences corresponding to $`S`$.

To compute the CTC loss, a projection layer maps the lip features to output probabilities:

```math
\mathbf{p}_{\lx@text@underscore}t=\mathrm{softmax}\big(\mathbf{W}_{\lx@text@underscore}p\mathbf{e}_{\lx@text@underscore}t+\mathbf{b}_{\lx@text@underscore}p\big),
```

where $`\mathbf{W}_{\lx@text@underscore}p\in\mathbb{R}^{D\times U}`$ and $`\mathbf{b}_{\lx@text@underscore}p\in\mathbb{R}^{U}`$ are learnable parameters, $`D`$ is the dimension of the lip feature vector $`\mathbf{e}_{\lx@text@underscore}t`$, and $`U=33`$ is the output vocabulary size (Peng et al., [2023a](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.01627v3#bib.bib40)). The predicted probability vector $`\mathbf{p}_{\lx@text@underscore}t`$ at each time step is then used in the CTC loss to account for all possible alignments between lip features and the target transcription.

### 3.1. Mamba Layer

The Scan module (S6) within Mamba (Gu and Dao, [2023](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.01627v3#bib.bib17)) is critical in capturing temporal patterns and dependencies in multiple time steps. This module applies a set of trainable operations to each segment of the input sequence, learning key temporal features during training. The parameters governing these operations are optimized to handle long-range dependencies, which makes Mamba particularly effective for generating facial animation, where audiovisual synchronization, fine-grained motion dynamics, and long contextual dependencies must be captured over extended sequences. Figure [2](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.01627v3#S3.F2 "Figure 2 ‣ 3.1. Mamba Layer ‣ 3. Proposed Method ‣ JambaTalk: Speech-driven 3D Talking Head Generation based on a Hybrid Transformer-Mamba Model") illustrates the architecture of the Mamba Layer, which consists of two sequential sub-blocks connected by residual pathways. The input first passes through an RMSNorm layer for normalization, followed by a Mamba block that captures long-range dependencies via state-space modeling. The output of the Mamba block is then added to the original input through a residual connection, enhancing gradient flow and stability. This intermediate result undergoes a second RMSNorm and then flows into a standard MLP (multi-layer perceptron) to further transform the features. A second residual connection adds the MLP output back to the intermediate features, forming the final output. The diagram visualizes this modular structure, where normalization, sequence modeling, and feedforward processing are interleaved with skip connections to facilitate deep learning and efficient representation learning.

Mamba improves upon prior state space models (Gu et al., [2022](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.01627v3#bib.bib18)) like Structured State Space Model (S4) (Nguyen et al., [2022](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.01627v3#bib.bib32)) by introducing a selective mechanism that dynamically generates the state parameters $`A`$, $`B`$, and $`C`$ from the input sequence. This dynamic generation is realized through fully connected layers that take the current input and produce the parameters as time-varying functions rather than fixed matrices, enabling adaptive modeling of various motion and speech patterns. Mamba employs the parallel scan algorithm, which leverages the associativity of operations like matrix multiplication to split the input into chunks, process them in parallel, and then merge the results efficiently, achieving parallelism comparable to convolution. The resulting Selective Scan combines the adaptability of dynamic recurrence with the speed of parallel computation. It selectively updates internal memory states based on input, enabling long-range reasoning with input-awareness similar to attention mechanisms, but without their high computational cost. For each batch and dimension, the model processes the input $`x_{\lx@text@underscore}t`$, the hidden state $`h_{\lx@text@underscore}t`$, and output $`y_{\lx@text@underscore}t`$ at each time step $`t`$. The formulation of the model is as follows:

```math
\begin{matrix}h_{\lx@text@underscore}t=\hat{A}_{\lx@text@underscore}th_{\lx@text@underscore}{t-1}+\hat{B}_{\lx@text@underscore}tx_{\lx@text@underscore}t,\\
y_{\lx@text@underscore}t=C_{\lx@text@underscore}th_{\lx@text@underscore}t,\end{matrix}
```

where $`\hat{A}_{\lx@text@underscore}t`$, $`\hat{B}_{\lx@text@underscore}t`$, and $`C_{\lx@text@underscore}t`$ are matrices and vectors that are updated at each time step, enabling the model to adjust to the temporal dynamics of the input sequence. To discretize the continuous-time dynamics, a sampling interval $`\Delta`$ is introduced, and the matrices $`\hat{A}_{\lx@text@underscore}t`$ and $`\hat{B}_{\lx@text@underscore}t`$ are computed as follows:

```math
\begin{matrix}\hat{A}_{\lx@text@underscore}t=exp(\Delta A_{\lx@text@underscore}t),\\
\hat{B}_{\lx@text@underscore}t=(\Delta A_{\lx@text@underscore}t)^{-1}(exp(\Delta A_{\lx@text@underscore}t)-I).\Delta B_{\lx@text@underscore}t,\end{matrix}
```

where $`A_{\lx@text@underscore}t`$ and $`B_{\lx@text@underscore}t`$ are generated by the input-dependent projection layers, and $`I`$ is the identity matrix. This approach allows the model to handle variable-length sequences and complex temporal behaviors efficiently (Xu et al., [2024c](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.01627v3#bib.bib72)).

Modern GPUs excel at computation but are bottlenecked by the slow transfer of data between small, fast SRAM (close to the processor) and large, slow DRAM (farther away). Deep learning models that repeatedly write intermediate results to DRAM and read them back suffer major slowdowns. Mamba tackles this hardware inefficiency with three key optimizations. First, Kernel Fusion combines multiple sequential operations, such as discretization, selective scanning, and matrix multiplication, into a single fused operation that runs entirely within SRAM, avoiding costly DRAM access. Second, during training, Mamba uses Recomputation, choosing to recalculate intermediate values when needed for backpropagation instead of saving them, which saves time by avoiding DRAM reads. Finally, these techniques are combined with Mamba's dynamic input-sensitive architecture in what are called Selective State Space Models (S6), allowing efficient long-sequence modeling with Transformer-level performance, but significantly reduced memory and compute overhead.

JambaTalk places Mamba layers both before and after the Transformer blocks to capture both local dependencies (via attention) and long-range structures (via SSM). This hybrid layering helps model detailed lip movements and head pose shifts over extended sequences, which are essential for generating high-quality 3D talking head animation.

### 3.2. MoE_Mamba Layer

JambaTalk employs MoE*Mamba layers to optimize computational efficiency (Lieber et al., [2024](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.01627v3#bib.bib29)). Instead of using all experts in the network for each facial motion, JambaTalk selectively routes data to a subset of experts using a dynamic routing mechanism. The MoE_Mamba Layer integrates a Mamba block with a Mixture-of-Experts (MoE) (Fedus et al., [2022](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.01627v3#bib.bib14)) mechanism to efficiently model long-range dependencies, while dynamically scaling model capacity as depicted in Figure [2](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.01627v3#S3.F2 "Figure 2 ‣ 3.1. Mamba Layer ‣ 3. Proposed Method ‣ JambaTalk: Speech-driven 3D Talking Head Generation based on a Hybrid Transformer-Mamba Model"). First, the input sequence is normalized using RMSNorm and passed through a Mamba block, which captures temporal patterns through a recurrent formulation with learnable convolution and state parameters. A residual connection adds stability by combining the Mamba output with the original input. The result is then normalized again and routed through an MoE layer, where each token is processed by a small, learned subset of experts, enabling the model to specialize across different input patterns. The final output combines the MoE result with the original input via another residual connection, allowing the layer to leverage both sequence modeling and expert specialization in a computationally efficient manner. The MoE routing mechanism operates as follows: given an input $`x`$, the router computes logits $`h*{\lx@text@underscore}{x}=W\_{\lx@text@underscore}{r.x}`$, which are converted to gating probabilities via a softmax:

```math
p_{\lx@text@underscore}i(x)=\frac{e^{h(x)_{\lx@text@underscore}i}}{\sum_{\lx@text@underscore}j^{N}e^{h(x)_{\lx@text@underscore}j}}.
```

The top-$`k`$ gate values are chosen to route the token $`x`$. If $`\tau`$ is the set of selected top-$`k`$ indices, the layer's output is computed as a linearly weighted combination of each expert's computation on the token, weighted by the gate values:

```math
y=\sum_{\lx@text@underscore}{i\in\tau}p_{\lx@text@underscore}i(x)E_{\lx@text@underscore}i(x).
```

This approach enables efficient utilization of experts, as only the most relevant ones are engaged for each input. It also preserves model capacity by leveraging the diversity of experts to specialize in different tasks or aspects of the input data.

### 3.3. Transformer Layer

#### Low-Rank Learned Rotary Positional Embedding.

In our experiments, Low-Rank Learned Rotary Positional Embedding (LRL-RoPE), an advanced positional embedding technique, is utilized to improve generalization ability for longer sequences. The original RoPE encodes absolute positional information using a rotation matrix, thereby directly integrating relative position dependency into the self-attention mechanism. The method provides several benefits, including adaptability to sequences of varying lengths, a gradual reduction in token inter-dependency as relative distances increase, and incorporating relative position encoding into linear self-attention.

Given an input vector $`x_{\lx@text@underscore}i\in\mathbb{R}^{d}`$, where $`d`$ is even, instead of applying a rotation to the entire $`d`$-dimensional vector at once, the space is divided into $`d/2`$ separate two-dimensional subspaces. Each subspace corresponds to a pair of dimensions, such as $`(x_{\lx@text@underscore}1,x_{\lx@text@underscore}2),(x_{\lx@text@underscore}3,x_{\lx@text@underscore}4),\ldots`$, and a rotation is applied independently within each pair. As a result, the overall $`d\times d`$ rotary matrix $`R_{\lx@text@underscore}{\Theta,t}^{d}`$ is constructed as a block diagonal matrix, where each block is a $`2\times 2`$ rotation matrix responsible for rotating the corresponding two-dimensional subspace. The rotary matrix applied at position $`t`$ is:

```math
R_{\lx@text@underscore}{\theta,t}^{d}=\begin{pmatrix}cos(t\theta_{\lx@text@underscore}1)&-sin(t\theta_{\lx@text@underscore}1)&0&0&\dots&0&0\\
sin(t\theta_{\lx@text@underscore}1)&cos(t\theta_{\lx@text@underscore}1)&0\\
0&0&cos(t\theta_{\lx@text@underscore}2)&-sin(t\theta_{\lx@text@underscore}2)&\dots&0&0\\
0&0&sin(t\theta_{\lx@text@underscore}2)&cos(t\theta_{\lx@text@underscore}2)&\dots&0&0\\
\vdots&\vdots&\vdots&\vdots&\ddots&\vdots&\vdots\\
0&0&0&0&\dots&cos(t\theta_{\lx@text@underscore}{d/2})&-sin(t\theta_{\lx@text@underscore}{d/2})\\
0&0&0&0&\dots&sin(t\theta_{\lx@text@underscore}{d/2})&cos(t\theta_{\lx@text@underscore}{d/2})\\
\end{pmatrix}.
```

The rotation frequency parameters are defined as:

```math
\theta_{\lx@text@underscore}i=10000^{-2(i-1)/d},i\in[1,2,...,d/2].
```

Different rotation speeds (frequencies) are assigned to each $`2D`$ subspace, enabling the model to capture both short- and long-range positional information. Each input vector $`x_{\lx@text@underscore}t`$ at position $`t`$ is first transformed into two different representations called query and key by multiplying with learned projection matrices $`W_{\lx@text@underscore}q`$ and $`W_{\lx@text@underscore}k`$, respectively. These matrices are trained during model learning to map the input features into spaces suitable for attention computation. Next, the Rotary Positional Embedding (RoPE) is applied by multiplying these query and key vectors with a position-dependent rotation matrix $`R_{\lx@text@underscore}{\Theta,t}^{d}`$ for queries and $`R_{\lx@text@underscore}{\Theta,\acute{t}}^{d}`$ for keys. This rotation matrix encodes positional information by rotating each $`2D`$ subspace of the vector based on the token position and predefined frequencies, effectively injecting relative position information into the vectors:

```math
q_{\lx@text@underscore}t=R_{\lx@text@underscore}{\Theta,t}^{d}W_{\lx@text@underscore}qx_{\lx@text@underscore}t,\quad k_{\lx@text@underscore}{\acute{t}}=R_{\lx@text@underscore}{\Theta,\acute{t}}^{d}W_{\lx@text@underscore}kx_{\lx@text@underscore}{\acute{t}},
```

where $`R_{\lx@text@underscore}{\Theta,t}^{d}`$ and $`R_{\lx@text@underscore}{\Theta,\acute{t}}^{d}`$ are rotary matrices applied at positions $`t`$ and $`\acute{t}`$, encoding positional information through rotations. The self-attention dot product between query at position $`t`$ and and key at position $`\acute{t}`$ is:

```math
q_{\lx@text@underscore}t^{\top}k_{\lx@text@underscore}{\acute{t}}=\left(R_{\lx@text@underscore}{\Theta,t}^{d}W_{\lx@text@underscore}qx_{\lx@text@underscore}t\right)^{\top}\left(R_{\lx@text@underscore}{\Theta,\acute{t}}^{d}W_{\lx@text@underscore}kx_{\lx@text@underscore}{\acute{t}}\right).
```

Since the rotary matrices $`R_{\lx@text@underscore}{\Theta,t}^{d}`$ are orthogonal, their transpose equals their inverse:

```math
\left(R_{\lx@text@underscore}{\Theta,t}^{d}\right)^{\top}=\left(R_{\lx@text@underscore}{\Theta,t}^{d}\right)^{-1}.
```

Therefore, the dot product simplifies to:

```math
q_{\lx@text@underscore}t^{\top}k_{\lx@text@underscore}{t^{\prime}}=x_{\lx@text@underscore}t^{\top}W_{\lx@text@underscore}q^{\top}R_{\lx@text@underscore}{\Theta,t^{\prime}-t}^{d}W_{\lx@text@underscore}kx_{\lx@text@underscore}{t^{\prime}},
```

where $`R_{\lx@text@underscore}{\Theta,t^{\prime}-t}^{d}=(R_{\lx@text@underscore}{\Theta,t}^{d})^{\top}R_{\lx@text@underscore}{\Theta,t^{\prime}}^{d}`$ incorporates relative positional information through the matrix $`R_{\lx@text@underscore}{\Theta,\acute{t}-t}^{d}`$ depending only on the position difference $`\acute{t}-t`$. This multiplicative encoding preserves stability and allows the model to capture positional relationships more naturally compared to additive embeddings (Su et al., [2024](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.01627v3#bib.bib55)).

In the standard Rotary Position Embedding (RoPE) formulation of ([11](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.01627v3#S3.E11 "In Low-Rank Learned Rotary Positional Embedding. ‣ 3.3. Transformer Layer ‣ 3. Proposed Method ‣ JambaTalk: Speech-driven 3D Talking Head Generation based on a Hybrid Transformer-Mamba Model")), the positional encoding for the $`t`$-th position is defined as a fixed sinusoidal vector, where $`\theta_{\lx@text@underscore}i`$ represents the pre-defined angular frequency for the $`i`$-th channel. These angles are calculated using a closed-form formula and are not updated during training, which could restrict the model's ability to adapt to particular tasks or domains. To address this limitation, we introduce a low-rank learnable correction to the fixed RoPE angles. The approach applies a bottleneck projection to $`R_{\lx@text@underscore}{\theta,t}^{d}`$, reducing it to a low-dimensional representation:

```math
h_{\lx@text@underscore}t=W_{\lx@text@underscore}1^{\top}R_{\lx@text@underscore}{\theta,t}^{d},\quad W_{\lx@text@underscore}1\in\mathbb{R}^{d\times r},\quad r\ll d,
```

Then, mapping back to the original dimension:

```math
\Delta_{\lx@text@underscore}t=W_{\lx@text@underscore}2^{\top}h_{\lx@text@underscore}t,\quad W_{\lx@text@underscore}2\in\mathbb{R}^{r\times d}.
```

In these last equations, $`W_{\lx@text@underscore}1\in\mathbb{R}^{d\times r}`$ is a learnable matrix whose transpose, $`W_{\lx@text@underscore}1^{\top}`$, projects the original $`d`$-dimensional vector into an $`r`$-dimensional bottleneck, $`h_{\lx@text@underscore}t`$, where $`r\ll d`$. This low-dimensional representation captures the most important features of the positional embedding, reducing the number of parameters and computational cost while allowing learning. Similarly, $`W_{\lx@text@underscore}2\in\mathbb{R}^{r\times d}`$ is a learnable matrix whose transpose, $`W_{\lx@text@underscore}2^{\top}`$, maps $`h_{\lx@text@underscore}t`$ back to the original $`d`$-dimensional space, producing the final transformed positional embedding $`\Delta_{\lx@text@underscore}t\in\mathbb{R}^{d}`$. The two matrices $`W_{\lx@text@underscore}1`$ and $`W_{\lx@text@underscore}2`$ together form a low-rank approximation of a learnable transformation, which makes the positional embeddings more expressive and compatible with downstream layers while keeping the original dimensionality.

The resulting vector $`\Delta_{\lx@text@underscore}t`$ is divided into two halves, $`\Delta\sin_{\lx@text@underscore}t\in\mathbb{R}^{d/2}`$ and $`\Delta\cos_{\lx@text@underscore}t\in\mathbb{R}^{d/2}`$, which serve as learnable offsets for the trigonometric components:

$`\displaystyle\sin_{\lx@text@underscore}{\text{final},t}`$$`\displaystyle=\sin(t\theta_{\lx@text@underscore}d)+\Delta\sin_{\lx@text@underscore}t,`$$`\displaystyle\cos_{\lx@text@underscore}{\text{final},t}`$$`\displaystyle=\cos(t\theta_{\lx@text@underscore}d)+\Delta\cos_{\lx@text@underscore}t.`$

The rotary transformation is then applied to each 2$`d`$ subspace of the token vector $`x_{\lx@text@underscore}t\in\mathbb{R}^{d}`$:

```math
\begin{matrix}x^{\prime}_{\lx@text@underscore}{t,2i}=x_{\lx@text@underscore}{t,2i}\cdot\cos_{\lx@text@underscore}{\text{final},t,i}-x_{\lx@text@underscore}{t,2i+1}\cdot\sin_{\lx@text@underscore}{\text{final},t,i},\\
x^{\prime}_{\lx@text@underscore}{t,2i+1}=x_{\lx@text@underscore}{t,2i}\cdot\sin_{\lx@text@underscore}{\text{final},t,i}+x_{\lx@text@underscore}{t,2i+1}\cdot\cos_{\lx@text@underscore}{\text{final},t,i}.\end{matrix}
```

This formulation preserves the RoPE's ability to encode relative positions, while allowing task-specific rotation adjustments. The low-rank factorization constrains the additional parameters to $`O(dr)`$, providing flexibility at minimal computational cost. The complete mechanism is summarized in Algorithm [1](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.01627v3#alg1 "Algorithm 1 ‣ Low-Rank Learned Rotary Positional Embedding. ‣ 3.3. Transformer Layer ‣ 3. Proposed Method ‣ JambaTalk: Speech-driven 3D Talking Head Generation based on a Hybrid Transformer-Mamba Model").

#### Grouped Query Attention.

JambaTalk replaces Multi-head Attention (MHA) (Ashish, [2017](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.01627v3#bib.bib4); Shazeer, [2019](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.01627v3#bib.bib48)) with Grouped-query Attention (GQA) (Ainslie et al., [2023](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.01627v3#bib.bib2)) in the Transformer block, reducing the number of key and value heads to a more manageable number, which in turn decreases both the size of the key-value cache and the overall amount of data processed during attention calculations. This reduction leads to faster training and inference times, while maintaining performance quality close to that of MHA.

## 4. Experiments and Results

### 4.1. Experiments Setting

#### $`\bm{BIWI}`$ Dataset.

The $`BIWI`$ dataset (Fanelli et al., [2010](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.01627v3#bib.bib13)) includes 14 subjects, each tasked with reciting 40 distinct sentences, captured at a frame rate of 25 frames per second. Each sentence is about 5 seconds, and the 3D facial meshes are aligned to a uniform topology to ensure consistency across subjects. The $`BIWI_{\lx@text@underscore}6`$ (Nocentini et al., [2024a](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.01627v3#bib.bib34)) is a reduced dataset that holds a fixed topology with 3,895 vertices and 7,539 faces, while preserving sufficient detail for facial motion analysis. Utilizing this dataset enables more efficient processing without significantly compromising the accuracy of the models, making it well-suited for experiments, where computational resources are a limiting factor. The data set is divided into several subsets: a training set ($`BIWI_{\lx@text@underscore}6`$-Train) comprising 191 sentences spoken by six subjects (32 sentences per subject) and a validation set ($`BIWI_{\lx@text@underscore}6`$-Val) consisting of 24 sentences from the same six subjects (4 sentences each).

#### Vocaset Dataset.

We employed the publicly accessible 3D dataset, VOCASET (Cudeiro et al., [2019](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.01627v3#bib.bib9)), for training and testing. This dataset includes 480 speech sequences derived from 3D face scans, showcasing the 12 identities found in the CoMA dataset (Ranjan, [2018](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.01627v3#bib.bib43)). Each 3D face mesh comprises 5,023 vertices. To provide a fair comparison, we employ the same training, validation, and testing splits as VOCA (Cudeiro et al., [2019](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.01627v3#bib.bib9)), referred to as VOCA-Train, VOCA-Val, and VOCA-Test, respectively. The dataset provides paired audio and 3D facial scans of English utterances. VOCASET includes 255 unique sentences, with some sentences shared across different speakers.

#### Baseline Methods.

We compare JambaTalk with four state-of-the-art methods, FaceFormer (Fan et al., [2022](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.01627v3#bib.bib12)), CodeTalker (Xing et al., [2023](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.01627v3#bib.bib68)), FaceDiffuser (Stan et al., [2023](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.01627v3#bib.bib52)), and SelfTalk (Peng et al., [2023a](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.01627v3#bib.bib40)). We employ the official implementations of these models for training and testing on the Vocaset and $`BIWI_{\lx@text@underscore}6`$ datasets.

#### Implementation Details.

We used the Adam optimizer with a learning rate of $`10^{-4}`$ for Vocaset, and $`10^{-5}`$ for $`BIWI_{\lx@text@underscore}6`$. All model training was conducted on a Linux system with an ASUS NVIDIA GeForce RTX 4090 GPU, and the procedure took approximately 90 minutes (100 epochs) to complete. The encoder parameters are initialized with pre-trained Wav2Vec 2.0 weights. During training, only the TCN parameters remain fixed.

### 4.2. Quantitative Comparison

Following FaceFormer (Fan et al., [2022](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.01627v3#bib.bib12)), CodeTalker (Xing et al., [2023](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.01627v3#bib.bib68)), FaceDiffuser (Stan et al., [2023](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.01627v3#bib.bib52)), and SelfTalk (Peng et al., [2023a](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.01627v3#bib.bib40)), we utilize the Mean Vertex Error (MVE) to measure 3D facial mesh reconstruction accuracy over time, the Lip Vertex Error (LVE) to assess lip synchronization, and upper-face motion statistics to evaluate overall facial dynamics, as publicly available metrics to evaluate speech-driven facial animation. Additionally, inspired by ARTalk (Chu et al., [2025](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.01627v3#bib.bib8)) and StyleSpeaker (Yang et al., [2025](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.01627v3#bib.bib73)), we employ the Fourier Frequency Error (FFE) to analyze the frequency domain characteristics of motion, and the Motion Offset Deviation (MOE) to assess the smoothness of frame-to-frame transitions. To quantify changes in motion speed, we further use the Acceleration Error (AE).

#### Mean Vertex Error

The Mean Vertex Error (MVE) measures the average Euclidean distance between predicted and ground-truth 3D facial vertices across all frames in a sequence. It serves as an indicator of how accurately the model reconstructs the 3D facial mesh over time. A lower MVE value signifies better performance, with values closer to zero indicating that the predicted mesh aligns closely with the true facial geometry. This metric is widely used to assess the overall quality and precision of 3D face reconstruction models (Stan et al., [2023](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.01627v3#bib.bib52)).

#### Lip Vertex Error

This metric measures the deviation of the lips in a sequence compared to the ground-truth by computing the maximum error $`L2`$ of all the lip vertices for each frame and then averaging these values across all frames (Fan et al., [2022](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.01627v3#bib.bib12); Xing et al., [2023](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.01627v3#bib.bib68); Peng et al., [2023a](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.01627v3#bib.bib40)).

#### Upper-Face Dynamics Deviation

The Upper-Face Dynamics Deviation (FDD) is only loosely related to speech, as it can vary based on individual speaking styles and the content of the speech. To address this, we measure the variation in facial dynamics of a motion sequence relative to the ground-truth (Xing et al., [2023](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.01627v3#bib.bib68)).

#### Fourier Frequency Error

In motion synthesis tasks, assessing the accuracy of generated movements is relatively straightforward; however, measuring style consistency remains challenging due to the absence of a universally accepted quantitative metric. Although earlier studies commonly rely on FDD to evaluate style, this approach may fail to capture the unique motion characteristics of different speakers. The Frequency Feature Error (FFE) addresses this limitation by analyzing the frequency domain representation of motion, offering a more precise measure of how well the synthesized motion's rhythmic and frequency patterns align with those of the reference (Yang et al., [2025](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.01627v3#bib.bib73)).

#### Motion Offset Deviation

The Motion Offset Deviation (MOD) evaluates how closely the frame-to-frame transitions in the predicted motion sequence align with those in the ground-truth, effectively capturing the temporal accuracy of the animation. A lower MOD indicates more natural and well-timed motions (Chu et al., [2025](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.01627v3#bib.bib8)).

#### Acceleration Error

The Acceleration Error (AE) quantifies the discrepancy in motion acceleration between predicted and ground-truth sequences, providing a measure of temporal smoothness and realism in facial animation. Let $`\hat{V}_{\lx@text@underscore}t`$ and $`V_{\lx@text@underscore}t`$ denote the predicted and ground-truth vertex positions at time $`t`$, respectively. The velocity is computed as the first-order difference: $`\Delta\hat{V}_{\lx@text@underscore}t=\hat{V}_{\lx@text@underscore}t-\hat{V}_{\lx@text@underscore}{t-1}`$ and $`\Delta V_{\lx@text@underscore}t=V_{\lx@text@underscore}t-V_{\lx@text@underscore}{t-1}`$. The acceleration is then estimated using the second-order difference: $`\hat{a}_{\lx@text@underscore}t=\Delta\hat{V}_{\lx@text@underscore}t-\Delta\hat{V}_{\lx@text@underscore}{t-1}`$ and $`a_{\lx@text@underscore}t=\Delta V_{\lx@text@underscore}t-\Delta V_{\lx@text@underscore}{t-1}`$. The Acceleration Error is defined as the mean L2 distance between predicted and ground-truth accelerations over all valid time steps:

```math
AE=\frac{1}{T-2}\sum_{\lx@text@underscore}{t=2}^{T-1}|\hat{a}_{\lx@text@underscore}t-a_{\lx@text@underscore}t|_{\lx@text@underscore}2,
```

where $`T`$ is the total number of frames.

#### Discussion

We compare FaceFormer (Fan et al., [2022](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.01627v3#bib.bib12)), CodeTalker (Xing et al., [2023](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.01627v3#bib.bib68)), FaceDiffuser (Stan et al., [2023](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.01627v3#bib.bib52)), SelfTalk (Peng et al., [2023a](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.01627v3#bib.bib40)), and our proposed model, JambaTalk. Evaluation is conducted using six complementary metrics: Mean Vertex Error (MVE), Lip Vertex Error (LVE), Upper-Face Dynamics Deviation (FDD), Fourier Frequency Error (FFE), Motion Offset Deviation (MOD), and Acceleration Error (AE). These metrics capture accuracy, synchronization, dynamic consistency, frequency characteristics, temporal smoothness, and motion realism. This points to complementary roles: MoE modules capture expressive variation, whereas Mamba layers stabilize temporal consistency. The Lip Reading module and Velocity Loss each provide smaller yet consistent benefits, with their removal increasing errors across most metrics, indicating their complementary roles in improving articulation accuracy and temporal smoothness.

We did not utilize both the motion prior and style embedding, as well as the lip-reading module, simultaneously because the lip-reading module alone provides strong phoneme-to-motion alignment, which overlaps with some of the temporal dynamics captured by the motion prior. Combining both would increase model complexity and the number of parameters, potentially leading to overfitting or optimization difficulties, especially given the limited size of available datasets. ## 6. Runtime Efficiency Analysis

We have implemented a real-time, speech-driven 3D talking face generation pipeline that efficiently handles both microphone and audio file inputs. Audio signals are streamed into a queue in small chunks, enabling continuous processing without blocking the user interface. Each chunk is converted into features using a Wav2Vec2-based processor and cast to half precision (float16) to optimize GPU memory usage. The model processes these features in overlapping windows, allowing low-latency inference while maintaining temporal continuity. This approach supports high-throughput frame generation, with each audio window producing the corresponding 3D mesh vertices for the talking head.

To render the generated sequences, a dedicated off-screen 3D renderer updates the mesh in real time, providing smooth and interactive visualization. Mesh predictions are placed into a secondary queue consumed by the rendering thread, ensuring synchronization between inference and display. This design enables efficient pipelining, allowing JambaTalk to achieve real-time performance on standard GPUs (e.g., NVIDIA RTX 4090) with low latency and significant speedup over real-time audio playback. The system can generate infinite-length sequences using a sliding-window approach. Runtime performance metrics are summarized in Table [7](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.01627v3#S5.T7 "Table 7 ‣ 5. We conducted a user study using Amazon Mechanical Turk services to evaluate the quality of animated faces in terms of perceptual lip synchronization and realism, comparing our method with FaceFormer (Fan et al., [2022](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.01627v3#bib.bib12)), CodeTalker (Xing et al., [2023](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.01627v3#bib.bib68)), FaceDiffuser (Stan et al., [2023](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.01627v3#bib.bib52)), and SelfTalk (Peng et al., [2023a](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.01627v3#bib.bib40)). A user study is a reliable method for evaluating 3D talking faces, so we employed A/B tests for each comparison, pitting our method against competitors in terms of realistic facial animation and lip synchronization (Figure [8](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.01627v3#S7.F8 "Figure 8 ‣ 7. We calculated the ratio of user choices to measure satisfaction. As illustrated in Table [8](https://arxiv.org/html/2408.01627v3#S7.T8 "Table 8 ‣ 7. In particular, $`86.4\%`$ of the users preferred our realism and $`67.8\%`$ favored our lip synchronization on the Vocaset dataset compared to CodeTalker.

## 8. Conclusions

In this work, JambaTalk demonstrated significant progress in predicting facial motion by combining the most acceptable elements of Transformer and Mamba architectures using a mixture-of-experts strategy for speech-driven 3D facial animation. This hybrid design delivered high throughput, efficient memory utilization, and the capacity to manage extensive contexts. This makes our approach a flexible and potent model for a wide range of talking head generation tasks. The quantitative analysis illustrated that our approach outperforms other state-of-the-art methods. However, the overall perceptual quality remains behind the ground-truth, mainly due to the limited availability of paired audio-visual data. In addition, the generic motion prior obtained conforms to the motion patterns defined by the training set, which may differ from actual facial movements in the real world. Although most systems today depend on audio or text prompts, exploring control through modalities such as gestures, gaze, and emotions presents an exciting area for future research.
