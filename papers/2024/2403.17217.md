# DiffusionAct: Controllable Diffusion Autoencoder for One-shot Face Reenactment

Stella Bounareli¹, Christos Tzelepis², Vasileios Argyriou¹, Ioannis Patras³,\
Georgios Tzimiropoulos³\
¹ School of Computer Science and Mathematics, Kingston University London ² School of Science and Technology, City University of London ³ School of Electronic Engineering and Computer Science, Queen Mary University of London

###### Abstract

Video-driven neural face reenactment aims to synthesize realistic facial images that successfully preserve the identity and appearance of a source face, while transferring the target head pose and facial expressions. Existing GAN-based methods suffer from either distortions and visual artifacts or poor reconstruction quality, i.e., the background and several important appearance details, such as hair style/color, glasses and accessories, are not faithfully reconstructed. Recent advances in Diffusion Probabilistic Models (DPMs) enable the generation of high-quality realistic images. To this end, in this paper we present DiffusionAct, a novel method that leverages the photo-realistic image generation of diffusion models to perform neural face reenactment. Specifically, we propose to control the semantic space of a Diffusion Autoencoder (DiffAE), in order to edit the facial pose of the input images, defined as the head pose orientation and the facial expressions. Our method allows one-shot, self, and cross-subject reenactment, without requiring subject-specific fine-tuning. We compare against state-of-the-art GAN-, StyleGAN2-, and diffusion-based methods, showing better or on-par reenactment performance. ^(†)^(†)Project page: [https://stelabou.github.io/diffusionact/](https://stelabou.github.io/diffusionact/)

## 1 Introduction

Neural face reenactment aims to generate photo-realistic head avatars of a source subject. That is, given a source facial image, the goal of face reenactment is to animate it using either audio driving signals \[[45](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.17217v2#bib.bib45), [17](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.17217v2#bib.bib17), [19](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.17217v2#bib.bib19), [51](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.17217v2#bib.bib51), [54](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.17217v2#bib.bib54)\] or video driving sequences \[[71](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.17217v2#bib.bib71), [61](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.17217v2#bib.bib61), [36](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.17217v2#bib.bib36), [4](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.17217v2#bib.bib4)\] from the same (self reenactment), or different subjects (cross-subject reenactment), as shown in Fig. [1](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.17217v2#S0.F1 "Figure 1 ‣ DiffusionAct: Controllable Diffusion Autoencoder for One-shot Face Reenactment"). In doing so, the appearance and the identity characteristics of the source face should be preserved, while the target facial pose (which in the literature of neural face reenactment is typically defined as the head pose orientation and the facial expressions \[[7](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.17217v2#bib.bib7), [71](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.17217v2#bib.bib71), [4](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.17217v2#bib.bib4)\]) is accurately transferred to the reenacted face. Neural face reenactment has recently drawn significant attention both from the research community and the industry, due to its wide range of applications, e.g. in entertainment, creative arts, video conferencing and video production.

The advent of Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs) \[[18](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.17217v2#bib.bib18), [27](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.17217v2#bib.bib27), [28](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.17217v2#bib.bib28)\] and Diffusion Probabilistic Models (DPMs) \[[21](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.17217v2#bib.bib21), [53](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.17217v2#bib.bib53), [49](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.17217v2#bib.bib49)\] has led to impressive performance in the tasks of image synthesis and manipulation. Such methods are able to produce realistic and aesthetically pleasing synthetic images, but also provide controllability over the generative process \[[59](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.17217v2#bib.bib59), [57](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.17217v2#bib.bib57), [39](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.17217v2#bib.bib39), [58](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.17217v2#bib.bib58), [43](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.17217v2#bib.bib43), [24](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.17217v2#bib.bib24), [8](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.17217v2#bib.bib8)\]. Whilst recent generative methods have been extensively used for face reenactment \[[71](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.17217v2#bib.bib71), [7](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.17217v2#bib.bib7), [52](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.17217v2#bib.bib52)\], it remains a remarkably challenging task due to the wide range of variations of the human faces, different facial shapes, appearances and expressions, head pose movements and lighting conditions. A certain line of research proposes training controllable GAN models \[[71](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.17217v2#bib.bib71), [14](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.17217v2#bib.bib14), [36](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.17217v2#bib.bib36), [47](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.17217v2#bib.bib47)\]. However, training such models often leads to deformations and visual artifacts on the generated images. As shown in Fig. [1](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.17217v2#S0.F1 "Figure 1 ‣ DiffusionAct: Controllable Diffusion Autoencoder for One-shot Face Reenactment"), GAN-based methods \[[22](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.17217v2#bib.bib22), [65](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.17217v2#bib.bib65), [42](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.17217v2#bib.bib42)\] often fail to produce realistic images under head pose movements between the source and target faces. Furthermore, learning disentangled representations of identity/appearance and facial pose, which is of paramount importance for face reenactment, is a non trivial task. Failure in achieving disentanglement can lead to identity leakage from the driving faces to the source ones \[[71](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.17217v2#bib.bib71), [70](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.17217v2#bib.bib70)\]. To overcome the above challenges, recent methods \[[3](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.17217v2#bib.bib3), [67](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.17217v2#bib.bib67), [4](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.17217v2#bib.bib4), [40](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.17217v2#bib.bib40)\] focus on leveraging the photo-realistic generative ability and the disentangled properties of pre-trained StyleGAN2 models \[[29](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.17217v2#bib.bib29)\]. Nevertheless, a significant limitation of these approaches is that the encoding of real images onto StyleGAN2 space remains a challenge, leading usually to unfaithful reconstruction and poor reenactment. As illustrated in Fig. [1](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.17217v2#S0.F1 "Figure 1 ‣ DiffusionAct: Controllable Diffusion Autoencoder for One-shot Face Reenactment"), the StyleGAN2-based method of HyperReenact \[[4](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.17217v2#bib.bib4)\] fails to precisely reconstruct appearance details, such as hair styles and glasses, that are crucial to maintain the overall perceptual quality of the reenacted images. Recent works incorporate DPMs for the task of audio-driven talking head sequences generation \[[51](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.17217v2#bib.bib51), [54](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.17217v2#bib.bib54), [15](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.17217v2#bib.bib15)\]. However, these methods mainly focus on editing the mouth region. In contrast, DiffusionRig \[[13](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.17217v2#bib.bib13)\] enables editing the head pose and expression of input images, however, it requires subject-specific fine-tuning with around 20 images to reconstruct the source identity characteristics, limiting its feasibility. Finally, IP-Adapter \[[66](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.17217v2#bib.bib66)\] and InstantID \[[60](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.17217v2#bib.bib60)\] condition Stable Diffusion (SD) models \[[49](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.17217v2#bib.bib49)\] to generate controllable images. Nevertheless, such methods are not able to preserve both the identity and appearance of input faces, making them impractical for the task of face reenactment.

In this work, we propose a novel neural face reenactment framework, called DiffusionAct, which incorporates the generative power of a pre-trained Denoising Diffusion Implicit Model (DDIM) \[[53](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.17217v2#bib.bib53)\] and the remarkable reconstruction performance of a Diffusion AutoEncoder (DiffAE) \[[46](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.17217v2#bib.bib46)\]. The latter learns a semantic encoder that maps input images into meaningful semantic codes, which then are decoded by a standard DDIM sampler, allowing for image editing and, thus, can be employed for face reenactment. In contrast to StyleGAN2-based methods \[[3](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.17217v2#bib.bib3), [67](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.17217v2#bib.bib67), [4](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.17217v2#bib.bib4)\], that typically suffer from the reconstruction-editability trade-off \[[55](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.17217v2#bib.bib55)\], our method achieves effective face reenactment performance relying on the near-perfect reconstruction capability of DiffAE. Furthermore, our method requires a single frame from the source face, in contrast to other GAN- or DPM-based methods \[[7](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.17217v2#bib.bib7), [23](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.17217v2#bib.bib23), [13](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.17217v2#bib.bib13)\]. Concretely, we devise a conditioning mechanism on the semantic encoder of DiffAE, which we call reenactment encoder, that enables the transfer of the target facial pose into the reenacted image. More specifically, we optimise our reenactment encoder to encode in its semantic space the appearance/identity characteristics of the source face, and the target facial pose, given in terms of informative facial landmarks. The resulting “reenacted” code is then decoded by DDIM to generate the reenacted face.

The main contributions of this paper can be summarized as follows:

1.  1.  We introduce DiffusionAct, a novel approach for face reenactment based on a pre-trained diffusion autoencoder model \[[46](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.17217v2#bib.bib46)\]. To the best of our knowledge, our work is the first that uses a pre-trained DPM and adapts it to the face reenactment task.
2.  2.  Inspired by ControlNet \[[72](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.17217v2#bib.bib72)\], we propose a conditioning mechanism on a pre-trained semantic encoder to enable the generation of controllable images using the target facial landmarks. In contrast to ControlNet \[[72](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.17217v2#bib.bib72)\], that requires the costly optimisation of the DPM’s UNet \[[50](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.17217v2#bib.bib50)\], our method optimises only the reenactment encoder.
3.  3.  We report extensive quantitative and qualitative results on VoxCeleb1 \[[38](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.17217v2#bib.bib38)\] and VoxCeleb2 \[[9](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.17217v2#bib.bib9)\] datasets, both on self and cross-subject reenactment and we compare against 5 state-of-the-art GAN-based methods, 3 StyleGAN2-based methods and 1 DPM-based method. We demonstrate that our method effectively tackles the challenging task of neural face reenactment by generating artifact-free images and by accurately transferring the target facial pose and faithfully reconstructing the identity and appearance characteristics of the source faces across different challenging conditions.

## 2 Related work

Diffusion Probabilistic Models (DPMs): DPMs \[[21](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.17217v2#bib.bib21), [53](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.17217v2#bib.bib53), [12](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.17217v2#bib.bib12)\] are generative models that learn a data distribution through a denoising process that iteratively transforms noisy samples into the target distribution. The recently introduced Latent Diffusion Models (LDMs) \[[49](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.17217v2#bib.bib49)\], transfer the denoising process into a lower-dimension latent space, enabling both faster training and better image generation performance. Currently, DPMs excel in conditional image generation \[[49](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.17217v2#bib.bib49), [72](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.17217v2#bib.bib72), [44](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.17217v2#bib.bib44), [66](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.17217v2#bib.bib66), [60](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.17217v2#bib.bib60)\] and image editing \[[34](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.17217v2#bib.bib34), [35](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.17217v2#bib.bib35), [31](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.17217v2#bib.bib31), [37](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.17217v2#bib.bib37), [41](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.17217v2#bib.bib41), [24](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.17217v2#bib.bib24), [32](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.17217v2#bib.bib32)\]. A recent approach for conditioning a DPM on various modalities (such as segmentation maps and keypoints) is ControlNet \[[72](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.17217v2#bib.bib72)\], that does so by learning to adapt a pre-trained diffusion model on the aforementioned additional conditions. Finally, Preechakul et al. \[[46](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.17217v2#bib.bib46)\] proposed DiffAE, a method that learns an image encoder to predict a semantic code which is then used to condition the diffusion process. Similarly to StyleGAN2-based methods \[[59](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.17217v2#bib.bib59), [25](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.17217v2#bib.bib25)\], DiffAE allows for semantic/latent code editing which leads to meaningful changes in the input images \[[46](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.17217v2#bib.bib46), [69](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.17217v2#bib.bib69)\], while in contrast to SD, DiffAE enables accurate image reconstruction.

Figure 2: Overview of the proposed method: Given a pair of a source ($`\mathbf{x}_{0}^{s}`$) and a target ($`\mathbf{x}_{0}^{t}`$) images, we condition the reenactment encoder $`\mathcal{E}_{r}`$ on the target facial landmarks $`\mathbf{y}_{t}`$, in order to predict the reenactment semantic code $`\mathbf{z}_{r}`$ that, when decoded by the DDIM, generates the reenacted image $`\mathbf{x}_{0}^{r}`$ that captures the source identity/appearance and the target head pose and facial expressions.

Neural face reenactment: The majority of existing face reenactment methods train controllable GANs conditioned on various facial information or interest (such as facial landmarks or keypoints). There is a line of works that propose to drive the image generation process using facial landmarks \[[71](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.17217v2#bib.bib71), [70](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.17217v2#bib.bib70)\], extracted from off-the-shelf facial landmark estimation methods such as \[[6](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.17217v2#bib.bib6)\]. Nevertheless, these methods suffer from identity leakage from the target faces to the reenacted ones on cross-subject reenactment, as facial landmarks typically maintain the facial shape of the input faces. Several approaches \[[14](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.17217v2#bib.bib14), [3](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.17217v2#bib.bib3), [4](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.17217v2#bib.bib4), [47](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.17217v2#bib.bib47), [30](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.17217v2#bib.bib30)\] leverage the disentangled properties of 3D Morphable Models (3DMMs) \[[2](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.17217v2#bib.bib2)\] to drive the generation process. Utilizing 3DMMs allows for disentanglement of the facial shape from the facial pose, which mitigates the identity leakage on cross-subject reenactment. Additionally, warping-based methods rely on learning keypoints that represent the facial motion between the source and target faces \[[52](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.17217v2#bib.bib52), [61](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.17217v2#bib.bib61), [75](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.17217v2#bib.bib75), [22](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.17217v2#bib.bib22)\]. Specifically, FOMM \[[52](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.17217v2#bib.bib52)\] proposes to model the motion change between the source and the target faces by calculating the changes in the position of the learned keypoints. This method can only work on sequences with minor changes of head pose, generating many visual artifacts and deformations otherwise. Several methods build upon FOMM \[[52](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.17217v2#bib.bib52)\] by introducing 3D keypoints \[[61](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.17217v2#bib.bib61)\] or using depth-guided facial keypoints \[[22](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.17217v2#bib.bib22)\]. Nevertheless, the warping-based approaches still produce unnatural deformations on the reenacted images.

A recent line of works \[[5](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.17217v2#bib.bib5), [3](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.17217v2#bib.bib3), [4](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.17217v2#bib.bib4), [67](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.17217v2#bib.bib67), [40](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.17217v2#bib.bib40)\] propose to leverage the photo-realistic image generation of pre-trained StyleGAN2 models \[[29](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.17217v2#bib.bib29)\]. Bounareli et al. \[[3](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.17217v2#bib.bib3)\] investigate the $`\mathcal{W}`$ latent space of StyleGAN2 to find the directions that are responsible for facial pose editing. In a similar fashion, Oorloff et al. \[[40](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.17217v2#bib.bib40)\] propose to use a combination of $`\mathcal{W}`$ space and style space $`\mathcal{S}`$ of StyleGAN2. Such methods are able to faithfully edit real images, however they require to optimize the weights of the GAN generator \[[48](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.17217v2#bib.bib48)\] to preserve the appearance of the source images. StyleHeat \[[67](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.17217v2#bib.bib67)\] proposes to use the feature space $`\mathcal{F}`$ of StyleGAN2, which despite improving reconstruction, leads to poor reenactment performance even under moderate head pose variations. Finally, HyperReenact \[[4](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.17217v2#bib.bib4)\], optimizes a hypernetwork \[[20](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.17217v2#bib.bib20)\] to alter the weights of StyleGAN2 generator, simultaneously refining the source appearance and transferring the target facial pose. In spite of the ability of HyperReenact to faithfully transfer the target head pose and expression, it often tends to produce over-smoothed reenacted images, missing important details of the source image (e.g., glasses, background or hair styles).

Finally, recent works train conditional DPMs to generate audio-driven talking head sequences \[[51](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.17217v2#bib.bib51), [54](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.17217v2#bib.bib54), [15](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.17217v2#bib.bib15)\]. However, audio-driven methods typically focus on reenacting the mouth region only, limiting their ability to edit the head pose and facial expressions. DiffusionRig \[[13](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.17217v2#bib.bib13)\] enables editing both the head pose and facial expressions by learning a DPM driven by a 3DMM \[[16](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.17217v2#bib.bib16)\]. However, it requires subject-specific fine-tuning with a small portrait dataset to accurately reconstruct the source appearance and identity. Additionally, the authors of \[[64](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.17217v2#bib.bib64)\] learn to animate a source face using multimodal guidance from audio signals and 3D parameters. Nevertheless, all the aforementioned methods require training a diffusion model which is computationally expensive. On the contrary, in this work we propose to use a pre-trained DPM and condition the generation process in order to perform one-shot face reenactment.

## 3 Proposed method

In this section we present DiffusionAct for one-shot neural face reenactment. An overview of the proposed method is illustrated in Fig. [2](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.17217v2#S2.F2 "Figure 2 ‣ 2 Related work ‣ DiffusionAct: Controllable Diffusion Autoencoder for One-shot Face Reenactment"). Our method builds on a pre-trained DiffAE \[[46](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.17217v2#bib.bib46)\], where we propose to learn a “reenactment encoder” $`\mathcal{E}_{r}`$ and condition it on the target facial landmarks $`\mathbf{y}_{t}`$ so as to learn to predict the semantic code $`\mathbf{z}_{r}`$ that reenacts, via the generative DDIM module, the source image $`\mathbf{x}_{0}^{s}`$ given a target image $`\mathbf{x}_{0}^{t}`$. This way, the reenacted image $`\mathbf{x}_{0}^{r}`$ preserves the identity/appearance of the source image, while it transfers the head orientation and facial expressions of the target image. In the rest of the section, we first briefly present the adopted DiffAE method (Sect. [3.1](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.17217v2#S3.SS1 "3.1 Background ‣ 3 Proposed method ‣ DiffusionAct: Controllable Diffusion Autoencoder for One-shot Face Reenactment")), and then we describe the proposed framework (Sect. [3.2](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.17217v2#S3.SS2 "3.2 DiffusionAct ‣ 3 Proposed method ‣ DiffusionAct: Controllable Diffusion Autoencoder for One-shot Face Reenactment")) and the adopted training protocol (Sect. [3.3](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.17217v2#S3.SS3 "3.3 Training protocol ‣ 3 Proposed method ‣ DiffusionAct: Controllable Diffusion Autoencoder for One-shot Face Reenactment")).

### 3.1 Background

We briefly discuss DiffAE, a recent method that equips a standard DDIM sampler with the editing property via a semantic vector space (similar to the latent space of a GAN). DiffAE utilizes a conditional DDIM \[[53](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.17217v2#bib.bib53)\] that learns to iteratively transform noisy samples into images that resemble a target distribution.

In contrast to a Denoising Diffusion Probabilistic Model (DDPM) \[[21](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.17217v2#bib.bib21)\] that uses a Markov Chain model to progressively add noise on a real image $`q{(\left. \mathbf{x}_{t} \middle| \mathbf{x}_{t - 1} \right.)}`$, DDIM speeds up the generation process by defining it as a non-Markovian process $`q{(\left. \mathbf{x}_{t} \middle| {\mathbf{x}_{t - 1},\mathbf{x}_{0}} \right.)}`$, further conditioned on the input image $`\mathbf{x}_{0}`$. The reverse (denoising) process $`p{(\left. \mathbf{x}_{t - 1} \middle| \mathbf{x}_{t} \right.)}`$ is learned using a function $`\epsilon_{\theta}{(\mathbf{x}_{t},t)}`$ that takes as input a noisy image $`\mathbf{x}_{t}`$ and learns to predict the added noise $`\epsilon`$ to $`\mathbf{x}_{0}`$ in order to get $`\mathbf{x}_{t}`$. This function is modeled as a UNet \[[50](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.17217v2#bib.bib50)\] and is trained via minimising $`\parallel{{\epsilon_{\theta}{(\mathbf{x}_{t},t)}} - \epsilon}\parallel`$. The denoised image at step $`t - 1`$ is calculated as

```math
{\mathbf{x}_{t - 1} = {{\sqrt{a_{t - 1}}f_{\theta}{(\mathbf{x}_{t},t)}} + {\sqrt{1 - a_{t - 1}}\epsilon_{\theta}{(\mathbf{x}_{t},t)}}}},
```

where $`f_{\theta}{(\mathbf{x}_{t},t)}`$ is the estimated real image $`{\hat{\mathbf{x}}}_{0}`$ at step $`t`$,

```math
{{f_{\theta}{(\mathbf{x}_{t},t)}} = \frac{\mathbf{x}_{t} - {\sqrt{1 - a_{t}}\epsilon_{\theta}{(\mathbf{x}_{t},t)}}}{\sqrt{a_{t}}}},
```

and $`a_{t} = {\prod_{s = 1}^{t}{({1 - \beta_{s}})}}`$, where $`\beta_{t}`$ is a fixed or learned variance schedule that represents the noise levels. The generation process of a DDIM can be deterministic, meaning that there is a deterministic mapping between the real image $`\mathbf{x}_{0}`$ and the noisy image $`\mathbf{x}_{T}`$, and vice versa.

For endowing a DDIM with a semantic space, DiffAE learns a semantic encoder $`\mathcal{E}`$ that learns to encode an image $`\mathbf{x}_{0}`$ into a semantic code $`\mathbf{z} \in {\mathbb{R}}^{512}`$. The diffusion process $`\epsilon_{\theta}{(\mathbf{x}_{t},t,\mathbf{z})}`$ is then conditioned on the semantic code $`\mathbf{z}`$. Using the semantic encoder $`\mathcal{E}`$ and the DDIM sampler, a real image $`\mathbf{x}_{0}`$ is mapped onto the stochastic subcode $`\mathbf{x}_{T}`$ using the reverse process of ([1](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.17217v2#S3.E1 "Equation 1 ‣ 3.1 Background ‣ 3 Proposed method ‣ DiffusionAct: Controllable Diffusion Autoencoder for One-shot Face Reenactment")):

```math
{\mathbf{x}_{t + 1} = {{\sqrt{a_{t + 1}}f_{\theta}{(\mathbf{x}_{t},t,\mathbf{z})}} + {\sqrt{1 - a_{t + 1}}\epsilon_{\theta}{(\mathbf{x}_{t},t,\mathbf{z})}}}}.
```

Finally, a real image is reconstructed remarkably faithfully by the DDIM of DiffAE via decoding its semantic code $`\mathbf{z}`$ and stochastic subcode $`\mathbf{x}_{T}`$.

### 3.2 DiffusionAct

In this section we present our framework for neural face reenactment. The proposed method employs a DDIM (pre-trained on the FFHQ \[[28](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.17217v2#bib.bib28)\] dataset) and a semantic encoder, which –in contrast to DiffAE that aims to predict a semantic code encoding the input image– learns to predict a semantic code that encodes the pose of a target face, and the appearance and identity characteristics of a source face. For doing so, we propose to condition the semantic encoder, which hereinafter will be referred to as the reenactment encoder, on the facial landmarks and the gaze direction of the target face, as shown in Fig. [2](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.17217v2#S2.F2 "Figure 2 ‣ 2 Related work ‣ DiffusionAct: Controllable Diffusion Autoencoder for One-shot Face Reenactment").

Concretely, given a pair of source ($`\mathbf{x}_{0}^{s}`$) and target ($`\mathbf{x}_{0}^{t}`$) images, we aim to generate the reenacted $`\mathbf{x}_{0}^{r}`$ image, that preserves the source appearance and identity characteristics (i.e., background, hair style/color, facial accessories) and transfers the target facial pose (i.e., head pose orientation and facial expressions). To do so, we propose learning to predict the desired reenacted semantic code $`\mathbf{z}_{r}`$ by conditioning the reenactment encoder $`\mathcal{E}_{r}`$ on the facial landmarks and the gaze position of the target face, $`\mathbf{y}_{t}`$, extracted by the pre-trained EMOCA \[[10](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.17217v2#bib.bib10)\] network and the pre-trained gaze estimation network of \[[73](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.17217v2#bib.bib73)\]. We detail the construction of $`\mathbf{y}_{t}`$ in the supplementary material.

For conditioning the reenactment encoder ($`\mathcal{E}_{r}`$) on the target facial pose ($`\mathbf{y}_{t}`$), inspired by the recently published ControlNet \[[72](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.17217v2#bib.bib72)\], we introduce the target landmark condition through a “zero” convolution, the output of which we add to the source image before we feed it to the reenactment encoder $`\mathcal{E}_{r}`$, as shown in left part of Fig. [2](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.17217v2#S2.F2 "Figure 2 ‣ 2 Related work ‣ DiffusionAct: Controllable Diffusion Autoencoder for One-shot Face Reenactment"). We note that, according to \[[72](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.17217v2#bib.bib72)\], a “zero” convolution refers to an $`1 \times 1`$ convolution layer whose weights are only initially set to zero and allowed to gradually change during training injecting the desired spatial landmark-based condition into the training of the proposed reenactment encoder $`\mathcal{E}_{r}`$. Finally, as illustrated in the right part of Fig. [2](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.17217v2#S2.F2 "Figure 2 ‣ 2 Related work ‣ DiffusionAct: Controllable Diffusion Autoencoder for One-shot Face Reenactment"), in order to generate the reenacted image, we first encode the source image $`\mathbf{x}_{0}^{s}`$ into the stochastic noisy subcode $`\mathbf{x}_{T}^{s}`$ using Eq. [3](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.17217v2#S3.E3 "Equation 3 ‣ 3.1 Background ‣ 3 Proposed method ‣ DiffusionAct: Controllable Diffusion Autoencoder for One-shot Face Reenactment") and the source semantic code $`\mathbf{z}_{s}`$. Given the noisy subcode $`\mathbf{x}_{T}^{s}`$ and the reenacted code $`\mathbf{z}_{r}`$, the DDIM decoder generates the reenacted image $`\mathbf{x}_{0}^{r}`$.

### 3.3 Training protocol

In this section we present the training process of the proposed framework which is split into two stages: i) first, we pre-train the reenactment encoder $`\mathcal{E}_{r}`$ under the self reenactment setting, and ii) we continue training the reenactment encoder $`\mathcal{E}_{r}`$ by incorporating the DDIM sampler to synthesize the reenacted images.

Pre-training: We first pre-train the reenactment encoder $`\mathcal{E}_{r}`$ to predict the reenacted semantic code $`\mathbf{z}_{r}`$ that encodes the identity/appearance of the source face and the facial pose of the target face. We do so under the self-reenactment setting, i.e., where the source and target faces have the same identity – i.e., we first learn to predict a reenacted code $`\mathbf{z}_{r}`$ that is identical to the code of the target face, $`\mathbf{z}_{t}`$, minimising their $`\ell_{1}`$ distance, $`{\parallel{\mathbf{z}_{r} - \mathbf{z}_{t}}\parallel}_{1}`$. We note that we initialize the reenactment encoder $`\mathcal{E}_{r}`$ by adopting the weights of the semantic encoder $`\mathcal{E}`$ of DiffAE. Additionally, the DDIM sampler is not part of this pre-training process.

After training the proposed reenactment encoder $`\mathcal{E}_{r}`$ using only this objective, we obtain an encoder that predicts a semantic code $`\mathbf{z}_{r}`$ encoding both the appearance of the source face and the target facial pose. This means that given the predicted code $`\mathbf{z}_{r}`$ and the stochastic subcode of the source image $`\mathbf{x}_{T}^{s}`$, the DDIM decoder generates the reenacted image that depicts the source identity in the target facial pose. Whilst this training stage does not result in optimal face reenactment performance (i.e., the reenacted images are prone to some visual artifacts), we show in Sect. [4.3](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.17217v2#S4.SS3 "4.3 Ablation studies ‣ 4 Experiments ‣ DiffusionAct: Controllable Diffusion Autoencoder for One-shot Face Reenactment") that it is crucial as a warm-up pre-training stage, before we proceed to the main training stage of our framework discussed below.

Training: After obtaining the proposed reenactment encoder $`\mathcal{E}_{r}`$ pre-trained on the task of self-reenactment, as discussed above, we proceed to the main training process of $`\mathcal{E}_{r}`$ where we additionally incorporate DDIM as a generative module (to generate the reenacted images), along with a series of well-studied reconstruction and pose transfer losses, following the common practice in literature \[[3](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.17217v2#bib.bib3), [63](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.17217v2#bib.bib63), [4](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.17217v2#bib.bib4), [42](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.17217v2#bib.bib42)\]. We illustrate the training process of our method in Fig. [2](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.17217v2#S2.F2 "Figure 2 ‣ 2 Related work ‣ DiffusionAct: Controllable Diffusion Autoencoder for One-shot Face Reenactment"). The total loss we minimise is given as:

```math
{\mathcal{L} = {\mathcal{L}_{rec} + \mathcal{L}_{pose}}},
```

where $`\mathcal{L}_{rec}`$ and $`\mathcal{L}_{pose}`$ are the reconstruction and pose losses, respectively, which we discuss briefly below and in detail in the supplementary material. We propose the following reconstruction loss to guide the reenacted images to depict the identity and the appearance of the source faces:

```math
{\mathcal{L}_{rec} = {{\lambda_{pix}\mathcal{L}_{pix}} + {\lambda_{per}\mathcal{L}_{per}} + {\lambda_{id}\mathcal{L}_{id}} + {\lambda_{bg}\mathcal{L}_{bg}} + {\lambda_{st}\mathcal{L}_{st}}}},
```

where $`\mathcal{L}_{pix}`$, $`\mathcal{L}_{per}`$, and $`\mathcal{L}_{id}`$ are respectively the pixel-wise, perceptual, and identity losses, calculated as in \[[4](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.17217v2#bib.bib4)\]. We additionally calculate a background loss $`\mathcal{L}_{bg}`$ by using the off-the-shelf pre-trained face segmentation network \[[68](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.17217v2#bib.bib68)\] to create a mask around the face area and keep only the background and hair areas. Finally, inspired by \[[1](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.17217v2#bib.bib1)\], we calculate the style loss $`\mathcal{L}_{st}`$ as an $`\ell_{2}`$ distance in the ViT space of the pre-trained FaRL \[[76](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.17217v2#bib.bib76)\]. Furthermore, in order to force the effective and realistic transfer of the target facial pose (head orientation and facial expressions) onto the reenacted face, we propose to use the following loss term:

```math
{\mathcal{L}_{pose} = {{\lambda_{g}\mathcal{L}_{g}} + {\lambda_{sh}\mathcal{L}_{sh}} + {\lambda_{hp}\mathcal{L}_{hp}}}},
```

where $`\mathcal{L}_{g}`$, $`\mathcal{L}_{sh}`$, and $`\mathcal{L}_{hp}`$ denote the gaze, shape, and head pose losses, respectively. We calculate the gaze loss as the $`\ell_{1}`$ distance of the gaze direction extracted using the off-the-shelf gaze estimation network of \[[73](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.17217v2#bib.bib73)\], the shape loss as the $`\ell_{1}`$ distance of the 2D facial landmarks \[[10](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.17217v2#bib.bib10)\], and the head pose loss as the average $`\ell_{1}`$ distance of the three Euler angles (i.e., yaw, pitch, and roll) calculated using the head pose coefficients extracted by EMOCA \[[10](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.17217v2#bib.bib10)\].

Towards achieving improved reenactment performance, we propose a training protocol that involves both self reenactment and image reconstruction tasks. Specifically, during training, we split the mini-batch into two halves. In the first half, the source and target images depict the same subject but on different facial pose (self reenactment), while in the second half, the source and target images are the same (image reconstruction). In our ablation studies (Sect. [4.3](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.17217v2#S4.SS3 "4.3 Ablation studies ‣ 4 Experiments ‣ DiffusionAct: Controllable Diffusion Autoencoder for One-shot Face Reenactment")), we show that this training protocol improves our qualitative and quantitative results.

Finally, to further improve the performance of the proposed framework, we suggest to fine-tune the DDIM sampler, while being jointly optimized with the proposed reenactment encoder $`\mathcal{E}_{r}`$ (see Fig. [2](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.17217v2#S2.F2 "Figure 2 ‣ 2 Related work ‣ DiffusionAct: Controllable Diffusion Autoencoder for One-shot Face Reenactment")). We show that this strategy further improves the reenactment performance, since the adopted DDIM \[[46](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.17217v2#bib.bib46)\] has been pre-trained on the FFHQ dataset \[[28](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.17217v2#bib.bib28)\], which has a different distribution of the VoxCeleb datasets \[[38](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.17217v2#bib.bib38), [9](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.17217v2#bib.bib9)\] in terms of different head poses and expressions \[[3](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.17217v2#bib.bib3)\].

## 4 Experiments

In this section, we provide the implementation and training details of our approach in Sect. [4.1](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.17217v2#S4.SS1 "4.1 Implementation details ‣ 4 Experiments ‣ DiffusionAct: Controllable Diffusion Autoencoder for One-shot Face Reenactment"), we report comparisons of our method with 9 state-of-the-art methods both on self and on cross-subject reenactment tasks in Sect. [4.2](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.17217v2#S4.SS2 "4.2 Comparisons to state-of-the-art ‣ 4 Experiments ‣ DiffusionAct: Controllable Diffusion Autoencoder for One-shot Face Reenactment"), and we present ablation studies on the impact of the various design choices in Sect. [4.3](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.17217v2#S4.SS3 "4.3 Ablation studies ‣ 4 Experiments ‣ DiffusionAct: Controllable Diffusion Autoencoder for One-shot Face Reenactment").

### 4.1 Implementation details

We use a DDIM model \[[46](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.17217v2#bib.bib46)\] pre-trained on the FFHQ dataset \[[28](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.17217v2#bib.bib28)\]. We train our model on the VoxCeleb1 dataset \[[38](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.17217v2#bib.bib38)\], which contains around 20K videos. During the pre-training stage of the reenactment encoder, we use a learning rate of $`10^{- 3}`$ and a batch size of $`32`$, while during the main training stage we use $`T_{tr} = 8`$ steps to synthesize the images, a learning rate of $`10^{- 4}`$, and a batch size of $`4`$. Finally, we fine-tune the DDIM sampler along with the reenactment encoder using a learning rate of $`10^{- 5}`$ and a batch size of $`4`$. In all training stages we optimize our models using the AdamW \[[33](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.17217v2#bib.bib33)\] optimizer. We also set $`{\lambda_{pix},\lambda_{per},\lambda_{id},\lambda_{st}} = 20`$, $`\lambda_{bg} = 10`$, $`\lambda_{sh} = 0.5`$ and $`{\lambda_{g},\lambda_{hp}} = 2`$. During inference, we use $`T = 50`$ steps to calculate the stochastic subcodes $`\mathbf{x}_{T}^{s}`$ and $`T = 20`$ steps to generate the reenacted images. For our experiments we used 2 Nvidia Quadro RTX 8000.

Self reenactment task: On the self reenactment task we report 8 evaluation metrics following the related literature. For APD and AED, we calculate the average $`\ell_{1}`$ distance of the three Euler angles and the expression coefficients extracted using the pre-trained EMOCA \[[10](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.17217v2#bib.bib10)\] network. We note that all metrics are calculated between the target and reenacted images. As shown, our method performs on par with HyperReenact \[[4](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.17217v2#bib.bib4)\] on head pose transfer (APD), while it performs better on expression transfer (AED). Additionally, our method outperforms all other methods on L1, PSNR, and SSIM metrics by generating high quality images that resemble the real ones. Finally, on identity preservation (CSIM) our method remains competitive, however we argue that a higher CSIM score does not necessarily imply better reenactment. Specifically, ArcFace \[[11](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.17217v2#bib.bib11)\] network, which is used to extract the identity features for calculating the CSIM metric, has been designed and trained to be invariant to certain appearance details (e.g., glasses) or distortions, which are crucial for face reenactment. As shown in the qualitative comparisons (Fig. [3](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.17217v2#S4.F3 "Figure 3 ‣ 4.2 Comparisons to state-of-the-art ‣ 4 Experiments ‣ DiffusionAct: Controllable Diffusion Autoencoder for One-shot Face Reenactment")), some methods that exhibit higher CSIM scores than our method (such as DaGAN \[[22](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.17217v2#bib.bib22)\] and Face2Face \[[65](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.17217v2#bib.bib65)\]) tend to produce visual artifacts and deformations, which CSIM fails to capture, while also HyperReenact \[[4](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.17217v2#bib.bib4)\] fails to accurately reconstruct the source appearance details, e.g., glasses, hair styles (Fig. [4](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.17217v2#S4.F4 "Figure 4 ‣ 4.2 Comparisons to state-of-the-art ‣ 4 Experiments ‣ DiffusionAct: Controllable Diffusion Autoencoder for One-shot Face Reenactment")). In the supplementary material we provide visual comparisons against the methods that have higher CSIM scores. As shown, while our reenacted images are visually superior, the CSIM score is lower compared to the other methods.

Our method can accurately transfer the target facial pose, while also preserve the identity and appearance of the source faces both on self and on cross-subject reenactment tasks. Compared to the GAN-based methods, namely DaGAN \[[22](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.17217v2#bib.bib22)\], Rome \[[30](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.17217v2#bib.bib30)\], Face2Face \[[65](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.17217v2#bib.bib65)\], UniFace \[[63](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.17217v2#bib.bib63)\] and DPE \[[42](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.17217v2#bib.bib42)\], which achieve good quantitative results on CSIM metric, our method is able to more accurately transfer the target head pose and expression and also maintain the appearance of the source faces, without producing artifacts. We also perform on par on facial pose transfer with the StyleGAN2-based methods, namely StyleHeat \[[67](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.17217v2#bib.bib67)\], FD \[[3](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.17217v2#bib.bib3)\] and HyperReenact \[[4](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.17217v2#bib.bib4)\]. Regarding the image quality, StyleHeat \[[67](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.17217v2#bib.bib67)\] and FD \[[3](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.17217v2#bib.bib3)\] produce visual artifacts especially when there is difference on the head pose between the source and target faces, while our reenacted images are mostly artifact-free. Finally, while HyperReenact \[[4](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.17217v2#bib.bib4)\] is able to produce visually pleasing images, it tends to over-smooth the faces and cannot accurately reconstruct details such as the hair styles, glasses or background elements, which are important for maintaining the overall perceptual quality of the generated images. A more detailed comparison with HyperReenact is illustrated in Fig. [4](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.17217v2#S4.F4 "Figure 4 ‣ 4.2 Comparisons to state-of-the-art ‣ 4 Experiments ‣ DiffusionAct: Controllable Diffusion Autoencoder for One-shot Face Reenactment"). As shown, while this method does not produce visual artifacts and successfully transfers the target facial pose, it fails in reconstructing important appearance details of the source images, e.g., glasses, hats, hair styles and background details. Finally, DiffusionRig \[[13](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.17217v2#bib.bib13)\], which is a diffusion-based method similar to ours, even though being able to transfer the target facial pose, it cannot produce realistic results using only one source frame, i.e. under one-shot settings.

### 4.3 Ablation studies

In this section, we present our ablation studies to assess how our training choices contribute to the final results. Specifically, we evaluate the contribution of (a) the pre-training process (“w/o pre-training”), (b) the training protocol that involves both self reenactment and image reconstruction tasks (“w/o batch split”), and (c) the joint optimization of the DDIM decoder with the proposed reenactment encoder $`\mathcal{E}_{r}`$ (“w/o fine-tuning”). We provide both quantitative and qualitative comparisons in Table [3](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.17217v2#S4.T3 "Table 3 ‣ 4.3 Ablation studies ‣ 4 Experiments ‣ DiffusionAct: Controllable Diffusion Autoencoder for One-shot Face Reenactment") and Fig. [5](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.17217v2#S4.F5 "Figure 5 ‣ 4.3 Ablation studies ‣ 4 Experiments ‣ DiffusionAct: Controllable Diffusion Autoencoder for One-shot Face Reenactment"), respectively. In the supplementary material we also provide results of our final model with different steps $`T`$ to assess how the different steps contribute to the reconstruction quality of the generated images.

Regarding (a), we show results of our method without performing pre-training of the reenactment encoder, described in Sect. [3.3](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.17217v2#S3.SS3 "3.3 Training protocol ‣ 3 Proposed method ‣ DiffusionAct: Controllable Diffusion Autoencoder for One-shot Face Reenactment"). Instead the reenactment encoder is directly trained with the reconstruction losses. As shown in Table [3](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.17217v2#S4.T3 "Table 3 ‣ 4.3 Ablation studies ‣ 4 Experiments ‣ DiffusionAct: Controllable Diffusion Autoencoder for One-shot Face Reenactment"), omitting the pre-training step (“w/o pre-training”) results in less accurate transfer of head pose and expression. ## 5 Conclusions

In this paper, we introduced a neural face reenactment framework, called DiffusionAct, which is based on a pre-trained DPM. Our method employs a pre-trained diffusion autoencoder (DiffAE) \[[46](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.17217v2#bib.bib46)\], which consists of a semantic encoder and a DDIM sampler. The semantic encoder of DiffAE is able to encode an input image into a semantically meaningful code which can be used for image editing. Inspired by ContolNet \[[72](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.17217v2#bib.bib72)\], we propose to control the semantic encoder of DiffAE using the target facial pose (reenactment encoder). Given a source image and a target facial pose, our reenactment encoder learns to predict the reenacted code that guides the generation process of DDIM to synthesize the reenacted image. We demonstrate that, in comparison to several state-of-the-art approaches, our method exhibits better or on-par reenactment performance, leading to facial images with less visual artifacts and deformations, showing that a pre-trained DDIM can effectively be adapted to the problem of neural face reenactment.

## Appendix B Facial landmarks with EMOCA \[[10](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.17217v2#bib.bib10)\]

As discussed in the Sect. 3.2 of the main paper, the facial landmarks extracted using an off-the-shelf facial landmark detection method \[[6](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.17217v2#bib.bib6)\] contain identity details, i.e., the facial shape of the input faces. As a result, when face reenactment methods use facial landmarks to drive the generation process, on the task of cross-subject face reenactment, identity leakage from the target faces into the reenacted ones is observed \[[70](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.17217v2#bib.bib70), [71](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.17217v2#bib.bib71)\]. To alleviate this issue, we use a 3D reconstruction method, namely EMOCA \[[10](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.17217v2#bib.bib10)\], which is based on 3D Morphable Models (3DMMs) \[[2](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.17217v2#bib.bib2)\] and thus allows for reconstruction of the facial landmarks using the disentangled representations of the facial shape and pose. Specifically, a 3D facial shape $`\text{s} \in {\mathbb{R}}^{3N}`$ can be parameterized as:

```math
{\text{s} = {\overline{\text{s}} + {\text{S}_{i}\mathbf{p}_{i}} + {\text{S}_{\theta}\mathbf{p}_{\theta}} + {\text{S}_{e}\mathbf{p}_{e}}}},
```

where $`\overline{\text{s}}`$ denotes the mean 3D facial shape, $`\text{S}_{i} \in {\mathbb{R}}^{{3N} \times m_{i}}`$, $`\text{S}_{\theta} \in {\mathbb{R}}^{{3N} \times m_{\theta}}`$, and $`\text{S}_{e} \in {\mathbb{R}}^{{3N} \times m_{e}}`$ denote respectively the PCA bases for identity, head pose orientation, and expression, and $`\mathbf{p}_{i}`$, $`\mathbf{p}_{\theta}`$, and $`\mathbf{p}_{e}`$ denote respectively the corresponding identity, head pose orientation, and expression coefficients.

Having this representation of the 3D facial shape and considering as input a pair of source and target images from different identities, we can calculate the ground-truth target facial shape $`\text{s}^{t}`$. Specifically, $`\text{s}^{t}`$ is obtained using the identity coefficients $`\mathbf{p}_{i}^{s}`$ of the source image and the head pose and expression coefficients $`\mathbf{p}_{\theta}^{t},\mathbf{p}_{e}^{t}`$ of the target image as follows:

```math
{\text{s}^{t} = {\overline{\text{s}} + {\text{S}_{i}\mathbf{p}_{i}^{s}} + {\text{S}_{\theta}\mathbf{p}_{\theta}^{t}} + {\text{S}_{e}\mathbf{p}_{e}^{t}}}}.
```

Hence, the corresponding target facial landmarks contain the head pose and expression of the target image and the facial shape of the source image, overcoming the identity leakage from the target image to the reenacted ones. An example of cross-subject reenactment is illustrated in Fig. [6](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.17217v2#A2.F6 "Figure 6 ‣ Appendix B Facial landmarks with EMOCA [10] ‣ DiffusionAct: Controllable Diffusion Autoencoder for One-shot Face Reenactment"). The first and second columns show respectively the source and the target images which have large facial shape difference; i.e., the source image has long facial shape, while the target image has round shape. In the third column, we present the reenacted image generated using the target facial landmarks extracted from a pre-trained landmark estimation network \[[6](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.17217v2#bib.bib6)\]. The last column shows the reenacted images generated using the reconstructed facial landmarks extracted from EMOCA \[[10](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.17217v2#bib.bib10)\] as described in ([8](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.17217v2#A2.E8 "Equation 8 ‣ Appendix B Facial landmarks with EMOCA [10] ‣ DiffusionAct: Controllable Diffusion Autoencoder for One-shot Face Reenactment")) above. Clearly, when employing the target facial landmarks from \[[6](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.17217v2#bib.bib6)\], the reenacted image inherits the facial shape of the target image, which results in missing important identity details from the source image.

![Refer to caption](https://arxiv.org/html/x6.png)

Figure 6: Example of cross-subject face reenactment driven by the target facial landmarks extracted from the off-the-shelf facial landmark estimation network of \[[6](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.17217v2#bib.bib6)\] and by the reconstructed facial landmarks extracted from EMOCA \[[10](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.17217v2#bib.bib10)\] (as described in Eq. [8](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.17217v2#A2.E8 "Equation 8 ‣ Appendix B Facial landmarks with EMOCA [10] ‣ DiffusionAct: Controllable Diffusion Autoencoder for One-shot Face Reenactment")). Clearly, when employing the target facial landmarks from \[[6](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.17217v2#bib.bib6)\], the reenacted image has the facial shape of the target image (identity leakage), which is avoided using the adopted EMOCA \[[10](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.17217v2#bib.bib10)\] network.

Finally, in order to control the gaze direction on the reenacted faces, as discussed in Sect. 3.2 of the main paper, along with the standard facial landmarks, we further used an additional landmark point that corresponds to the gaze position. Specifically, we estimated the gaze direction angles $`\alpha,\beta`$ using the pre-trained gaze estimation network of \[[73](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.17217v2#bib.bib73)\], and by using the landmark points that correspond to the eyes, we calculated the width $`w_{e}`$, height $`h_{e}`$, and the center $`c_{e}`$ of each eye, and the gaze position $`(g_{x},g_{y})`$ as follows:

$`{g_{x} = {c_{e} - {\frac{w_{e}}{2} \cdot {\sin{(\beta)}} \cdot {\cos{(\alpha)}}}}},`$$`{g_{y} = {c_{e} - {\frac{h_{e}}{2} \cdot {\sin{(\alpha)}}}}}.`$

## Appendix C Training objectives

As discussed in the Sect. 3.3 of the main paper, we propose to train our method in two stages; a pre-training stage and a main training stage. Specifically, as illustrated in Fig. [7](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.17217v2#A3.F7 "Figure 7 ‣ Appendix C Training objectives ‣ DiffusionAct: Controllable Diffusion Autoencoder for One-shot Face Reenactment"), we pre-train the reenactment encoder $`\mathcal{E}_{r}`$ under the self reenactment setting, where we only use the $`\ell_{1}`$ loss, $`\mathcal{L}_{z} = {\parallel{\mathbf{z}_{r} - \mathbf{z}_{t}}\parallel}_{1}`$, between the reenacted code $`\mathbf{z}_{r}`$ predicted by the trainable model $`\mathcal{E}_{r}`$ and the target code $`\mathbf{z}_{t}`$ predicted by the semantic encoder $`\mathcal{E}`$ of DiffAE \[[46](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.17217v2#bib.bib46)\]. We then continue training the reenactment encoder $`\mathcal{E}_{r}`$ (main training stage) by incorporating the DDIM sampler \[[53](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.17217v2#bib.bib53)\] as a generative module to synthesize the reenacted images (as illustrated in Fig. 2 of the main paper). Our training objective in this stage combines both reconstruction and poss losses to ensure that the reenacted image will depict the source identity in the target facial pose (head orientation and facial expressions). In this section we present in detail the adopted objectives used during training.

![Refer to caption](https://arxiv.org/html/x7.png)

Figure 7: Pre-training process: We pre-train the reenactment encoder $`\mathcal{E}_{r}`$ under the self reenactment setting using only the $`\ell_{1}`$ loss between the reenacted code $`\mathbf{z}_{r}`$ and the target code $`\mathbf{z}_{t}`$ given by the semantic encoder $`\mathcal{E}`$ of DiffAE \[[46](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.17217v2#bib.bib46)\].

Pixel-wise loss $`\mathcal{L}_{pix}`$ The pixel-wise loss $`\mathcal{L}_{pix}`$ is defined as the $`\ell_{2}`$-distance between the target $`\mathbf{x}_{0}^{t}`$ and the reenacted images $`\mathbf{x}_{0}^{r}`$:

```math
\mathcal{L}_{pix} = {\parallel{\mathbf{x}_{0}^{t} - \mathbf{x}_{0}^{r}}\parallel}_{2}
```

Perceptual loss $`\mathcal{L}_{per}`$ Instead of relying only on the pixel-wise loss, we calculate the perceptual loss $`\mathcal{L}_{per}`$, which is defined as the sum of $`\ell_{2}`$-distances between the intermediate features of a pre-trained VGG19 network $`\phi`$ \[[26](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.17217v2#bib.bib26)\]:

```math
{\mathcal{L}_{per} = {\sum\limits_{l = 1}^{N}{\parallel{{\phi_{l}{(\mathbf{x}_{0}^{t})}} - {\phi_{l}{(\mathbf{x}_{0}^{r})}}}\parallel}_{2}}},
```

where $`N`$ is the number of layers used from the VGG19 network to compute the perceptual loss.

Identity loss $`\mathcal{L}_{id}`$ In order to preserve the identity characteristics of the input faces, we calculate the identity loss $`\mathcal{L}_{id}`$, which is defined as the cosine similarity of the features extracted from the pre-trained ArcFace \[[11](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.17217v2#bib.bib11)\] network $`\mathcal{F}`$:

```math
\mathcal{L}_{id} = {1 - \frac{{{\mathcal{F}{(\mathbf{x}_{0}^{t})}} \cdot \mathcal{F}}{(\mathbf{x}_{0}^{r})}}{{\|{\mathcal{F}{(\mathbf{x}_{0}^{t})}}\|}{\|{\mathcal{F}{(\mathbf{x}_{0}^{r})}}\|}}}
```

Background loss $`\mathcal{L}_{bg}`$ In order to enhance the background preservation, we calculate the background loss $`\mathcal{L}_{bg}`$ by using the off-the-shelf pre-trained face segmentation network of \[[68](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.17217v2#bib.bib68)\] to create a mask $`m_{f}`$ around the face area so as to keep only the background and hair areas. We then calculate the background loss as:

```math
{\mathcal{L}_{bg} = {\parallel{{\mathbf{x}_{0}^{t} \odot m_{f}^{t}} - {\mathbf{x}_{0}^{r} \odot m_{f}^{r}}}\parallel}_{2}},
```

where $`m_{f}^{t}`$, $`m_{f}^{r}`$ are the corresponding masks of the target and the reenacted images.

Style loss $`\mathcal{L}_{st}`$ To improve the preservation of the input facial appearance, we incorporate the style loss $`\mathcal{L}_{st}`$, similarly to \[[1](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.17217v2#bib.bib1)\]. Specifically, we use features from the pre-trained FaRL \[[76](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.17217v2#bib.bib76)\] ViT-based image encoder, which is trained on image and text pairs to learn meaningful feature representations of facial images. We calculate the style loss as the $`\ell_{2}`$-distance of the $`512`$-dimensional feature vectors extracted from FaRL encoder $`\mathcal{E}_{farl}`$ as follows:

```math
{\mathcal{L}_{st} = {\parallel{{\mathcal{E}_{farl}{(\mathbf{x}_{0}^{t})}} - {\mathcal{E}_{farl}{(\mathbf{x}_{0}^{r})}}}\parallel}_{2}}.
```

Gaze loss $`\mathcal{L}_{g}`$ To control the gaze direction on the reenacted images, we calculate the gaze loss, which is defined as the $`\ell_{1}`$-distance of the gaze direction, i.e., the gaze angles, between the target and the reenacted images extracted from the pre-trained gaze estimation network of \[[73](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.17217v2#bib.bib73)\], as follows:

```math
\mathcal{L}_{g} = {\parallel g{(\mathbf{x}_{0}^{t})})} - g{(\mathbf{x}_{0}^{r})}\parallel_{1}.
```

Shape loss $`\mathcal{L}_{shape}`$ In order to transfer the target facial pose defined as the head pose orientation and the facial expressions we calculate the shape loss, which is defined as the $`\ell_{1}`$-distance between the target and the reenacted 2D facial landmarks extracted from EMOCA \[[10](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.17217v2#bib.bib10)\]:

```math
{\mathcal{L}_{shape} = {\parallel{l^{t} - l^{r}}\parallel}_{1}}.
```

Head pose loss $`\mathcal{L}_{hp}`$ To improve the head pose transfer, we calculate the mean $`\ell_{1}`$-distance of the head pose orientation, i.e., the three Euler angles $`a`$, yaw, pitch and roll, between the target and the reenacted images:

```math
{\mathcal{L}_{hp} = {\parallel{a^{t} - a^{r}}\parallel}_{1}},
```

## Appendix E Experiments

### E.1 Analysis on identity preservation

Neural face reenactment is a complex task that requires several quantitative and qualitative results in order to fairly evaluate the various methods. Thus, we provide several metrics in order to evaluate the reconstruction quality and identity preservation of the reenacted images, i.e., PNSR, SSIM, L1, LPIPS and CSIM and additional metrics to assess the head pose and expression transfer, i.e., NME, APD and AED. Along with the quantitative metrics we also conduct a user study to assess the human quality perception. Our approach surpasses all other methods on PSNR, SSIM and L1 metrics, demonstrating our higher reconstruction ability. Nevertheless, on identity preservation, i.e., CSIM metric, although our method remains competitive, DaGAN \[[22](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.17217v2#bib.bib22)\], Face2Face \[[65](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.17217v2#bib.bib65)\], DPE \[[42](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.17217v2#bib.bib42)\] and HyperReenact \[[4](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.17217v2#bib.bib4)\] have higher CSIM scores. We argue that the CSIM metric is not the most suitable to evaluate the reenactment methods and as a result a higher CSIM score does not necessarily imply better reenacted images. In order to support our argument we show in Fig. [10](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.17217v2#A5.F10 "Figure 10 ‣ E.1 Analysis on identity preservation ‣ Appendix E Experiments ‣ DiffusionAct: Controllable Diffusion Autoencoder for One-shot Face Reenactment") visual comparisons between the methods that achieve higher CSIM scores. As shown the generated images of DaGAN \[[22](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.17217v2#bib.bib22)\], Face2Face \[[65](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.17217v2#bib.bib65)\] and DPE \[[42](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.17217v2#bib.bib42)\] have visual artifacts and deformations, however the corresponding CSIM scores are high, indicating that CSIM is invariant to such distortions. Additionally, HyperReenact \[[4](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.17217v2#bib.bib4)\] over-smooths the generated images and misses important appearance details.

### E.2 Additional comparisons

We provide comparisons with two recent methods, namely InstantID \[[60](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.17217v2#bib.bib60)\] and IP-Adapter \[[66](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.17217v2#bib.bib66)\] that condition SD models \[[49](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.17217v2#bib.bib49)\] to generate controllable images that maintain the style of the input faces. In contrast, our method leverages the high quality generation of Diffusion Probabilistic Models (DPMs) and produces accurate reconstructions.

## Appendix F Limitations

In this work, we present a method for neural face reenactment based on a pre-trained diffusion model. In our experimental results (Sect. 4.2 of the main paper), we show that the proposed framework is able to accurately transfer the head pose and facial expressions of the target faces, while at the same time the identity and the appearance characteristics of the source face are faithfully preserved. Nevertheless, as shown in Table [7](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.17217v2#A5.T7 "Table 7 ‣ E.2 Additional comparisons ‣ Appendix E Experiments ‣ DiffusionAct: Controllable Diffusion Autoencoder for One-shot Face Reenactment") in this document, our method, which builds on a DDIM-based method \[[53](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.17217v2#bib.bib53), [46](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.17217v2#bib.bib46)\], exhibits slower inference time compared to GAN- and StyleGAN2-based methods, as expected. Additionally, we observe that, in some cases, the reenacted images appear brighter than the real ones, as illustrated in Fig. [12](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.17217v2#A5.F12 "Figure 12 ‣ E.2 Additional comparisons ‣ Appendix E Experiments ‣ DiffusionAct: Controllable Diffusion Autoencoder for One-shot Face Reenactment"). Notably, these changes in brightness are particularly prominent in darker, low-resolution videos. We attribute this to the adopted DDIM sampler, which, since it has been pre-trained on the FFHQ dataset \[[28](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.17217v2#bib.bib28)\], differs significantly from the VoxCeleb datasets \[[38](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.17217v2#bib.bib38), [9](https://arxiv.org/html/2403.17217v2#bib.bib9)\] in terms of image quality.

## Appendix G Ethical considerations

Neural face reenactment methods can be used in various domains, such as in film and video production, video conferencing, video games, and in augmented and virtual reality applications. However, we recognize that it can also be used for malicious purposes to generate deep fake videos and spread false information, that could potentially harm individuals and the society. Nowadays, there are many neural face reenactment methods that can create high-quality deep fake videos that are indistinguishable from the real ones. As a result, currently there is great interest on deep fake detection methods, that could prevent the malicious use of deep fake generated videos. We will share our model and results with the deepfake detection community to help researchers train more accurate deep fake detection algorithms.
