# Introduction

The automatic synthesis of lifelike videos depicting a 3D speaking individual, driven by audio input and a single reference image, presents a groundbreaking advancement with profound implications across a myriad of domains, including film-making, augmenting human-computer interaction paradigms, and virtual reality. Intuitively, the pivotal aspects of audio-driven human synthesis comprise: 1) generating high-fidelity bodily movements and facial expressions, including precise lip synchronization, vivid emotions, and a broad range of gestures; 2) the capacity to yield photo-realistic and temporally coherent talking videos; and 3) supporting the continuous view-point control and 3D consistency.

Early attempts towards addressing audio-driven human synthesis often concentrate on the generation of mouth region coherent with guiding audio signals, neglecting other body parts . Benefiting from the progress of face reenactment methods , producing full-head talking videos derived from single portrait images has been widely explored . Recently, Corona et al. \[\] further presents an approach that leverages a single portrait image and an audio clip to synthesize talking human videos. This is achieved by firstly mapping audio input to corresponding body gestures and facial expressions, followed by rendering these predicted motions utilizing advanced human video generation methodologies . Although achieving remarkable audio-conditional image-to-video synthesis, it frequently generates unrealistic or blurry visual artifacts in the face and hand regions and does not explore view controllability.

We present LLM-Talker, a novel framework that aims for audio-driven, view-controllable human video synthesis. To enhance the generation capabilities of Diffusion-based Models in human limb details and human body novel-view synthesis, we integrate Mixture-of-Experts (MoE) modules, including mask-MoE and view-MoE, to inject prior information into the generation network. The mask-MoE is responsible for depicting different body parts in human images, where each expert is guided by a segmented region mask. This enables the model to distinguish different image parts and improves the rendering quality of the generated speaking avatar. The view-MoE is responsible for depicting human appearance from different camera perspectives. Specifically, we assign a specific viewpoint to each expert, and the diffusion rendering network can effectively fuse the appearance information embedded in each expert, according to the distance between the given viewpoint and each viewpoint. View-MoE plays a key role in maintaining consistency across viewpoints.

Moreover, existing audio-driven human motion generation approaches posit that body movements are mainly correlated to low-level rhythmic audio features. In contrast, recent studies have emphasized the critical role of high-level semantic content in enriching and diversifying body gesture generation. Consequently, this oversimplified hypothesis leads to a restricted variety in the produced motion sequences. In contrast, we propose integrating powerful large language model (LLM) priors within our audio-driven gesture synthesis framework. Aligning speech features with linguistic representations bolsters our comprehension of the contextual nuances inherent within the spoken content. Specifically, our methodology first employs a pre-trained audio feature extractor to distill high-dimensional semantic information from the input speech, subsequently projecting these features into the latent space of large language models. Subsequently, these language-aligned features are utilized by large language models to augment semantic understanding. Finally, the enriched semantic features serve as conditional inputs to diffusion models, guiding the generation of gestures. The incorporation of large language model priors empowers our system with the capability to synthesize gestures that are not only coherent with the paired audio but also exhibit an elevated degree of stability and diversity.

To ensure the generalization ability of our framework, we also present a large-scale High-definition Audio-Visual dataset (HDAV) containing 4023 identities with 2D human keypoint annotations and detail property labels.

In summary, our major contributions are as follows:

- We present a framework designed for the one-shot generation of high-fidelity talking human videos, surpassing the performance of existing methods.

- We leverage the powerful large language model priors to guide cross-modal translation and elevate the diversity of gesture generation.

- We devise a mask-guided MoE module, which improves the overall visual rendering quality and stability by enhancing the distinction among varying human image regions.

- We release a large-scale dataset that significantly lowers the hurdles associated with training robust models for human video generation.

# RELATED WORK

## Co-speech Gesture Generation.

Pioneering efforts in co-speech gesture generation typically rely on linguistic rules that map audio data to pre-defined gesture segments and generate gestures in a search-and-connect manner . However, rule-based methods entail substantial endeavors in collecting gesture data and designing intricate rules. To address this limitation, researchers explore learning the mapping relationship between audio and gestures using deep neural networks . With the aim of enhancing expressiveness in generated outputs, researchers propose to predict facial landmarks in addition to body gestures , thus enriching the realism and emotional nuance in the synthesized content. These methods typically model co-gesture generation in a deterministic manner and ignore the many-to-many relationship between speech and gesture, leading to challenges in achieving a precise alignment of meaning between the generated gestures and the driven audio . In response to this challenge, methods based on probabilistic models have been widely explored . In pursuit of high-quality visual realism, researchers further propose unified frameworks designed to synthesize co-speech gesture videos under audio guidance. However, most methods simply hypothesize that human skeletons are mainly correlated with rhythm and beat and generate human skeletons based on only low-dimensional rhythm features, which limits their expressive capability. To fill this gap, researchers explore involving both rhythmic and semantic features to enhance the coherence between generated gestures and the associated speech content . Nevertheless, these methodologies learn distinctions between low-level rhythmic elements and high-level semantic features within a narrow dataset scope, consequently yielding sub-optimal results. Capitalizing on the recent progress in employing large language model (LLM) priors to facilitate cross-modal alignment tasks , we view co-gesture generation as a language translation problem and borrow the power of LLM priors to significantly enhance the fidelity and diversity of gesture synthesis.

## Video Generation.

The synthesis of video content has been approached using a variety of frameworks, including autoregressive transformers , recurrent neural networks , normalizing flows , and generative adversarial networks . Benefiting from the robust and scalable training objective, diffusion models have shown their advantages in video generation . Voleti *et. al.* \[\] devise a general framework that simultaneously enables video generation, prediction, and interpolation using a probabilistic conditional score-based denoising diffusion model. Harvey *et. al.* \[\] introduce a generative model that can produce long-duration video by generating sparse frames first, followed by the interpolation of the missing frames to ensure a smooth and continuous visual narrative. Hu *et. al.* \[\] propose to merge detailed features via spatial attention and generate high-quality human videos with human skeleton sequences as conditional input. Corona *et. al.* \[\] make the first attempt to bridge the gap between video diffusion models and talking-human generation. However, these methods typically render the whole image uniformly, while ignoring the specific properties of different image regions. Inspired by the success of Mixture of Experts (MoE) , we incorporate a mask-guided MoE module within the diffusion framework, thereby improving the differentiation among diverse image regions. This strategic integration enhances not only the quality of generated outputs but also improves the stability of the generation process.

# Method

This section presents LLM-Talker, a novel method that enables the generation of a speaking individual depending on the provided audio and a single reference image. The pipeline of the overall framework is depicted in Figure <a href="#fig:main" data-reference-type="ref" data-reference="fig:main">1</a>. We first introduce how to employ pre-trained large language models to establish a sophisticated mapping between raw audio and human skeleton sequences in Section <a href="#Method_audio" data-reference-type="ref" data-reference="Method_audio">3.1</a>. Then, we describe the process of rendering the generated human motion into visually authentic speaking videos in Section <a href="#Method_render" data-reference-type="ref" data-reference="Method_render">3.2</a>. In this section, we introduce the design of our proposed mask-guided Mixture of Experts (MoE) module, which aims at augmenting both the authenticity and stability of the synthesized video output, thereby pushing the boundaries of video generation quality. Finally, we describe the detail property of the proposed large-scale human video dataset in Section <a href="#Method_dataset" data-reference-type="ref" data-reference="Method_dataset">3.3</a>.

Given a single portrait image and a sequential audio input $`\textbf{a} = [a_1, ..., a_N]`$, our method aims to synthesize a coherent talking video sequence $`\textbf{x}`$ = $`[x_1, ..., x_N]`$, which not only ensures synchronization of lip motions with the speech but also incorporates body gestures that are harmoniously aligned with the nuances of the driving audio, thereby achieving a highly immersive and naturalistic visual-audio experience. As shown in Figure <a href="#fig:main" data-reference-type="ref" data-reference="fig:main">1</a>, our approach accomplishes this in a two-stage process. Firstly, we leverage powerful large language models to convert the audio sequence $`\textbf{a}`$ into a series of per-frame human skeletal representations, denoted as $`\textbf{p} = [p_1, ..., p_N]`$. Following this, we employ the video diffusion technique to render these skeletal frames $`\textbf{p}`$ into photo-realistic talking video frames $`\textbf{x}`$.

## LLM-based Audio-Driven Motion Generation

Most prior studies posit that body gestures mainly correlate with speech rhythm and can be adequately produced using low-dimensional rhythm features. Conversely, drawing inspiration from the remarkable success of large language models (LLMs) in bridging different modalities , we aspire to elevate the diversity of our generated motion sequences in a similar way. Specifically, our approach firstly extracts high-level semantic features of audio input, then aligns these features with the textual feature domain, where the semantic latent space is comprehensible to the core LLM.

To extract high-level semantic audio features, we first utilize the pre-trained wav2vec2.0 speech model . This model is comprised of an audio feature extractor and a subsequent transformer encoder. The feature extractor contains a series of temporal convolutional layers, designed to process raw audio inputs $`\textbf{a}`$ and transform them into a series of feature vectors. Following this, a transformer encoder, constructed from multiple stacked multi-head attention layers, refines these feature vectors, imbuing them with contextual information to yield a sequence of contextualized latent speech representations.

These high-level semantic features, however, cannot be sent to large language models directly due to inherent domain gaps and require further processing. To bridge this domain gap effectively, we employ a projection network that serves as a semantic translator, transforming the rich semantic content encapsulated within the high-level speech features into a representation that is more consistent with the textual latent space understood by the LLM. Given that these high-level speech features contain a wealth of semantic information, a minor project network is adequate for generating features aligned with LLM. This streamlined approach ensures the efficient semantic congruence without necessitating excessive computational complexity.

To further enrich the semantic correlation and improve the feature quality, we adopt Flan-T5-Base model as language model backbone and fine-tune this model to co-gesture generation task using LoRA , written as:
``` math
\begin{equation}
    \textbf{z}= \mathcal{E}_t(\mathcal{M}(\mathcal{E}_a(\textbf{a})))
\end{equation}
```
where $`\mathcal{E}_t`$ indicates the pre-trained T5 model encoder, $`\mathcal{M}`$ the projection network, $`\mathcal{E}_a`$ the pre-trained audio encoder, and $`\textbf{z} = [z_1, ..., z_N]`$ indicates the enriched latent feature. Notably, our methodology incorporates only the encoder component of T5, a powerful pre-trained model, with the objective of refining feature representations.

In the end, we opt to train a diffusion network to decode **z** to pose sequences **p**, underpinned by the recognition that probabilistic models excel in handling generation process, thereby enhancing the overall outputs authenticity and coherence. Similar to previous works , we train a denoising diffusion probabilistic models which progressively adds gaussian noise $`\bm{\epsilon}`$ to ground-truth samples $`\textbf{p}_0`$:
``` math
\begin{equation}
    % \mathcal{L}_d = \mathbbm{E} (|| \bm{\epsilon} - \bm{\epsilon}_{\theta}(\sqrt{{\hat{a}_t}}\textbf{p}_0 + \sqrt{1 - \hat{a}_t} \bm{\epsilon}, \textbf{z}, t)||)
    \mathcal{L}_p = \mathbbm{E}_{\epsilon, \textbf{p}_t, \textbf{c}, t} (|| \bm{\epsilon} - \bm{\epsilon}_{\theta}(\textbf{p}_{t}, \textbf{c}, t)||)
\end{equation}
```
where $`\textbf{p}_{t}`$ is the noisy pose sequences at timestep *t*, $`\textbf{c}`$ consists of latent feature $`\textbf{z}`$ and initial pose $`p_1`$. By doing so, we adopt the capabilities of pre-trained large language models to facilitate a richer and more sophisticated generation of gestures, thereby enhancing the overall expressiveness and naturalness of the synthesized outputs.

## Masked-guided MoE-based Human Rendering

Generating temporal consistency videos with photo-realistic details is an important yet challenging task. Capitalizing on the recent breakthroughs in video diffusion models , methodologies for human video generation grounded in stable diffusion processes have garnered extensive exploration . Nonetheless, these systems render the complete image uniformly depending on the U-net backbone, inadvertently disregarding the unique characteristics inherent in distinct body regions. To tackle this challenge, we introduce a mask-guided Mixture-of-Experts (MoE) mechanism designed to accommodate the distinctive traits of varying image segments.

Specifically, we address this challenge through a structured approach: the entire image is segmented into three distinct parts termed as face, body, and background by leveraging the facial landmarks and human gestures detected by DWPose . Subsequently, the uniform dense feed-forward network layers within the U-Net architecture are decomposed into three separate sparse expert layers, each acquiring the corresponding region mask as a latent feature mask and acting as a specialist catering to the unique demands of its respective image portion. Considering the instability issues commonly encountered with detected masks and their unavailability during inference (Figure <a href="#fig:mask" data-reference-type="ref" data-reference="fig:mask">2</a>), we devise an auxiliary lightweight module trained to estimate human masks directly from skeletal data. This module comprises a dual-encoder and a single decoder. The dual-encoder setup is intended to process human mask images and human skeleton images separately, while the decoder consolidates this encoded information to reconstruct human masks. By generating high-quality masks and incorporating this information into the temporal module of U-net through MoE, the rendering model is able to distinguish the background, body, and face parts, thus improving the temporal consistency and stability of output videos.

Throughout the training phase, the mask encoder, denoted as $`\mathcal{E}_m`$, processes the $`i`$-th human mask $`\textbf{m}_i`$, while the concurrent skeleton encoder, $`\mathcal{E}_p`$, handles both the $`i`$-th and $`j`$-th skeletons $`\textbf{p}_i`$ and $`\textbf{p}_j`$. The resultant triplet of the encoded representations is then fed into the decoder, $`\mathcal{D}`$, which leverages this fused information with the objective of accurately predicting the $`j`$-th human mask $`\textbf{m}_j`$:
``` math
\begin{equation}
    \bm{{\hat{m}}_j} = \mathcal{D}(\mathcal{E}_m(\bm{m}_i), \mathcal{E}_p(\bm{p}_i), \mathcal{E}_p(\bm{p}_j))
\end{equation}
```
``` math
\begin{equation}
    \mathcal{L}_m =  \mathbbm{E}_i(\mathcal{L}_{kl}(\bm{m}_i, \mathcal{E}_m, \mathcal{E}_p) + \mathcal{L}_{rec}(\bm{m}_i, \bm{{\hat{m}}_i}, \mathcal{D}))
\end{equation}
```
where $`\mathcal{L}_{rec}`$ represents mean square error loss and $`\mathcal{L}_{kl}`$ represents Kullback-Leibler loss. Through this strategic partitioning and specialized attention, our model achieves a heightened capability to retain and separately process diverse semantic elements within an image. The training of pose-guided diffusion render network can be formulated as follows:
``` math
\begin{equation}
    \mathcal{L}_x = \mathbbm{E}_{\epsilon, \textbf{x}_t, \textbf{p}, \textbf{m}, t} (|| \bm{\epsilon} - \bm{\epsilon}_{\theta}(\textbf{x}_{t}, \textbf{p}, \textbf{m}, t)||)
\end{equation}
```
where $`\textbf{x}_{t}`$ is the noisy human images at timestep *t*. By enhancing the differentiation among image regions, our framework fosters enhanced rendering consistency and overall stability, ultimately yielding improved visual outputs.

## Dataset

Recent advancements in diffusion models have greatly facilitated the progress of one-shot human video synthesis . Nonetheless, these models typically necessitate substantial training data and heavily rely on large-scale human video datasets comprising a vast number of distinct identities to guarantee their stability and generalizability. However, there are no publicly available datasets that contain enough identities for learning such a robust model, which largely blocks the development of this area. To fill this gap, we propose a High-definition Audio-Visual dataset (HDAV), which consists of 4,023 character video clips and the corresponding detail annotation. Specifically, we first collect 6,258 online videos from internet and 1325 videos from existing datasets. We adopt a cut detection pipeline [^1] to detect video jumps and unify the frame rate based on moviepy [^2]. A skeleton detector is further adopted to crop the human region. Next, we manually annotate the detailed properties of each video such as gender and clothes type, and remove invalid videos, resulting in 4,023 character videos with an average length of 8-15s. The comparisons with existing datasets are shown in Table <a href="#tab:dataset" data-reference-type="ref" data-reference="tab:dataset">1</a>. The raw video link, the processed pose skeletons, and the detail property labels will be released upon acceptance.

## Ablation Studies

In this part, we conduct an ablation study to evaluate the impact of the key components in our framework. Specifically, we consider three framework variations, *i.e.*, our framework without large language model priors (w/o LLM), our framework without mask-guided mixture of experts module (w/o MoE), and our framework without mask prediction and training with the detected mask (w/o MP). Table <a href="#tab:ablation_gesture" data-reference-type="ref" data-reference="tab:ablation_gesture">5</a> illustrates that the integration of large language model priors significantly bolsters the motion quality. The absence of these priors notably degrades from both the diversity of generated motions and the holistic output excellence, emphasizing their pivotal role.

Furthermore, as evidenced in Table <a href="#tab:ablation_Moe" data-reference-type="ref" data-reference="tab:ablation_Moe">4</a>, the integration of the mask-guided MoE module alongside the mask prediction module is observed to have a positively beneficial effect on the overall quality of the generated content. Specifically, without the region segmentation mask involved in the mask-guided MoE module, the performance of the rendering model notably declines, particularly as evaluated by the BRISQUE metric, which directly assesses local image regions and texture quality in the spatial domain. This evidences that our mask-guided MoE module, by incorporating mask information, enhances the distinction among different regions of human images, thereby effectively improving rendering quality and stability.

We also observe that training with our predicted masks yields better performance in comparison with training with solely detected masks. This improvement stems from the fact that detected masks can introduce misleading guidance, due to the inaccuracies and instability inherent in matting algorithms. Conversely, our mask generation module incorporates human skeletons as conditional inputs, thereby enhancing the stability and quality of the mask guidance, which in turn, leads to an overall performance improvement.

# Discussion and Conclusion

**Limitations** The constraints of our methodology can be condensed into two primary points. First, our model encounters challenges in consistently generating natural and stable hand results, particularly when faced with scenarios involving significant occlusions of hand movements. These extremes in hand gestures can lead to degradation in the quality and realism of the generated output. Second, since our system relies on a single portrait image to synthesize talking videos, it inherently adheres to a single view of the individual, thereby failing to establish a comprehensive representation of the identity. This limitation may introduce biases into the rendering process. Consequently, the result videos might occasionally exhibit inconsistencies that could impair the overall viewing experience.

**Social Impact** Our technology empowers the creation of high-quality talking human videos coherent with specify audio and single portrait image. However, it could potentially be misused to generate deceptive or misleading content. Thus, it is imperative to proactively consider and implement stringent safeguards against such malicious applications prior to any deployment.

**Conclusion** This paper introduces LLM-talker, a one-shot talking human synthesis framework. By leveraging the power of large language model priors in guiding cross-modal translation, we enhance the diversity and semantic alignment of co-gesture generation. In order to improve the temporal consistency of rendering videos, we further propose a mask-guided MoE module to guide the render model in distinguishing varying image parts. This is complemented by an additional variational autoencoder (VAE) network, trained specifically to predict detailed human masks from skeletal data, which not only improves the accuracy and stability of guided masks at training time but also enables mask guidance at inference time. Acknowledging the hurdles in training human video generation systems, we also unveil a comprehensive human video dataset comprising 4,023 unique identities, each meticulously annotated with detailed properties. This dataset aims to reduce barriers and facilitate advancements in the field. We believe that our LLM-talker framework and the accompanying dataset will have profound implications across a spectrum of downstream applications, notably in augmented and virtual reality domains, by enabling more immersive and natural interactions.
