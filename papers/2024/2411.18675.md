# GaussianSpeech: Audio-Driven Gaussian Avatars

Shivangi Aneja¹     Artem Sevastopolsky¹     Tobias Kirschstein¹     Justus Thies^(2,3)\
    Angela Dai¹     Matthias Nießner¹\
¹Technical University of Munich  ²MPI-IS, Tübingen  ³TU Darmstadt

###### Abstract

We introduce GaussianSpeech¹¹1Project Page: [https://shivangi-aneja.github.io/projects/gaussianspeech](https://shivangi-aneja.github.io/projects/gaussianspeech), a novel approach that synthesizes high-fidelity animation sequences of photo-realistic, personalized 3D human head avatars from spoken audio. To capture the expressive, detailed nature of human heads, including skin furrowing and finer-scale facial movements, we propose to couple speech signal with 3D Gaussian splatting to create realistic, temporally coherent motion sequences. We propose a compact and efficient 3DGS-based avatar representation that generates expression-dependent color and leverages wrinkle- and perceptually-based losses to synthesize facial details, including wrinkles that occur with different expressions. To enable sequence modeling of 3D Gaussian splats with audio, we devise an audio-conditioned transformer model capable of extracting lip and expression features directly from audio input. Due to the absence of high-quality dataset of talking humans in correspondence with audio, we captured a new large-scale multi-view dataset of audio-visual sequences of talking humans with native English accents and diverse facial geometry. GaussianSpeech consistently achieves state-of-the-art quality with visually natural motion, while encompassing diverse facial expressions and styles.

## 1 Introduction

Generating animated sequences of photorealistic 3D head avatars from spoken audio is important for many graphics applications, including immersive telepresence, movies, and virtual assistants. In particular, rendering photorealistic views of such animated avatars from various viewpoints is crucial for realistic, immersive digital media, for instance, telepresence to a meeting room requires a photorealistic appearance for all viewpoints of the people in the room, or AR/VR where users can freely change their viewpoint.

Creating such photorealistic animated 3D avatars from audio remains challenging, as it requires maintaining photorealistic fidelity throughout the animation sequence, as well as from various viewpoints. Existing work thus focuses on addressing these objectives independently; various works focus on re-enacting videos in the 2D domain \[[21](https://arxiv.org/html/2411.18675v1#bib.bib21), [72](https://arxiv.org/html/2411.18675v1#bib.bib72), [46](https://arxiv.org/html/2411.18675v1#bib.bib46), [69](https://arxiv.org/html/2411.18675v1#bib.bib69), [3](https://arxiv.org/html/2411.18675v1#bib.bib3), [70](https://arxiv.org/html/2411.18675v1#bib.bib70), [32](https://arxiv.org/html/2411.18675v1#bib.bib32), [48](https://arxiv.org/html/2411.18675v1#bib.bib48), [40](https://arxiv.org/html/2411.18675v1#bib.bib40), [9](https://arxiv.org/html/2411.18675v1#bib.bib9), [33](https://arxiv.org/html/2411.18675v1#bib.bib33)\], creating front-view video animations, while others focus on animating 3D face geometry from audio \[[44](https://arxiv.org/html/2411.18675v1#bib.bib44), [18](https://arxiv.org/html/2411.18675v1#bib.bib18), [67](https://arxiv.org/html/2411.18675v1#bib.bib67), [55](https://arxiv.org/html/2411.18675v1#bib.bib55)\]. In contrast, we aim to create innately 3D audio-driven avatars enabling 3D-consistent, free-viewpoint photorealistic synthesis needed for immersive digital communication.

In order to characterize audio-driven 3D animation of a person from multi-view input, we propose to represent animated head sequences with explicit 3D Gaussian points, leveraging the detailed and expressive representation space of 3D Gaussian Splatting (3DGS) \[[30](https://arxiv.org/html/2411.18675v1#bib.bib30)\]. 3DGS offers a flexible representation capable of handling complex and irregular facial geometry and appearance (e.g., different skin tones, beard, skin creasing) and real-time rendering, making it a well-suited choice for facial animation.

Thus, we design an efficient, personalized 3D Gaussian avatar representation from multi-view input observations of a person, containing relatively few Gaussian splats in order to make sequence modeling of photorealistic 3DGS tractable and allowing us to operate at real-time rendering rates. This is achieved through learning expression- and view-dependent color, and our losses focusing on perceptual face quality using a face recognition network, as well as focusing on fine-scale details through wrinkle detection.

Our efficient, high-quality avatar can handle the nuances of the facial geometry, like skin tone variation and dynamic wrinkles. We then use this person-specific avatar to guide audio-driven head animation, enabled by our transformer-based sequence model. We learn lip motion features and wrinkle features directly from audio to obtain expression input to train our transformer model, enabling photorealistic generation of a coherent animation sequence.

To create high-fidelity, audio-driven animated 3D head avatars, we require high-resolution multi-view data paired with high-quality audio recordings. Existing multiview datasets \[[62](https://arxiv.org/html/2411.18675v1#bib.bib62), [31](https://arxiv.org/html/2411.18675v1#bib.bib31)\] unfortunately lack either high-quality video or high-quality audio captures. In the absence of large-scale and high-quality paired audio-multiview data of people speaking, we collected a new multiview dataset with 16 cameras for 6 native English participants captured at 30 fps and 3208x2200 resolution with overall recordings of $`\sim`$3.5 hours, an order of magnitude larger than the existing datasets. We will make the dataset and the corresponding 3D face trackings publicly available for research purposes.

In this work, we introduce a novel approach for high-fidelity and multi-view consistent sequence animation of photorealistic 3D head avatars for content creation applications. To summarize, our contributions are:

- •
  The first transformer-based sequence model for audio-driven head animation synthesis of a lightweight 3DGS based avatar. By animating our optimized 3DGS avatar directly with our transformer model, we achieve temporally coherent animation sequences while characterizing fine-scale face details and speaker-specific style.
- •
  A new high-quality audio-video dataset, comprising high-resolution 16-view dataset of 6 native English speakers (Standard American & British). The dataset has a total of 2500 sequences, with overall recordings of $`\sim`$3.5 hours.

## 2 Related Work

Audio-driven facial animation plays an important role in digital media. Here we discuss audio-driven animation methods generating different output representations.

### 2.1 2D-Based Methods.

There is a large corpus of works in the field of 2D audio-driven facial animation operating on monocular RGB videos, synthesizing 2D sequences directly \[[57](https://arxiv.org/html/2411.18675v1#bib.bib57), [38](https://arxiv.org/html/2411.18675v1#bib.bib38), [63](https://arxiv.org/html/2411.18675v1#bib.bib63), [68](https://arxiv.org/html/2411.18675v1#bib.bib68), [61](https://arxiv.org/html/2411.18675v1#bib.bib61), [20](https://arxiv.org/html/2411.18675v1#bib.bib20), [8](https://arxiv.org/html/2411.18675v1#bib.bib8), [11](https://arxiv.org/html/2411.18675v1#bib.bib11), [64](https://arxiv.org/html/2411.18675v1#bib.bib64), [5](https://arxiv.org/html/2411.18675v1#bib.bib5), [51](https://arxiv.org/html/2411.18675v1#bib.bib51), [41](https://arxiv.org/html/2411.18675v1#bib.bib41), [6](https://arxiv.org/html/2411.18675v1#bib.bib6), [28](https://arxiv.org/html/2411.18675v1#bib.bib28), [74](https://arxiv.org/html/2411.18675v1#bib.bib74), [59](https://arxiv.org/html/2411.18675v1#bib.bib59), [60](https://arxiv.org/html/2411.18675v1#bib.bib60), [73](https://arxiv.org/html/2411.18675v1#bib.bib73), [14](https://arxiv.org/html/2411.18675v1#bib.bib14), [7](https://arxiv.org/html/2411.18675v1#bib.bib7), [26](https://arxiv.org/html/2411.18675v1#bib.bib26), [23](https://arxiv.org/html/2411.18675v1#bib.bib23), [47](https://arxiv.org/html/2411.18675v1#bib.bib47), [50](https://arxiv.org/html/2411.18675v1#bib.bib50), [22](https://arxiv.org/html/2411.18675v1#bib.bib22), [66](https://arxiv.org/html/2411.18675v1#bib.bib66)\]. However, these methods operate in pixel space and can produce very limited side views. Another line of work also operating on frontal RGB videos but using intermediate 3D representations are based on 3DMMs \[[71](https://arxiv.org/html/2411.18675v1#bib.bib71), [49](https://arxiv.org/html/2411.18675v1#bib.bib49), [56](https://arxiv.org/html/2411.18675v1#bib.bib56), [25](https://arxiv.org/html/2411.18675v1#bib.bib25), [16](https://arxiv.org/html/2411.18675v1#bib.bib16), [52](https://arxiv.org/html/2411.18675v1#bib.bib52)\]. Although these methods generate photorealistic results, they use 3DMMs as a proxy to improve the animation quality and are still limited to frontal and limited side views. In contrast, we model head avatars with explicit 3D Gaussian points, thus, enabling simultaneous free-viewpoint rendering for different viewpoints which is critical for telepresence applications.

### 2.2 Parametric Model Based Methods.

Another promising line of work is to animate 3D facial geometry directly. A vast majority of these works model speech-conditioned animation for either artist-designed template meshes \[[29](https://arxiv.org/html/2411.18675v1#bib.bib29), [12](https://arxiv.org/html/2411.18675v1#bib.bib12), [44](https://arxiv.org/html/2411.18675v1#bib.bib44), [18](https://arxiv.org/html/2411.18675v1#bib.bib18), [67](https://arxiv.org/html/2411.18675v1#bib.bib67), [55](https://arxiv.org/html/2411.18675v1#bib.bib55), [13](https://arxiv.org/html/2411.18675v1#bib.bib13), [54](https://arxiv.org/html/2411.18675v1#bib.bib54)\] or blendshapes for 3D parametric head model \[[39](https://arxiv.org/html/2411.18675v1#bib.bib39), [1](https://arxiv.org/html/2411.18675v1#bib.bib1)\]. While these methods can faithfully match facial motion with the speech signal and can be rendered from different viewpoints, they do not model any appearance or texture information and cannot handle complex and irregular facial geometry. The synthesized animations, therefore, do not look realistic. Compared to these, our method optimizes a 3DGS-based avatar and models appearance using expression and view-dependent color, generating photorealistic results.

### 2.3 Radiance Fields Based Methods.

Recent speech-driven animation methods based on radiance fields \[[21](https://arxiv.org/html/2411.18675v1#bib.bib21), [69](https://arxiv.org/html/2411.18675v1#bib.bib69), [46](https://arxiv.org/html/2411.18675v1#bib.bib46), [35](https://arxiv.org/html/2411.18675v1#bib.bib35), [70](https://arxiv.org/html/2411.18675v1#bib.bib70), [32](https://arxiv.org/html/2411.18675v1#bib.bib32), [40](https://arxiv.org/html/2411.18675v1#bib.bib40)\] have gained popularity due to their ability to model directly from images. Neural Radiance Fields (NeRF) \[[37](https://arxiv.org/html/2411.18675v1#bib.bib37)\] possess the capability to render a scene from arbitrary viewpoints, however, existing audio-driven methods utilizing NeRF are designed for monocular videos.

Concurrent to ours, few recent works \[[9](https://arxiv.org/html/2411.18675v1#bib.bib9), [33](https://arxiv.org/html/2411.18675v1#bib.bib33), [24](https://arxiv.org/html/2411.18675v1#bib.bib24)\] leverage 3DGS \[[30](https://arxiv.org/html/2411.18675v1#bib.bib30)\] for generating audio-driven talking heads. GaussianTalker \[[9](https://arxiv.org/html/2411.18675v1#bib.bib9)\] and TalkingGaussian \[[33](https://arxiv.org/html/2411.18675v1#bib.bib33)\] focus on improving the rendering speed for monocular videos. EmoTalk3D \[[24](https://arxiv.org/html/2411.18675v1#bib.bib24)\] can synthesize multi-view renders, however these methods generate sequences frame-by-frame, thus suffer from jitter and scaling artefacts. In contrast, our method synthesizes multi-view consistent and temporally smooth results, including fine-scale details like dynamic wrinkles, by leveraging a transformer-based sequence model and an efficient 3DGS-based avatar.

## 3 Multi-View Audio-Visual Dataset

We collected a novel dataset consisting of six native English speakers captured using a multiview rig of 16 cameras (see Supp.). We record sequences at 30 FPS at 3208 x 2200 resolution. To achieve quality and diversity, we specifically capture native English speakers with different accents, including American, British, and Canadian. We selected participants aged 20-50 with different genders and facial geometry including beard and glasses to increase the diversity, see Fig. [2](https://arxiv.org/html/2411.18675v1#S3.F2 "Figure 2 ‣ 3 Multi-View Audio-Visual Dataset ‣ GaussianSpeech: Audio-Driven Gaussian Avatars"). We collected 415 sequences for every subject, leading to an overall recording time of 30-35 minutes for each of the 16 cameras. The spoken sentences are chosen from the TIMIT \[[19](https://arxiv.org/html/2411.18675v1#bib.bib19)\] corpus to maximize the phonetic diversity. Our dataset stands out from the existing datasets in terms of quality and quantity.

While certain datasets with audio-visual talking faces exist, they are limited in quality. The RAVDESS dataset \[[36](https://arxiv.org/html/2411.18675v1#bib.bib36)\] contains a set of native speakers, but it has only 2 unique sequences per participant with North American accent, while we captured three different English accents and 415 unique sentences. The MEAD dataset \[[62](https://arxiv.org/html/2411.18675v1#bib.bib62)\] captured the participants with 250 unique sentences per participant. However, they focus on emotional speech synthesis due to which they capture only 40 unique natural expression/emotion per participant at a relatively lower resolution. The Nersemble \[[31](https://arxiv.org/html/2411.18675v1#bib.bib31)\] dataset captures the participants at high resolution, but it only contains 10 audio sequences per participant. Closest to ours is MultiFace \[[65](https://arxiv.org/html/2411.18675v1#bib.bib65)\], which captured participants in a spherical rig of 150 cameras; however, it captured only 50 audio sequences per participant. Our dataset contains 415 sequences for every subject at high resolution, an order of magnitude larger than existing datasets, see Tab. [1](https://arxiv.org/html/2411.18675v1#S3.T1 "Table 1 ‣ 3 Multi-View Audio-Visual Dataset ‣ GaussianSpeech: Audio-Driven Gaussian Avatars"). ## 4 Method

Our method operates in two stages. First, we develop a lightweight and high-quality avatar initialization based on GaussianAvatars (Sec. [4.1](https://arxiv.org/html/2411.18675v1#S4.SS1 "4.1 Avatar Initialization ‣ 4 Method ‣ GaussianSpeech: Audio-Driven Gaussian Avatars")). Next, we train a transformer-based sequence model to animate our initialized avatar conditioned on personalized audio features (Sec. [4.2](https://arxiv.org/html/2411.18675v1#S4.SS2 "4.2 Sequence Model Training ‣ 4 Method ‣ GaussianSpeech: Audio-Driven Gaussian Avatars")). Since our method requires 3D face tracking, we compute them from our multiview sequence dataset, similar to \[[42](https://arxiv.org/html/2411.18675v1#bib.bib42)\].

### 4.1 Avatar Initialization

We propose an efficient optimization strategy to compute a 3DGS-based Gaussian avatar representation. We found that naively training GaussianAvatar \[[42](https://arxiv.org/html/2411.18675v1#bib.bib42)\] generates blurred/low-quality textures, especially, for scenarios with rapid facial movement like faster talking speed/head motion. In addition, GaussianAvatar can not effectively handle dynamic wrinkles. Therefore, we introduce expression-dependent colors and propose several regularizations to improve quality of our avatars described below and shown in Fig. [3](https://arxiv.org/html/2411.18675v1#S3.F3 "Figure 3 ‣ 3 Multi-View Audio-Visual Dataset ‣ GaussianSpeech: Audio-Driven Gaussian Avatars").

Volume-Based Pruning. We modify the pruning strategy used by GaussianAvatar. Instead of pruning 3D Gaussian splats based on a given opacity threshold $`\mathbf{\epsilon}_{\text{opacity}}`$, we select top 25,000 Gaussians with maximum opacity and 3D Gaussian’s scale volume combined at every pruning step as

```math
{\mathcal{G}_{i} = {\sigma_{i} \cdot {({s_{x} \cdot s_{y} \cdot s_{z}})}}},
```

where $`\sigma_{i}`$ refers to $`i^{th}`$ Gaussian’s opacity and $`s_{x},s_{y},s_{z}`$ refers to its scale along x, y, and z axis. Even when the optimization generates excessive splats during densification, this top-k pruning ensures that the optimized avatar does not contain too many 3D Gaussian splats. However, this leads to degradation in quality by removing small transparent 3D splats and generates blurry results. We, thus, propose to add additional regularizations to improve quality.

Expression-dependent Color. Instead of learning SH Color for 3D Gaussians, our method generates color with a lightweight two-layer color MLP $`{\mathbf{θ}}_{\text{color}}`$ to faithfully synthesize dynamic wrinkles. Given a FLAME \[[34](https://arxiv.org/html/2411.18675v1#bib.bib34)\] expression code $`\mathbf{ψ}`$ and viewing direction $`\mathbf{v}`$, we synthesize view- and expression-dependent color $`{\mathbf{c}}_{i}`$ as:

```math
{{\mathbf{c}}_{i} = {{\mathbf{θ}}_{\text{color}}{({\mathbf{ψ}};{\mathbf{z}}_{i};{\mathbf{v}})}}}.
```

Note that we additionally learn per Gaussian latent features $`{\mathbf{z}}_{i}`$ for sharper colors.

Perceptual Losses. To improve the sharpness of the color generated by $`{\mathbf{θ}}_{\text{color}}`$, we add a global and patch-based perceptual loss. The global perceptual loss $`\mathcal{L}_{\text{global}}`$ is based on the content and style features of the pre-trained face recognition model ArcFace \[[15](https://arxiv.org/html/2411.18675v1#bib.bib15)\]. The content loss $`\mathcal{L}_{\text{content}}`$ and style loss $`\mathcal{L}_{\text{style}}`$ are defined as:

$`\mathcal{L}_{\text{content}}`$$`{= {\sum\limits_{k = 1}^{K}{\|{{\phi_{k}{(I_{\text{render}})}} - {\phi_{k}{(I_{gt})}}}\|}_{1}}},`$$`\mathcal{L}_{\text{style}}`$$`{= {\sum\limits_{k = 1}^{K}{\|{{\mathcal{G}_{k}{(I_{\text{render}})}} - {\mathcal{G}_{k}{(I_{gt})}}}\|}_{1}}},`$

where $`\phi_{k}`$ and $`\mathcal{G}_{k}`$ refer to the feature maps and Gram matrices \[[27](https://arxiv.org/html/2411.18675v1#bib.bib27)\] for the layer $`k`$ respectively. $`I_{\text{render}}`$ and $`I_{\text{gt}}`$ refer to the rendered and ground-truth multiview image.

```math
{\mathcal{L}_{\text{global}} = {\mathcal{L}_{\text{content}} + \mathcal{L}_{\text{style}}}}.
```

$`\mathcal{L}_{\text{global}}`$ explained above improves the quality of the texture globally, however, it shows limited improvements for fine-scale skin areas and less observed regions like the mouth interior. We, therefore, employ a VGG-based loss on local image patches based on content features of the pre-trained VGG backbone as:

```math
{\mathcal{L}_{\text{patch}} = {\frac{1}{J}{\sum\limits_{j = 1}^{J}{\sum\limits_{k = 1}^{K}{\|{{\zeta_{k}{(I_{\text{render}}^{j})}} - {\zeta_{k}{(I_{\text{gt}}^{j})}}}\|}_{1}}}}},
```

where $`I_{\text{render}}^{j}`$ and $`I_{\text{gt}}^{j}`$ refer to the $`j^{th}`$ local patch regions from the rendered and ground-truth multiview images. We use $`128 \times 128`$ patches and sample 16 local patches uniformly for the facial area by employing alpha matting.

Wrinkle Regularization. Naive optimization of GaussianAvatar \[[42](https://arxiv.org/html/2411.18675v1#bib.bib42)\] cannot represent skin creasing and fine-scale wrinkles, since it learns a constant color for the avatar, irrespective of facial expression. To overcome this, we introduce a lightweight color MLP $`\theta_{color}`$ that can generate expression-dependent wrinkles. We employ a novel wrinkle feature loss $`\mathcal{L}_{\text{wrinkle}}`$ which focuses on refining dynamic wrinkles. Specifically, we run an off-the-shelf wrinkle detector \[[45](https://arxiv.org/html/2411.18675v1#bib.bib45)\] to extract wrinkle features and apply a content loss on its feature detection backbone during optimization:

```math
{\mathcal{L}_{\text{wrinkle}} = {\sum\limits_{k = 1}^{K}{\|{{\Psi_{k}{(I_{\text{render}})}} - {\Psi_{k}{(I_{\text{gt}})}}}\|}_{1}}}.
```

Note that our method synthesizes wrinkles faithfully for avatars whose captured data includes dynamic wrinkles when speaking; if the avatar did not display wrinkles during speech, our method will not generate them.

Mouth Region Subdivision. Since the mouth interior (especially teeth) is less frequently observed compared to other facial regions, the standard 3DGS-based densification cannot generate sufficient Gaussians for the mouth to synthesize high quality results. To address this, before optimization, we subdivide the triangles which are used to initialize the Gaussians corresponding to the teeth in the FLAME mesh using a uniform four-way subdivision. By doing so, we begin with a high density of Gaussians for the teeth, compensating for low gradient magnitude in this area, ensuring that teeth appear detailed and realistic.

To summarize, we optimize our 3DGS-based avatar using $`\mathcal{L}_{\text{total}}`$ loss as:

```math
\begin{array}{r}
{\mathcal{L}_{\text{total}} = {\mathcal{L}_{\text{rgb}} + {\lambda_{\text{pos}}\mathcal{L}_{\text{position}}} + {\lambda_{\text{s}}\mathcal{L}_{\text{scaling}}}}} \\
{{{+ {\lambda_{\text{g}}\mathcal{L}_{\text{global}}}} + {\lambda_{\text{p}}\mathcal{L}_{\text{patch}}} + {\lambda_{\text{w}}\mathcal{L}_{\text{wrinkle}}}},}
\end{array}
```

where $`\mathcal{L}_{\text{rgb}},\mathcal{L}_{\text{position}},\mathcal{L}_{\text{scaling}}`$ are defined in \[[42](https://arxiv.org/html/2411.18675v1#bib.bib42)\] (also explained in Supp. doc).

![Refer to caption](https://arxiv.org/html/x4.png)

Figure 4: Method Overview. From the given speech signal, GaussianSpeech uses Wav2Vec 2.0 \[[2](https://arxiv.org/html/2411.18675v1#bib.bib2)\] encoder to extract generic audio features and maps them to personalized lip feature embeddings $`{\mathbf{c}}^{1:T}`$ with Lip Transformer Encoder and wrinkle features $`{\mathbf{w}}^{1:T}`$ with Wrinkle Transformer Encoder. Next, the Expression Encoder synthesizes FLAME expressions $`{\mathbf{e}}^{1:T}`$ which are then projected via Expression2Latent MLP and concatenated with $`{\mathbf{c}}^{1:T}`$ for input to the motion decoder. The motion decoder employs a multi-head transformer decoder \[[58](https://arxiv.org/html/2411.18675v1#bib.bib58)\] consisting of Multihead Self-Attention, Cross-Attention, and Feed Forward layers. The concatenated lip-expression features are fused into the decoder via cross-attention layers with alignment mask $`\mathcal{M}`$. The decoder then predicts FLAME vertex offsets $`{\{{\mathbf{V}}_{\text{offset}}\}}^{1:T}`$ which gets added to the template mesh $`\mathbf{T}`$ to generate vertex animation in canonical space. During training, these are then fed to our optimized 3DGS avatar (Sec. [4.1](https://arxiv.org/html/2411.18675v1#S4.SS1 "4.1 Avatar Initialization ‣ 4 Method ‣ GaussianSpeech: Audio-Driven Gaussian Avatars")) and the color MLP $`{\mathbf{θ}}_{\text{color}}`$ and gaussian latents $`\mathbf{z}`$ are further refined via re-rendering losses  \[[30](https://arxiv.org/html/2411.18675v1#bib.bib30)\].

### 4.2 Sequence Model Training

GaussianSpeech performs high-fidelity and temporally-consistent generative synthesis of avatar motion sequences, conditioned on audio signal. To characterize complex face motions and fine-scale movements like dynamic wrinkles, we employ a transformer-based sequence model. We predict mesh animations with our sequence model and refine the dynamic motion attributes of the 3D Gaussian Splats of our optimized avatar to be consistent with audio features. An overview of our approach is illustrated in Fig. [4](https://arxiv.org/html/2411.18675v1#S4.F4 "Figure 4 ‣ 4.1 Avatar Initialization ‣ 4 Method ‣ GaussianSpeech: Audio-Driven Gaussian Avatars").

Audio Encoding. We employ the state-of-the-art pre-trained speech model Wav2Vec 2.0 \[[2](https://arxiv.org/html/2411.18675v1#bib.bib2)\] to encode the audio signal. Specifically, we use the audio feature extractor made up of temporal convolution layers (TCN) to extract audio feature vectors $`{\{ a_{i}\}}_{i = 1}^{N_{a}}`$ from the raw waveform, followed by a Frequency Interpolation layer to align the input audio signal $`{\{ a_{i}\}}_{i = 1}^{N_{a}}`$ (captured at frequency $`f_{a}`$ = 16kHz) with our dataset $`{\{ a_{i}\}}_{i = 1}^{N_{e}}`$ (captures at framerate $`f_{e}`$ = 30FPS).

Lip Features. A stacked multi-layer Lip Transformer Encoder processes these resampled audio features and predicts personalized lip content feature vectors $`{\mathbf{c}}^{1:T}`$. To avoid learning spurious correlation between upper face motion and audio, the Lip Transformer Encoder is trained with only lip vertices from the FLAME mesh with L2-reconstruction loss autoregressively as:

```math
{\mathcal{L}_{\text{lip}} = {\sum\limits_{n = 1}^{N}\left( {\sum\limits_{t = 1}^{T}{\|{{\mathbf{l}}_{\text{gt}}^{t} - {\mathbf{l}}_{\text{pred}}^{t}}\|}_{2}} \right)_{n}}},
```

where $`T`$ refers to the number of frames per sequence and $`N`$ total sequences, $`{\mathbf{l}}_{\text{gt}}`$ and $`{\mathbf{l}}_{\text{pred}}`$ refer to the ground truth and predicted lip vertices, respectively.

Wrinkle Features. Similarly, our Wrinkle Transformer Encoder conditioned on audio and lip features predicts personalized wrinkle feature vectors $`{\mathbf{w}}^{1:T}`$. The Wrinkle Transformer Encoder is trained with wrinkle features extracted using a wrinkle detector \[[45](https://arxiv.org/html/2411.18675v1#bib.bib45)\] from the RGB frames as:

```math
{\mathcal{L}_{\text{wrinkle}} = {\sum\limits_{n = 1}^{N}\left( {\sum\limits_{t = 1}^{T}{\|{{\mathbf{w}}_{\text{gt}}^{t} - {\mathbf{w}}_{\text{pred}}^{t}}\|}_{2}} \right)_{n}}},
```

where $`{\mathbf{w}}_{\text{gt}}`$ and $`{\mathbf{w}}_{\text{pred}}`$ refer to the ground truth and predicted wrinkle vertices respectively.

Expression Features. Using personalized lip features $`{\mathbf{c}}^{1:T}`$ and wrinkle features $`{\mathbf{w}}^{1:T}`$ obtained above, we train the Expression Encoder $`\mathcal{E}_{\text{exp}}`$. Specifically, we concatenate lip and wrinkle features to obtain combined features $`{{\mathbf{c}}{\mathbf{w}}^{1:T}} = \left\lbrack {\mathbf{c}}^{1:T};{\mathbf{w}}^{1:T} \right\rbrack`$. These combined features are fed to our Expression Encoder which predicts FLAME expressions as $`{\mathbf{e}}_{\text{pred}}^{1:T}`$ = $`\mathcal{E}_{\text{exp}}{({{\mathbf{c}}{\mathbf{w}}^{1:T}})}`$ and is trained with:

```math
{\mathcal{L}_{\text{expr}} = {\sum\limits_{n = 1}^{N}\left( {\sum\limits_{t = 1}^{T}{\|{{\mathbf{e}}_{\text{gt}}^{t} - {\mathbf{e}}_{\text{pred}}^{t}}\|}_{2}} \right)_{n}}},
```

where $`{\mathbf{e}}_{\text{gt}}`$ and $`{\mathbf{e}}_{\text{pred}}`$ refers to the ground truth and predicted FLAME expression parameters, respectively.

Audio-Conditioned Animation. We train a transformer decoder \[[58](https://arxiv.org/html/2411.18675v1#bib.bib58)\] network to synthesize mesh Vertex Offsets $`\left\{ {\mathbf{V}}_{\text{offset}} \right\}^{1:T}`$, where $`T`$ refers to the number of frames in a sequence. During training, we first project the predicted expression parameters $`{\mathbf{e}}_{\text{pred}}^{1:T}`$ via the Expression2Latent MLP $`\mathcal{E}`$ to the latent space of our model and concatenate it with lip features $`{\mathbf{c}}^{1:T}`$ to obtain combined lip-expression motion features $`{\mathbf{m}}^{1:T} = \left\lbrack {\mathbf{c}}^{1:T};{\mathcal{E}{({\mathbf{e}}^{1:T})}} \right\rbrack`$.

These motion features $`{\mathbf{m}}^{1:T}`$ are then processed through transformer decoder, and the Vertex Mapper MLP to synthesize Vertex Offsets $`\left\{ {\mathbf{V}}_{\text{offset}} \right\}^{1:T}`$ in canonical space. We leverage a look-ahead binary target mask $`\mathcal{T} \in {\mathbb{R}}^{N \times N}`$ in the multi-head self-attention layer to prevent the model from peeking into the future frames. The $`{(i,j)}^{th}`$ element of the matrix with $`{1 \leq i},{j \leq N}`$ is:

```math
\mathcal{T}_{ij} = \left\{ \begin{array}{lc}
{{{True}\quad{\text{if~}i}} \leq j} & \\
{{False}\quad\text{else}} &
\end{array} \right.
```

Input motion features $`{\mathbf{m}}^{1:T}`$ are fused into the transformer with the multi-head audio expression cross-attention layer via the alignment mask $`\mathcal{M}`$. The binary mask $`\mathcal{M} \in {\mathbb{R}}^{N \times N}`$ is a Kronecker delta function $`\delta_{ij}`$ such that the motion features for $`i^{th}`$ timestamp attend to vertex features at the $`j^{th}`$ timestamp if and only if $`i = j`$:

```math
\mathcal{M} = \delta_{ij} = \left\{ \begin{array}{lc}
{{{True}\quad{\text{if~}i}} = j} & \\
{{{False}\quad{\text{if~}i}} \neq j} &
\end{array} \right.
```

The vertex offsets are obtained as:

```math
{\left\{ {\mathbf{V}}_{\text{offset}} \right\}^{1:T} = {\mathcal{D}\left( {\mathbf{m}}^{1:T} \middle| {\mathcal{T},\mathcal{M}} \right)}},
```

where $`\mathcal{D}`$ refers to the transformer decoder network. These predicted offsets $`\left\{ {\mathbf{V}}_{\text{offset}} \right\}^{1:T}`$ are added to the template mesh $`\mathbf{T}`$ to obtain mesh animation in canonical space as:

```math
{\left\{ {\mathbf{V}}_{\text{pred}} \right\}^{1:T} = {{\mathbf{T}} + \left\{ {\mathbf{V}}_{\text{offset}} \right\}^{1:T}}}.
```

The Expression2Latent MLP $`\mathcal{E}`$ and the transformer decoder $`\mathcal{D}`$ are jointly trained with an L2-reconstruction loss:

```math
{\mathcal{L}_{\text{vertices}} = {\sum\limits_{n = 1}^{N}\left( {\sum\limits_{t = 1}^{T}{\|{{\mathbf{V}}_{\text{gt}}^{t} - {\mathbf{V}}_{\text{pred}}^{t}}\|}_{2}} \right)_{n}}},
```

The predicted vertices $`\left\{ {\mathbf{V}}_{\text{pred}} \right\}^{1:T}`$ are fed to our Optimized 3DGS avatar (Sec. [4.1](https://arxiv.org/html/2411.18675v1#S4.SS1 "4.1 Avatar Initialization ‣ 4 Method ‣ GaussianSpeech: Audio-Driven Gaussian Avatars")) and color related attributes of the avatar are further refined. We propose an alternating training strategy for the task as explained below.

\(a\) In the first step, we predict vertex displacements (from the rest pose) in the canonical space for the entire sequence (Eq. [14](https://arxiv.org/html/2411.18675v1#S4.E14 "Equation 14 ‣ 4.2 Sequence Model Training ‣ 4 Method ‣ GaussianSpeech: Audio-Driven Gaussian Avatars")). This learns the optimal parameters for transformer $`\mathcal{D}`$ and Expression2Latent MLP $`\mathcal{E}`$ as:

```math
{\mathcal{E}^{\ast},\mathcal{D}^{\ast}} = {\underset{\mathcal{E},\mathcal{D}}{\arg\min}\mathcal{L}_{\text{vertices}}}
```

\(b\) In the second step, we predict the 3D Gaussian attributes with our Optimized 3DGS avatar (Sec. [4.1](https://arxiv.org/html/2411.18675v1#S4.SS1 "4.1 Avatar Initialization ‣ 4 Method ‣ GaussianSpeech: Audio-Driven Gaussian Avatars")) and render the full animation sequence.

The color MLP $`{\mathbf{θ}}_{\text{color}}`$ of our optimized avatar is conditioned on predicted FLAME expression $`{\mathbf{e}}_{\text{pred}}`$ and per Gaussian latent $`{\mathbf{z}}_{i}`$, in addition to view direction $`\mathbf{v}`$, and predicts the view- and expression-dependent color as:

```math
{{\mathbf{c}}_{i} = {{\mathbf{θ}}_{\text{color}}{({\mathbf{e}}_{\text{pred}};{\mathbf{z}}_{i};{\mathbf{v}})}}}.
```

The predicted image $`I_{\text{pred}}`$ is obtained with the differentiable renderer $`\mathcal{R}`$ from Kerbl _et al_. \[[30](https://arxiv.org/html/2411.18675v1#bib.bib30)\] as:

```math
{I_{\text{pred}} = {\mathcal{R}\left( {\{{\mathbf{μ}}_{\mathbf{i}},{\mathbf{s}}_{\mathbf{i}},{\mathbf{q}}_{\mathbf{i}},{\mathbf{c}}_{\mathbf{i}}\}}^{1:G},{\lbrack\left. R \middle| t \right.\rbrack} \right)}},
```

where $`{\mathbf{μ}}_{\mathbf{i}},{\mathbf{s}}_{\mathbf{i}},{\mathbf{q}}_{\mathbf{i}}`$ refers to the optimized avatar’s position, scale, and rotations, respectively, and $`G`$ defines the total number of Gaussians. The predictions are supervised with the photometric loss $`\mathcal{L}_{\text{photo}}`$ for the sequence:

```math
{\mathcal{L}_{\text{photo}} = {\sum\limits_{t = 1}^{T}\left( {\mathcal{L}_{\text{rgb}} + {\lambda_{\text{g}}\mathcal{L}_{\text{global}}} + {\lambda_{\text{p}}\mathcal{L}_{\text{patch}}}} \right)_{t}}},
```

In this step, we refine per-Gaussian latents $`{\mathbf{z}}_{i}`$ and Color MLP $`{\mathbf{θ}}_{\text{color}}`$ with audio-conditioned expressions:

```math
{{\mathbf{θ}}_{\text{color}}^{\ast},{\{{\mathbf{z}}_{i}^{\ast}\}}^{1:G}} = {\underset{{\mathbf{θ}}_{\text{color}},{\{{\mathbf{z}}_{i}\}}^{1:G}}{\arg\min}\mathcal{L}_{\text{photo}}}
```

Overall, we optimize two losses in the alternating fashion: (a) $`\mathcal{L}_{\text{vertices}}`$ which learns audio-conditioned facial motion and (b) $`\mathcal{L}_{\text{photo}}`$ which refines the optimized avatar for more accurate and photorealistic appearance. We do not refine the position, scale, rotation, and opacity; empirically, we found that they did not make a noticeable difference in the overall quality.

## 5 Results

We evaluate GaussianSpeech on the tasks of (a) Avatar Representation and (b) Audio-Driven Animation. For (a), we evaluate standard perceptual image quality metrics SSIM, PSNR and LPIPS. For audio-driven animation, we evaluate lip synchronization LSE-D \[[41](https://arxiv.org/html/2411.18675v1#bib.bib41)\] as well as perceptual quality metrics. We train personalized avatars for different identities. Following GaussianAvatars \[[42](https://arxiv.org/html/2411.18675v1#bib.bib42)\], we train on all 15 cameras except the frontal and report results on the frontal camera for all our experiments. All images are resized to $`1604 \times 1100`$ during training. For avatar reconstruction, we use 30 short sequences. For audio-driven animation, we use 300 sequences for training and 50 for val and test set each. We encourage readers to watch the Supplementary Video for visual comparison of all results.

## 6 Conclusion

In this work, we propose a novel approach to create high-fidelity and photorealistic 3D head avatars that can be animated from audio input. We designed the first transformer-based sequence model for audio-driven head animation of 3DGS based avatar. Our sequence model is made possible by a lightweight and compact avatar initialization based on 3D Gaussian Splatting. We proposed several regularization techniques to handle dynamic wrinkles, skin creasing and sharpness of the texture. Our method produces (a) photorealistic and high-quality 3D head avatars that can be rendered from arbitrary viewpoints (b) visually natural animations like skin creasing during talking. We believe this is an important first step towards enabling the animation of detailed and lightweight 3D head avatar, which can enable many new possibilities for content creation and digital avatars for immersive telepresence.

Specifically, it represents a scene using a set of 3D Gaussian splats, each defined by a set of optimizable parameters, including a mean position $`{\mathbf{μ}} \in {\mathbb{R}}^{3}`$ and a positive semi-definite covariance matrix $`\Sigma \in {\mathbb{R}}^{3 \times 3}`$ as: `math {G({\mathbf{x}})} = e^{- {\frac{1}{2}{({{\mathbf{x}} - {\mathbf{μ}}})}^{T}\Sigma^{- 1}{({{\mathbf{x}} - {\mathbf{μ}}})}}}` Given that covariance matrix $`\Sigma`$ needs to be positive semidefinite to have physical meaning and gradient-based optimization methods cannot be constrained to produce such valid matrices, Kerbl _et al_. \[[30](https://arxiv.org/html/2411.18675v1#bib.bib30)\] first define an ellipsoid with scaling matrix $`S`$ and rotation matrix $`R`$ as: `math {\Sigma = {RSS^{T}R^{T}}}.` To allow for independent optimization for scale and rotation, separate 3D vectors are stored. The scale is represented using a scaling vector $`{\mathbf{s}} \in {\mathbb{R}}^{3}`$ and a quaternion $`{\mathbf{q}} \in {\mathbb{R}}^{4}`$ for rotation. For rendering every pixel on the image, the color $`\mathbf{C}`$ is computed by blending all the 3D Gaussians overlapping a pixels as: `math {\mathbf{C}} = {\sum\limits_{i = 1}^{N}{{\mathbf{c}}_{i}\alpha_{i}{\prod\limits_{j = 1}^{i - 1}\left( {1 - \alpha_{j}} \right)}}}` where $`{\mathbf{c}}_{i}`$ refers to 3-degree spherical harmonics (SH) \[[43](https://arxiv.org/html/2411.18675v1#bib.bib43)\] color obtained by blending $`N`$ ordered points overlapping the pixel, blending weight $`\alpha_{i}`$ is given by multiplying 2D projection of the 3D Gaussian with learnt per-point opacity. Paired with a differentiable tile rasterizer, this enables real-time rendering. To handle complex scenes and respect visibility order, depth-based sorting is applied to the Gaussian splats before blending. ### C.2 GaussianAvatars Due to the capability of 3DGS to represent fine geometric structures, it has proved to be an efficient representation for creating photorealistic head avatars, as shown by GaussianAvatars \[[42](https://arxiv.org/html/2411.18675v1#bib.bib42)\]. GaussianAvatars proposes a method for dynamic 3D representation of human heads based on 3DGS by rigging the anisotropic 3D Gaussians to the faces of a 3D morphable face model. Specifically, the method uses FLAME \[[34](https://arxiv.org/html/2411.18675v1#bib.bib34)\] as 3DMM due to its flexibility and compactness, consisting of only 5023 vertices and 9976 faces. To better represent mouth interior, it generated additional 120 vertices for teeth. Given a FLAME mesh, the idea is to first initialize a 3D Gaussian at the center of each triangle of the FLAME mesh and let the 3D Gaussian move with the faces of the FLAME mesh across different timesteps. For the paired 3D Gaussians with the faces of the FLAME mesh, the position $`\mathbf{μ}`$, rotation $`\mathbf{r}`$ and anisotropic scaling $`\mathbf{s}`$ are defined in local space. During rendering, these are converted to global space as: `math {{\mathbf{r}}' = {{\mathbf{R}}{\mathbf{r}}}},` `math {\mathbf{μ}}' = {{k{\mathbf{R}}{\mathbf{μ}}} + {\mathbf{T}}}` `math {{\mathbf{s}}' = {k{\mathbf{s}}}},` where $`\mathbf{T}`$ refers to the mean positions of the vertices of the triangle mesh, rotation matrix $`\mathbf{R}`$ describes the orientation of the triangles in the global space, scalar $`k`$ describes the triangle scaling. During avatar optimization, similar to 3DGS, the method uses adaptive density control strategy to add and remove splats based view-space positional gradient and opacity of each Gaussian. To prevent excessive pruning, the method also ensures that every triangle has at least one splat attached. The 3DGS parameters are then optimized using photometric loss $`\mathcal{L}_{\text{rgb}}`$, position loss $`\mathcal{L}_{\text{position}}`$ and scaling loss as $`\mathcal{L}_{\text{scale}}`$ as: `math \mathcal{L} = {\mathcal{L}_{\text{rgb}} + {\lambda_{\text{pos}}\mathcal{L}_{\text{position}}} + {\lambda_{\text{s}}\mathcal{L}_{\text{scaling}}}}` where $`\mathcal{L}_{\text{rgb}}`$ is combination of $`\mathcal{L}_{1}`$ and D-SSIM loss \[[30](https://arxiv.org/html/2411.18675v1#bib.bib30)\] between rendered and ground truth images. `math {\mathcal{L}_{\text{rgb}} = {{\left( {1 - \lambda} \right)\mathcal{L}_{\text{1}}} + {\lambda\mathcal{L}_{\text{D-SSIM}}}}},` $`\mathcal{L}_{\text{position}}`$ ensures that splats remain close to their parent triangles: `math {\mathcal{L}_{\text{position}} = {\|{\max\left( {\mathbf{μ}},\mathbf{\epsilon}_{\text{position}} \right)}\|}_{2}},` and $`\mathcal{L}_{\text{scaling}}`$ prevents excessive scaling of the splats: `math {\mathcal{L}_{\text{scaling}} = {\|{\max\left( {\mathbf{s}},\mathbf{\epsilon}_{\text{scaling}} \right)}\|}_{2}},` ## Appendix D Baselines We compare our method against audio-conditioned NeRF, 3DGS and mesh based methods. Since mesh-based methods can’t generate photorealistic avatars, we combined audio-to-mesh methods with recent state-of-the-art mesh-to-3D avatar method GaussianAvatars \[[42](https://arxiv.org/html/2411.18675v1#bib.bib42)\]. Current NeRF and 3DGS based methods are designed for monocular videos only, thus in the main paper we train these methods only on the front cameras recording from our dataset. We breifly describe these methods as follows: Faceformer \[[18](https://arxiv.org/html/2411.18675v1#bib.bib18)\]. Faceformer leverages Wav2Vec2.0 to encode audio features, which are then processed by transformer-based autoregressive model via cross-modal multi-head attention to synthesize mesh animations. The method additionally uses biased causal multi-head self attention and periodic positional encoding to improve generalization to longer sequences. The paper proposes a generic sequence model for a fixed set of identities with a style embedding to learn identity-specific speaking style. Codetalker \[[67](https://arxiv.org/html/2411.18675v1#bib.bib67)\]. Given an audio signal, Codetalker formulates speech-driven facial animation as code query task of a learnt codebook. The codebook is learnt by self-reconstruction of the mesh sequences with VQ-VAE. The learnt discrete codebook is then leveraged by code-query based temporal autoregressive model for speech-conditioned facial animation. The discrete motion space of finite cardinality can accurately audio-conditioned mesh animation. Similar to Faceformer, this method also encodes audio with Wav2Vec2.0. Imitator \[[55](https://arxiv.org/html/2411.18675v1#bib.bib55)\]. Similar to ours, Imitator learns a personalized model for speech-conditioned 3D facial animation. The method first pretrains a transformer based sequence model on high-quality VOCA dataset \[[12](https://arxiv.org/html/2411.18675v1#bib.bib12)\] and then finetunes it with short Flame tracked sequences of the personalized avatar. To model lip closures accurately, the paper further proposes a novel lip contact loss based on physiological cues of bilabial consonants ‘m’, ‘b’ ‘p’). RAD-NeRF \[[53](https://arxiv.org/html/2411.18675v1#bib.bib53)\]. The paper proposes audio-conditioned neural radiance fields for real-time rendering. The key contribution of the method is an efficient NeRF architecture by decomposing the video representation into three low-dimensional trainable feature grids. The first two feature grid model the audio and dynamic head motion respectively. The third feature grid models the torso motion. Compared to previous audio-conditioned NeRFs, this runs much faster and enables real-time inference. ER-NeRF \[[32](https://arxiv.org/html/2411.18675v1#bib.bib32)\]. Similar to RAD-NeRF, this paper also focusses on real-time rendering for audio-conditioned neural fields. However in contrast to RAD-NeRF, this method leverages triplane hash representation for learning spatial features. The method further captures the impact of audio features on different facial regions via region-aware attention module. To handle eye blinks, it uses explicit eye blinking control with a scalar. To model the torso, the method transforms a set of trainable 3D keypoints to normalized 2D coordinates and queries 2D neural field to predict the torso image. SyncTalk \[[40](https://arxiv.org/html/2411.18675v1#bib.bib40)\]. Building upon ER-NeRF, this method uses triplane hash representation and further improves the quality of lip synchronization. The method leverages face-synchronization controller that align lip motion with the corresponding audio signal and uses 3D facial blendshapes for capturing facial expressions. To model head pose, it utilizes 3D head tracker and stabilizes the head pose with a pretrained optical flow estimation model. Finally, it uses a portrait-sync generator to restore rest of the details like hair and background. GaussianTalker \[[9](https://arxiv.org/html/2411.18675v1#bib.bib9)\]. The paper proposes audio-conditioned talking head generation framework based on 3D Gaussian Splatting (3DGS). By leveraging the speed and efficiency of 3DGS, the authors construct a canonical 3DGS representation of the head and deform it in synchronization with the audio during audio-driven animation. Specifically, it encodes spatial information (3DGS position) of the head via multiresolution triplane feature grid and uses an MLP for predicting rest of the 3DGS attributes in canonical space. Finally, these Gaussian attributes are merged with audio features via the Spatial-Audio attention that predict per-frame 3DGS deformations, enabling stability and control. TalkingGaussian \[[33](https://arxiv.org/html/2411.18675v1#bib.bib33)\]. It is a deformation-based talking head synthesis framework, also leveraging 3DGS addressing the problem of facial distortion in existing radiance field methods. The authors represent dynamic talking head with deformable 3D Gaussians, and consists of (a) static persistent Gaussians representing persistent head structure and (b) neural grid-based motion field to handle dynamic facial motion. The model is decomposed into two branches to handle face and mouth interior separately with the goal to reconstruct more accurate motion and structure of mouth region. ## Appendix E Dataset Details Our multi-view dataset consists of native speakers in age group 20-50 and includes three male and female participants, see Tab.
